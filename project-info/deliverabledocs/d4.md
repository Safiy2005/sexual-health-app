## Tools

We have set up a GitLab repository to host our code, and made sure everyone can access it. We are using Jira to track our stories and sprints, as well as for its inbuilt burndown chart generation, which we plan to use in our next deliverables.

# Scenarios

## Managing appointments and Medication reminders

**People:** User who has recently been assigned a medical test, **Setting:** Their bedroom at home

**Action:** The user wants to check all their upcoming sexual health appointments and medication reminders in one place and add a new event.

The user is sitting on their bed after school and wants to check what health-related tasks they have coming up. The user opens the app, which initially appears as a **calculator for discretion** (they have chosen this option), and enters their **secret equation** to reveal the full interface. After selecting **health management** on the homepage and logging in , they are taken to their **Even feed** where they scroll through an organised timeline of appointments, tests and medication reminders. They notice they have forgotten to add a follow-up appointment from a clinic visit, so they tap the **"+ Add Test"** button. A form appears, prompting them to enter the appointment, date, time, and optional notes without requiring complex medical wording. After saving, the event immediately appears chronologically in the feed, alongside discreet reminders that appear **only inside the app** rather than as visible push notifications.
Curious about future commitments, the user taps the **calender icon**, opening the monthly view. They select a future date and see the event list instantly updates to show all events beginning from that day. Feeling more organised, the user returns to the event feed and closes the app using the quick **escape mode** to hide the screen.

### Justification

This scenario directly supports the story **As a user who wants to manage their health, I want a feed of all my events so I can stay organised**. It demonstrates the user’s need for a single, unified place to manage appointments and reminders, linking multiple related user needs as discreet notifications, adding tests separately, calender integration, and privacy first design. It reinforces theme of **reducing administrative burden**, and reflects the flowchart structure where the user can move easily from Homepage $`\to`$ Event Feed $`\to`$ Calender allowing test of navigation, event creation, privacy features and timetable consistency,

## Techniques Used

To ensure the quality and feasibility of our backlog, we applied the **INVEST** framework during our requirements engineering phase. By verifying that each user story was Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, and Testable, we ensured that stories could be developed in isolation without blocking dependencies. This technique was useful for breaking down larger features into "Small" units of work that fit within a single sprint, meeting the Agile principle of delivering frequent, working software.

We have also used **Extreme Programming** (XP) practices. To simulate the XP practice of **Pair Programming**, we will use a **Pull Request** workflow within GitLab. By ensuring that no code is merged into the main branch without a comprehensive **peer review**, we guarantee that every piece of logic is examined by at least two developers, fostering collective ownership and knowledge sharing similar to synchronous pairing. Additionally, we have set the ball rolling on implementing **Continuous Integration** (CI) pipelines. This technique automates the build and testing process every time code is committed, providing immediate feedback on integration errors.

In our architectural design, we utilized the **Model-View-Controller** (MVC) pattern as a technique for achieving **Separation of Concerns**. By **decoupling** the internal data representation (Model) from the user interface (View) and input logic (Controller), we reduced the dependency between classes, as detailed in our Class Diagram. This separation improves the modularity of our system, allowing the frontend and backend tasks to be developed in parallel during the sprint without causing conflict.

## Reflection on the Process

### What went well

Our use of consensus-based estimation with Planning Poker was significantly more effective at bringing us all together and agreeing on projection than previous techniques. By debating the complexity of user stories as a group until an agreement was reached, we avoided arbitrary timeframes and ensured that every team member understood the scope of the tasks, eliminating the issue of "unequal presentation" raised in previous feedback.

Our communication integrations proved highly successful - linking our various services to our Discord server created a transparent workflow where commits and backlog updates were instantly visible to the whole team.

We also demonstrated adaptability during our backlog refinement. When we received feedback regarding the inclusion of "Could Have" stories in the first sprint, we refactored our plan to focus strictly on "Must" and "Should" requirements, ensuring a more high-value delivery target.

### What could be improved

We experienced some friction with using Jira at the beginning. A considerable amount of meeting time was expended trying to configure the software to produce a specific format of the Burndown Chart rather than focusing on the planning itself. In the future, we should conduct tool investigations prior to critical planning sessions.

Our velocity was impacted by some external factors, including multiple team members falling ill or sustaining injuries during this deliverable window. While we managed to complete the work, this highlighted a lack of contingency planning - we should include more buffer time in our sprint planning to accommodate reduced team capacity without compromising deadlines.

The creation of our technical artifacts, specifically the UML Class diagrams, took longer than expected due to struggles with the diagramming software, we should standardize our modeling tools earlier to avoid fighting the software during design.

We struggled to track which team member had completed which user story card during the initial drafting phase. It would have been useful to track this to know which stories still needed attention
