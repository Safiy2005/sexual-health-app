# Literature Review

## Introduction

Sexual health is a very complicated topic, and conversation surrounding it is cast in the shadow of shame and embarrassment, making it a uniquely difficult area to design effective, beneficial processes for.
Digital tools offers a private and reachable way to address these challenges, especially for young people who are a lot more comfortable using technology . Nevertheless , a poorly designed system can reduce engagement if they lack trust, transparency or inclusivity. Concerns over privacy and data usage have continually been increasing , especially due to policy changes such as Roe , shows the importance for the need of digital services that are secure and user centred. This review explores how accessibility, trust, engagement and personalisation influence the effectiveness of digital sexual health tools and picks out crucial design opportunities to create a tool that is private, supportive and trustworthy for users.

## Accessibility and Enabling Access For Everyone

Sexual health is important to **everyone**, and thus must be accessible by as many people as possible.

McKellar et al. found that **mobile accessibility** was highly important. All participants in their study preferred access through mobile, and so it can be concluded that a service meant for young people, but without mobile accessibility and a mobile interface would be wholly unsuitable.

They also found that the individuality of the phone was important, as opposed to using a shared device to access the service. The role of privacy will be explored later on in this review.

This study focused specifically on teenagers. A similar study targeted towards older generations would provide very useful insights into designing a service that can be used comfortably by people of all age groups.

It’s also important that the service remains useful even in the case where users may lack an internet connection. In these cases, the app could offer pre-downloaded offline guides and help. Furthermore, it could buffer queries and interactions to be processed once a connection has been re-established .

It’s also important that the main functionality is easy to locate. Navigation should be clear and simple, with obvious buttons and labels. Users having to navigate a maze of different options and being unable to find what they need is a clear design failure .

And of course, obvious accessibility options such as colour-blindness support and support for screen readers are a must .

## Trust and Transparency

Sexual health is a very sensitive topic for many and therefore people take their sexual health privacy very seriously. Thus, it is important that any service that handles such sensitive data is transparent with how user data is collected, stored, and used, so as to reassure users that their data are not being mishandled or pawned off to third parties . Moreover, it was found that people who needed to disclose personal information to obtain help from professionals have greater trust doing so when interacting with a doctor. This is due to concerns that the disclosure of the same information on an online system may lead to potential confidentiality and security breaches .

These findings are further supported by Dewan et al , who found similar behaviours among teenagers. These teenagers avoided services which did not have clear information on how data was stored and used. The driving force for their concerns was social risk in the form of embarrassment and shame. By being transparent with our data practices we can reassure our users that only those who they want to have access to it are able to view their sensitive data.

Being aware of the societal context and narratives surrounding sexual health is essential, to ensure we do not inadvertently push and encourage unhealthy attitudes. This has been a major problem in the past for previous studies, for example Wood’s 2018 paper . They found that the sexual health workers involved with the study approached their work with the idea that young people and are misguided and need to be corrected and controlled. They also held views that digital technology was inherently antithetical to their idea of a "healthy" and "normal" relationship. Allowing these narratives to dominate the service resulted in things such as user feedback being ignored or overridden in favour of what the workers saw best. Whether they were good decisions or not was not the purpose of the paper, but rather to show that the decisions were **informed** by the workers’ societal views instead of rational justification. So, it’s important that any user feedback is considered in an unbiased way, and we maintain self-awareness of what narratives we may be pushing with our design.

## Engagement

Findings show that young people tend to have high expectations of digital tools and are less likely to engage with tools that seem poorly made or slow. Areas that are particularly expected to have a high standard include; Readability of text, the content shown in the tool, the layout of the tool, video resolution, colours used and overall age appropriateness .

Engagement can also come in the form of interactivity with users. McKellar *et al.* showed that *information-only* sites were very unpopular with users. Interaction in the form of videos and quizzes was key for user engagement. However it is important to note that pure interactivity without the information to back it up felt fake and unappealing. Furthermore, it’s important not to lock information behind interactivity as not everyone may wish to engage with it, they should be encouraged but optional.

## Personalization and Comfort

When using a tool relating to sexual health, which can be a sensitive or uncomfortable topic for some people, it is important that when interacting with the tool the user should not be made any more uncomfortable by how they interact with the service. Therefore it may be seen as important that the user should be able to interact with it in the way that makes them the most comfortable. For example if there are consultations in an app, the user should be able to choose between different formats such as; text based chats, video calls or audio calls .

A large amount of research also suggests that young people (from a population of age 13 to 25) are generally very comfortable using technology, therefore the use of digital tools in sexual health education and awareness could remove many of the friction points which arise and it is suggested that young people in general may prefer an online service rather than options such as face to face clinics where they may feel uncomfortable or embarrassed .

Brevity is an important principle, short sentences and bullet points are generally preferred over large blocks of text . This is due to long paragraphs being potentially disengaging and boring for the user .

## Conclusion

The reviewed literature consistently shows that digital sexual health tools can fill important gaps in accessibility and comfort for users, but their success depends on attentive and reflective design. As explored, trust, privacy and reliability are the crucial factors that determine whether users interact with the service. However, many current services overlook the need to be inclusive and transparent, which reduces long-term engagement. Limited information is available on how different identities and age groups experience these tools. Addressing these gaps can open the opportunity to design a tool that does more than just provide information; one that is more interactive, supportive, and helps users build autonomy, confidence and well being as they manage their sexual health.

# Job to be Done

Based on our thematic analysis and literature review, we have found that the most fitting Job to be Done is as follows:

> "Enable users to self-direct their own sexual health routines - including testing, appointments, and medication - through a fundamentally discreet system that removes administrative burden while providing tailored, relevant information that respects their individual contexts and identities."

The focus on self-directed management directly addresses the `Autonomy` theme that emerged strongly from our analysis. Users expressed clear desires to take control of their sexual health, but current systems create a tension where responsibility is given without adequate support. This autonomy must extend across all aspects of care - testing schedules, appointment management, and medication tracking - because separation of these elements was identified as a key pain point in the `Efficiency and Time` theme.

The emphasis on routine management rather than one-time interactions reflects insights from the `Developing Habits` theme. Users struggle not with initial access to services, but with maintaining consistent engagement over time. Life transitions, competing priorities, and the emotional weight of sexual health create disruptions that current systems fail to address. The job recognizes that including sustainable patterns of care requires active system support rather than providing passive information.

Discretion sits at the heart of this job because the `Embarrassment and Stigma` theme dominated our analysis as the primary emotional barrier to accessing sexual health resources. This isn’t just about privacy features but about designing a system where discretion is fundamental. Users need to engage with sexual health services without fear that their actions will expose them to social judgment. The stigma attached to sexual health is so significant that it overrides practical health concerns for many, making discretion non-negotiable rather than a nice-to-have feature.

The `Efficiency and Time` theme revealed frustration with separated services and complex scheduling, while the `Autonomy` theme showed users feeling overwhelmed despite wanting control. Users need systems that handle the logistics of care coordination -remembering appointments, tracking medication schedules, managing test results - without removing their sense of ownership.

The requirement for tailored, relevant information emerges from both the `Level of Detail` and `Inclusivity` themes. Users repeatedly encounter generic information that fails to address their specific situations, particularly those in the LGBTQ+ community or with specific health conditions. The frustration isn’t just with irrelevant content but with systems that assume a default user and fail to recognize diverse needs. Information must be simultaneously detailed enough to be useful and specific enough to be personally relevant.

Finally, respecting individual contexts and identities goes beyond simple personalization. The `Inclusivity` theme revealed how current services often make assumptions that alienate users, while the `Expectations` theme showed gaps between what users assume they are entitled to and what services provide. This job acknowledges that sexual health intersects with personal identity, relationship structures, and life circumstances in complex ways that one-size-fits-all approaches cannot address.

## Report on Specific Tasks that Make Up D1

### Literature Review

The purpose of the literature review was to identify design features that are *required* in a design solution for a sexual health service. It also allows us to identify gaps in current implementations, which allows us to make a start on how we can build a niche product ourselves. It involved distributing our workload, with each of us reading 1-2 papers and writing our notes on them. Once our notes were written, we could then collate all of the information we found into a single document that flowed naturally - as opposed to just writing one paragraph each with jarring differences between such paragraphs.

### Thematic Analysis

The thematic analysis allowed us to run *qualitative analysis* on the problem space. This allows us to have a specific piece of content that will allow us to justify the "why" behind our design decisions via the themes that we made. We also distributed the workload for the thematic analysis, with each of us having an interview or two to run *first-pass coding* on. We then collated all of these codes into one space and clustered them into themes in person, creating relevant themes that we could base the bulk of the analysis on. This whole process let us go beyond the surface level - meaning we now have a much deeper understanding of the problem.

### Job To Be Done

The Job To Be Done gives us a "mantra" of sorts to act as a strong foundation in our next design decisions and meetings. It allows us to be much more precise to what we need to do rather than just "make an interactive tool of some kind". It provides details that are much narrower than the original design challenge - letting us identify a specific problem - but still gives us enough creative space to make decision changes in the future.

### Techniques

Our work in D1 aligns with the first diamond of the *Double Diamond* framework - the "Discover" phase. This phase focuses on exploring the problem space broadly before focussing on specific insights. Our literature review and thematic analysis allowed us to explore the breadth of sexual health challenges, while our Job to be Done represents the cohesion towards a more focussed problem statement.

Our approach towards reviewing the academic literature involved distributing papers among team members to ensure comprehensive coverage. Each member wrote bullet points on their specific papers, and created a specific sentence referencing any *gaps* they found for us to touch on. We then collated these individual bullet points into a cohesive narrative which allowed us to identify patterns across all the studies we had rather than simply summarizing each paper in isolation.

We used *qualitative analysis* to generate our themes, this involved *first-pass coding* where each team member independently coded 1-2 interview transcripts, identifying and labelling meaningful segments of data. We then collaboratively clustered these codes using *affinity mapping* techniques, where we physically grouped related codes to identify any patterns we could find, this was a really good way of solidifying group understanding of the problem space and ensured contribution from all team members. We felt making each member feel included was very important, again to avoid social loafing.

## Reflection on the Process

Overall, the quality of our D1 submission is strong. The literature review successfully links our academic research with user needs, picking out crucial design factors such as accessibility, trust and personalisation. The themes picked from the interview transcripts are clear and well supported by quotes, giving us a strong base for our design.

The literature review identifies clear gaps, especially the problem of inclusivity and engagement, and stays focused on design implications. Putting this together at first proved a challenge, as the most straightforward approach to splitting the work up would have been for everyone to write a paragraph on the paper they themselves reviewed. However this would have created a very disconnected jarring product. Instead, we looked through all of our papers, identified key points and design implications that were touched on by multiple papers, and created a few broad subheadings to write points under. At this stage, the bullet points we each made on our papers came in very useful as it meant everyone could get a quick understanding of the most important conclusions from each paper. After creating the subheadings, we delved deeper into each problem area, showing how each paper supported and pushed the narrative further with their own relative findings, and concluding some design insights that are informed by multiple papers instead of just one.

Our "Job To Be Done" is succinct, but still captures all the important design choices from our literature, and the themes from the interviews. One improvement may be finding language that capture the ideas we’re trying to present more accurately, which would let us express the problem more simply. However, our current statement is more than apt to act as a guide for our future work.

Our process was precise, with a fair division of tasks and good communication. Project management tasks like shared documents and tracking of deadlines helped maintain the structure. However, ensuring individual tasks were completed before meetings would allow more productive discussions and help the process run more smoothly.

What went well was how we organised our work into clear bullet points and structured sections ahead of the deadline. This reduced last minute stress and helped keep the project on track. However, we could have improved by combining and reviewing our individual sections more efficiently to make the final document flow more smoothly.

We learned how design processes transition from understanding user problems to creating precise design goals. Using thematic analysis was important for uncovering emotional user needs, allowing us to understand user experiences and apply them directly to our design.
