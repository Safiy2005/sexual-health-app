# Low-fidelity Prototype

This low fidelity prototype shows how the system we will create will solve our Job To Be Done:

> "Enable users to self-direct their own sexual health routines - including testing, appointments, and medication - through a fundamentally discreet system that removes administrative burden while providing tailored, relevant information that respects their individual contexts and identities."

The prototype provides users with tools to manage their appointments, medication, and tests; this hits our goals of allowing users to "self-direct their own sexual health routines". We also provide an event feed for the user, which provides an all-in-one overview of their upcoming appointments and prescriptions, which should help remove mental overhead and "administrative burden" from managing their sexual health.

We provide features for discreetness. The predominant one is a false front to the app, in the form of a standard and unassuming calculator app. If the user wishes, they can turn this on, and it will change the app to a calculator when it is opened, requiring a secret equation to get into the rest of the app. This means users don’t have to worry about being embarrassed by someone looking through their phone and potentially finding this app there, because it will just appear to be a calculator. This makes the system "fundamentally discreet".

We provide "tailored, relevant information" to our users, in the form of a search that allows them to find specific information articles relevant to their own health, as well as a forum where users can share personal stories and advice with other users. It also provides functionality for moderators to ensure no inappropriate content is posted to the forum, as well as make posts that are official and verified.

More information on each part of the prototype is provided in the sections below. Design style between pages is not consistent, as the prototype exists primarily to demonstrate functionality rather than aesthetics. Not adhering to a specific aesthetic also allows us to experiment and see what we like the most, which will be useful when we come to develop the real app.

# Evaluation Plan

## Evaluation Overview

### Goal

To assess the overall usability and user experience of the high-fidelity prototype. We aim to determine how effectively the system supports users in self-directing their sexual health routines, specifically focusing on the efficiency of administrative workflows, the clarity and accessibility of educational content, and the extent to which the interface fosters a sense of safety through discretion and reduces user anxiety.

### Method

We will use a mixed-methods Usability Testing approach regarding the system’s performance and user experience. The primary qualitative method will be the Think-Aloud Protocol, where participants are asked to verbalize their thought processes, decisions, and frustrations in real-time as they attempt to navigate the interface. This will be supplemented by Direct Observation, allowing us to objectively record quantitative metrics such as task completion times and error rates without relying solely on user memory. Finally, we will use a Survey Method to capture data surrounding user attitudes: a pre-test questionnaire will create a baseline for technical literacy, while a post-test will use the System Usability Scale (SUS) to create a quantified score for perceived usability.

### Setting

We will be conducting the evaluation in a lab setting (a quiet, booked room on the University campus - likely in B60). With the acknowledgment that this environment is rather dissimilar to the environments that the app would actually be used.

## Participants And Recruitment

### Demographic

Participants will be recruited exclusively from **other COMP2300 student groups**. We have a group in mind, but have decided not to lock them in as our group as we don’t know how things may change seeing as D8 is quite far away. This demographic consists of university-aged students with a high baseline of technical literacy. All participants will interact with the app via a fictional persona rather than themselves, we will need to prepare them for this, and ask them to act realistically.

### Sample Size

We will aim to recruit **5 participants** for this evaluation. Research suggests that 5 participants are sufficient to uncover approximately 80% of usability problems in interface testing , making this an appropriate sample size for our evaluation.

### Ethical Considerations

An **informed consent form** will be given to participants, ensuring they are aware of the evaluation they are participating in, and all of the data we are collecting.

Because of how sensitive sexual health data is, no personal medical data will be requested. Instead, Participants will **role-play** a persona to ensure anonymity and prevent sensitive data being collected at any time.

## Data Analysis

### Qualitative Analysis

Think-Aloud Protocol: Audio recordings and observer notes from the Think-Aloud sessions will be transcribed. We will perform a Thematic Analysis on this data to identify recurring patterns of confusion, frustration, or positive outcomes.

Critical Incidents: We will specifically isolate and analyze "critical incidents" - moments where users were completely stuck or expressed significant anxiety - to prioritize high-impact fixes for the next iteration.

### Quantitative Analysis

Efficiency & Effectiveness: We will calculate the mean time on task and error rates for each scenario (for example, unlocking the app, adding reminders) to benchmark efficiency against our success criteria. Any task with a high error rate will be flagged for redesign.

SUS Score: We will process the post-test questionnaire data to derive a single "perceived usability" score for the prototype. Use the standard SUS calculation based on research - ensuring the SUS questionnaire has alternating positive and negative questions.

Linking Tech Literacy to Results: If our data allows, use statistical methods like Pearson correlation to identify if higher tech literacy leads to better efficiency or perceived usability, helping to determine if the app’s design accommodates varying skill levels.

## Limitations

### Ecological Validity and Artificiality

Sexual health apps are typically used in highly private, emotionally charged environments - such as a bedroom or bathroom. The user may also feel a lot more anxious in these home environment than a clinical lab setting. Testing in a quiet university room cannot simulate the genuine adrenaline or social pressure of a "near-miss" intrusion (like a parent walking in). Meaning some features like the "Escape Button" task may be clicked faster or calmer than they would be in a real-world scenario.

### Participant Bias

Recruiting members from another SDDP group means they are much more likely to have higher-than-typical tech literacy due to them being computer science students. They are likely to be able to navigate familiar UIs much more readily than a persona like Mary - an older user with low tech confidence.

### Hawthorne Effect

Participants may change their behavior simply because they know they are being observed. In the context of sexual health, participants might hesitate to click on certain sensitive articles or might act more "sensibly" than they would in private to avoid judgment from the evaluator, despite the use of personas. This could skew the qualitative feedback regarding how "comfortable" the app feels.

### Short-Term Data

We cannot assess the long term health routine management aspect of our Job to be Done. We can only test the setup of a reminder or appointment calendar event, not whether notifications successfully prompts action over time

### Techniques

**More on the 3 Cs Model**

As mentioned, we used the Three C’s model for generating our coarse user stories, this model allows for a large amount of collaboration and iteration in the future. It includes the **card**, which captures a stakeholder’s requirement in a concise statement, so we can see what is needed at a glance.

The **conversation** part of each story was particularly beneficial for us, as it gave us an idea early on potential issues that each may bring in the future. This helped us significantly to understand the potential scope of each story and made prioritisation a lot easier.

The **confirmation** helped solidify the *feasibility* of each card. It ensured that we know exactly when a requirement has been met during the testing phase and solidify benefits each story gives to stakeholders.

**Agile Methodologies**

Using MoSCoW prioritisation allowed us to keep our **scope** variable - and to ensure we know for sure which are the **minimum features** required to make a working product. This helps us to **maximize the work *not done*** - a core Agile principle. Constraining ourselves to a **low-fidelity** prototype, ensuring we do not have inappropriate fidelity, also met this principle and meant we avoided the sunk-cost fallacy, keeping the design flexible and easy to discard if the logic was flawed.

We also ensured we **Welcomed Change** and incorporated **Iterative Delivery** by treating the prototype not as a final artifact, but as a working model for discussion. During one of our meetings, we identified a gap in the absence of a medication timetable. Because we were working in short iterations rather than a rigid "Waterfall" plan, we were able to immediately integrate this requirement without severely disrupting the project timeline.

**DECIDE framework for evaluation planning**

The DECIDE framework acted as a basis for our evaluation plan. It involved us <u>**d**</u>etermining the goals, which allowed us to focus on the valuable parts of the project that are worth testing.

We then <u>**e**</u>xplored specific questions to guide the testing, such as “Can the user trigger the panic mechanism in under 2 seconds?” rather than vague inquiries.

We <u>**c**</u>hose a mixed-methods approach to the evaluation, combining the **Think-Aloud Protocol** with direct **Observation** for qualitative analysis.

We then <u>**i**</u>dentified practical issues, acknowledging that a university lab lacks the ecological validity of a private bedroom, and planned to solve this with persona role-play. This role-play was also the result of us <u>**d**</u>eciding on ethical issues. It ensures that no personal sexual health data is ever requested from participants, solving the privacy constraint.

Finally, we planned how to <u>**e**</u>valuate the data, choosing Thematic Analysis for the verbal feedback to identify critical incidents and **System Usability Scale (SUS)** and other statistical analysis for our various statistical metrics.
