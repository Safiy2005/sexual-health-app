**Bio**

Gertrude is a primary school teacher at a Christian school and is highly involved in the church parish. She believes in bringing up her daughter aligning with her "good and moral" Christian beliefs. She believes like most strict Christians that sex before marriage is a sin and is therefore very judgemental of people who "sleep around", this leads her to believe that sexual health services are made for people who have many sexual partners. She is not very educated on the topic of sexual health and STI’s, largely due to being uncomfortable discussing the topic, so does not know much about the transmission, effects, or treatment of them.

**Attitudes**

- Embarrassment: she would be very embarrassed if anyone found out that her daughter was using sexual health services

- Judgement: she would be very judgemental of her daughter if she had an STI or was accessing a sexual health tool

- Morality: She believes that sexual health information or tools encourage pre-marital sex

**Behaviours**

- Prioritises church teaching and religion over modern values

- Avoids embarrassing conversations such as those about sexual health

**Motivations**

- Try to bring up her daughter with her idea of good and moral Christian values

- Uphold her reputation and family image in her community.

- Maintain a level of control over her daughter to "protect" her from what she views as dangers in her faith.

**Frustrations**

- How discussions about sexual health may encourage people to sleep with others through normalising sex.

- How young people may be exposed to explicit images and content on sexual health services.

**Justification**

**Purpose of Persona**

Gertrude represents the demographic of people who may be highly critical of discussing and using sexual health services, this is an important group of people to consider when designing a service as they may affect the lives of people who do use sexual health services or may need to become more educated about the topic.
She does not directly interact with a theoretical tool directly, but it is important to consider how she may be exposed to or view the tool which is still a key part of our design to discuss and design for.

**Relation to Thematic Analysis**

- **Stigma / Embarrassment:** Gertrude is very conscious of what others in her community might think of her and therefore avoids anything that may be controversial, she also avoids conversations about sex as they make her uncomfortable leading to her being uneducated on the topic.

- **Autonomy** Gertrudes approach to parenting directly opposes the theme of autonomy that we have outlined as she wishes to maintain a level of control over her child, this means that the tool will have to be built with this kind of behaviour in mind.

**Relation to Job-To-Be-Done**
Gertrude mainly links to our Job to be done in the section which discusses the Discretion of the job, the JTBD (Job to be Done) states our findings in our analysis that the `Embarrassment and Stigma ` surrounding sexual health may be the main barrier to access, as she would contribute to this by treating the topic as shameful and trying to control her child’s access to health services. This creates the need to avoid this barrier as much as possible through **"making discretion non-negotiable"** when designing the tool.

**Bio**

Kento is a senior nurse practitioner at an NHS health clinic. With over 10 years of experience in community healthcare, he is enthusiastic about linking medical systems and everyday users, especially under-represented groups and young people.

He is responsible for implementing digital tools that improve patient engagement and help him provide consistent care. Kento has multiple tasks to manage : Patient education, consultations, follow-up scheduling, medication management. As a result, he does not have a lot of time.

Kento is open-minded and good with technology but is annoyed with outdated clinical systems that make daily work inefficient. He values innovation but needs solutions that fit well into existing workflows and would like it to save time rather than create more admin.

He believes sexual health services should be encouraging and stigma- free, but often sees patients avoiding follow-ups because of embarrassment or poor system usability. Kento wants a system that allows him to focus on the care of patients and not paperwork.

**Attitudes**

- Sees technology as crucial for reducing admin load and improving consistent care of patients

- Believes efficient and empathetic service design reduces stigma

- Thinks trust and discretion are the most important when it comes to sexual health care.

- Wants patients to be in control but understands a little follow up goes a long way.

**Behaviours**

- Uses multiple systems daily

- Prioritises the reassurance of patients especially for anxious or first-time users

- Frequently sends follow-up reminders

- Always up to trying new digital tools that work better or save time

**Motivations**

- To improve efficiency, saving time on admin to focus on patient interaction

- To support patients in keeping up testing and medication habits

- To implement a system that promotes reassurance and discretion, inspiring repeated engagement

- To provide accurate and inclusive information to earn patients’ trust.

**Frustrations**

- Existing systems are inefficient

- Patients often miss appointments, making it hard to provide consistent care.

- The interface feels complicated, too medical and can put off users.

- Finds it hard to keep things accurate while also keeping them simple for users

**Justification**

**Purpose of Persona**

Kento represents the implementer stakeholder, a key link between the system and end users. He ensures the tool actually works within real-world care structures. His efficiency challenges directly relates to the efficiency and time theme, while his longing to help users maintain habits aligns with Developing Habits and Autonomy.

Kento plays a key role in the design as if it saves time, keeps things accurate and benefits patients, it will easily be integrated into daily clinic workflows.

**Relation to Thematic Analysis**

- **Efficiency and Time**: Kento’s biggest frustrations are: too much admin and slow workflows which links to this theme

- **Developing Habits**: He sees patients fail to maintain testing routines and wants dynamic digital reminders

- **Reassurance and Comfort**: He wants to reduce patient anxiety with a calm and inclusive interface

- **Autonomy**: He wants patients to take charge of their care while keeping an eye on things professionally

- **Inclusivity**: He recognises how poorly designed systems isolate LGBT+ and minority users and wants this to change

**Relation to Job-To-Be-Done**

Kento expresses the **"removes administrative burden"** part, he needs a system that automates repetitive tasks while supporting users to manage their own routines. His frustrations with the loss of time and the lack of coordination align well with the need for coordinated and forward-looking daily management.

**Bio**

Josephene (Jo) is a teenage girl who has had very little education on sexual health services. She is entering a stage of her life where she is hearing more and more about sexual health from her friends, and wishes to find out more. Jo just got her first smartphone - her father’s old iPhone - so is also starting her online life. Jo likes to use Snapchat to talk to her friends, and TikTok to consume content - there is a lot of content on these services that confuse her, particularly content about sexual health. Jo is not aware of the dangers of misinformation or the potential of harm from these services. Jo appreciates a fun, engaging user interface when she is using her phone, she plays a lot of games that include fun colours and interactive elements. Jo is doing well in school, and has a keen interest to learn new things. Jo is not in a household that has open communication about sexual health - she has never spoken to her parents about the topic - in fact it would make her very uncomfortable to talk about it with her family.

**Attitudes**

- Believes seeking sexual health information is "doing something wrong" and is a source of intense potential embarrassment - particularly from family.

- Curious and keen to learn, but finds the topic of sexual health confusing and uncomfortable to talk about openly.

- Finds clinical or *boring* implementations and designs unappealing and intimidating.

- Expects what she does on her phone to be inherently private, but does not understand that is sometimes not the case.

**Behaviours**

- Passively uses Snapchat and TikTok as her primary sources for discovery of sexual health information - never uses applications solely related to sexual health.

- Actively avoids conversations about sexual health with her parents or any authority figures.

- When confused, she is more likely to ask friends (if at all) or passively consume more social media content rather than actively searching a trusted source for sexual information.

- Plays mobile games and engages with apps that have gamified or highly interactive elements.

- Will abandon a service if it feels medical, boring, or makes her feel judged.

**Motivations**

- To privately understand the confusing topics her friends and social media are talking about.

- To find a non-judgmental space where she can explore her many questions without fear of being shamed, discovered, or feeling like she’s doing something wrong.

- To feel socially confident and ’in-the-know’ with her peer group who is starting to talk about sexual health.

- To feel heard, and remain driven by engagement.

**Frustrations**

- She struggles with feeling intense discomfort at the idea of her family or even a professional knowing she is looking for this information.

- She is confused by conflicting, scary, or misinformed content on TikTok and not knowing what or who to trust.

- The rare times she has looked into more official sources for sexual health information, she feels overwhelmed or stupid as it is really hard for her to understand.

**Justification**

**Purpose of Persona**

Josephene (Jo) represents the most critical and vulnerable user group identified in our D1 research: the young, newly-curious user. She is not yet an active service user (e.g., booking tests), but is in the crucial exploratory phase where she is forming her lifelong attitudes towards sexual health. Her bio and behaviours show she is currently being failed by informal channels (misinformation on TikTok) and is too intimidated to access formal ones. She embodies the user with the highest barriers to entry, making her the most important stakeholder to design for. If we can solve her problem, we can solve it for less-vulnerable users as well.

**Relation to Thematic Analysis**

Jo’s persona is a direct embodiment of key themes identified in our D1 Thematic Analysis:

- **Embarassment and Stigma**: Jo has active avoidance of discussing sexual health matters to her parents - this directly mirrors our findings that fear of social judgment is a primary driver for avoiding care.

- **Level of Detail**: Jo falls perfectly into the conflict of this theme - while being frustrated by boring, clinical designs - she is still overwhelmed by too much content and overly medical terminology. She needs information that is engaging, and simple enough while still being detailed and trustworthy.

- **Comfort & Engagement**: This persona makes us focus seriously on a design that addresses how young people are less likely to engage to content that is unappealing. To retain Jo as a user, we must provide an environment in our service that is as comfortable and interactive as is reasonable.

- **Inclusivity**: To solidify the trait that this persona has very little understanding of sexual health, we have not assigned her a direct sexual orientation - this will hopefully lead us to make decisions that will support questioning users.

**Relation to Job-To-Be-Done**

Jo’s main motivation is to avoid embarrassment from her parents and other adults, this fits with our JBTD as it requires a **"fundamentally discreet system"**. Her frustration with conflicting TikTok content and overwhelming official sources demonstrates the need for **"tailored, relevant information that respects... individual contexts and identities"**. While she is not yet managing testing, appointments, and medication she is at the critical first step: trying to **"self-direct"** her *learning*. We must foster a foundational space of self-directed education before Jo can know how to use the administrative tasks available also.

**Motivations**

**Frustrations**

- Difficult to actually get to a clinic and be screened for STIs, due to her increased frailty and reduced mobility. This leaves her feeling quite isolated from help.

**Justification**

**Purpose of Persona**

Mary represents the older demographic, who obviously need to be included and designed for, but have significantly different needs.

She also poses an interesting design problem with her attitudes surrounding the LGBT community. Her views are antithetical to those held by members of the LGBT community, and designs that serve to make them, like Jamie Kendrick, feel included, will in equal measure put off people like Mary, and vice versa.

Designing around this is very difficult, but there are potential solutions like changing what content we show to users based on information we know about them.

- Mary values **Autonomy** and being able to manage her sexual health on her own, since it is difficult for her to go in person to clinics.

- She doesn’t want to spend lots of time navigating long complex interfaces, linking to our theme of **Efficiency and Time**.

**Relation to Job-To-Be-Done**

Mary needs to be able to **"self-direct"** her sexual health because it is difficult for her to get external help.

She also needs information that is **"tailored \[and\] relevant"** because any distraction or misdirection will be incredibly confusing for her.

**Bio**

They chose nursing because they are driven by a fundamental desire to care for people at their most vulnerable. They have been working as a full-time nurse for almost 8 years since graduating at 22. Jamie is deeply embedded in their local community, volunteering at an LGBTQ youth centre and organizing a monthly queer book club. Jamie treats their sexual health as routine maintenance. Getting STI screenings regularly and stays up to date with the latest sexual health guidelines. They prefer to talk to their doctor in person about anything to do with their sexual health. They struggle with online sexual health services as they usually lack the information they need. Overall they are pragmatic, empathetic, and articulate, but have little patience for bureaucracy or systems that fail to see the individual.

**Attitudes**

- Finds the healthcare system, particularly sexual health service, to be rigidly binary.

- They see STI testing as a basic, non-shameful part of personal health management.

- Believes online sexual health services are lacking in information specific to the type of sex they are having.

**Behaviours**

- Treats STI testing as routine maintenance. They get a full panel screening every 4-6 months, and always between new partners.

- As an RN, Jamie stays informed about the latest sexual guidelines. They have already has a detailed discussion with their doctor about PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis).

- Uses barrier methods consistently is comfortable communicating about the various methods (internal/external condoms, dental dams) with their sexual partners.

- If a provider makes an assumption based on their name or appearance, Jamie will politely but firmly correct them.

**Motivations**

- Feels a responsibility to "practice what they preach." They would feel hypocritical to advocate for the health of their patients while neglecting their own.

- They have seen the outcomes of untreated infections and poor sexual health management in their clinical work.

- Avoidance of gender dysphoria and emotional distress. They are motivated to find healthcare environments that are affirming of their sexual health needs.

**Frustrations**

- They often face intake forms that only offer "Male" or "Female" options, forcing them into an immediate, uncomfortable position of either misgendering themselves or having to "out" themselves to a receptionist.

- They often have to travel further or pay more (when needed) to see a provider or clinic that is known for LGBTQ+ competency, particularly for care that is often highly gendered.

**Justification**

**Purpose of Persona**

Jamie represents the user with the lowest representation: a non-binary person who does not have traditional men/women sex. As someone with experience using managing their own sexual health, they have had real lived experiences where the system fails. A persona like Jamie serves as a critical test of inclusion in our design process.

**Relation to Thematic Analysis**

- **Inclusivity**: As a non-binary person, this persona offers a crucial perspective that otherwise may be overlooked in the design process. Jamie has direct experiences of where the system fails. Pinpointing exact areas of exclusion, like binary forms, that ensures we make informed design decisions.

- **Level of Detail**: This persona embodies the user who needs more specific sexual health information. As a non-binary person, generic online advice based on binary men/women sex is often irrelevant or confusing, forcing them to spend excess time relating available information to themselves or giving up.

- **Embarassment & Stigma**: This persona is an example of how stigma and embarrassment can be created by the system. For Jamie, the stigma isn’t from the general awkwardness that comes from discussing sexual health some may experience. It’s the stigma of being non-binary in a traditionally binary system. They faced embarrassment not just from the topic, but from the tools themselves. Like the intake form that forces them to either misgender themselves or publicly out themselves to the administrative staff.

## Scenarios

### Scenario 1

**People:** Gertrude and her daughter Chloe

**Setting:** Her daughters bedroom

**Action:** Gertrude has seen her daughter open a sexual health website on her computer

Mary, a Christian primary school teacher has come home from work and wants to check on Chloe who she believes is working on an assignment she has been given for her college class. She walks into Chloe’s room without knocking, the desk in the room is across from the door and the monitor is visible from the doorway, Gertrude sees that Chloe is on a website called STI Help, with obvious branding and logo at the top of the page, along with large images that identifiable from where she is standing as rashes and sores.

She immediately jumps to the conclusion that her daughter has been sleeping with people and has caught an STI. She immediately starts shouting at Chloe and makes it clear that she is very upset that she believes her daughter has gone against Christian teaching and that she will be monitoring her more.

The daughter Chloe is also emotionally effected by this interaction as she feels as though she has let her mother down even though she has done nothing wrong and was only learning about sexual health because she had heard about one of her friends at college contracting one. She therefore wishes that there was a way she could have researched this that was less explicit and less obvious to a third party such as her mother.

**Justification**

This scenario links heavily with our theme, `Embarrassment and Stigma` and also demonstrates why there is a large focus on discretion and privacy in our Job to be done. Users need to be able to feel comfortable using the tool to avoid any friction to them getting possible needed help or educating themselves, and this outlines a way in which a barrier to use may occur.

Therefore design choices regarding the negatives of this interaction can be used in our design to avoid this type of scenario from occurring.

### Scenario 2

**People:** Kento Yamazaki

**Setting:** NHS sexual health clinic

**Action:** Kento uses the new digital system to streamline his workflow and help maintain consistent testing habits through discreet and motivated reminders.

It is Monday morning and there is a lot of activity in the NHS sexual health clinic, and Kento is a bit behind schedule. His calendar shows a long list of follow-up appointments and result updates. Normally, this would mean switching between multiple tabs and systems. However, today Kento uses the new digital system that syncs all appointments, results and reminders into one place.

A patient who has previously visited has missed two follow-ups and arrives looking nervous. Through the system’s dashboard, Kento sees the patient’s record, results and reminders in one clear view. The interface is very friendly and simple which avoids heavy medical language that can make the patient more anxious.

Kento notices the patient has not checked the previous test reminder sent last week. Quickly, he sends an in app reassurance message that normalises the experience; "Dont worry, it’s OK to feel nervous, remember this is part of your routine care.". The system sends a kind reminder for the next check up, allowing the patient to take ownership of their own care without feeling judged.

Later, Kento reviews the interactions of his patients. He feels reassured that well designed digital pushes are genuinely helping patients build testing habits. Instead of spending most of his time in paperwork, he’s able to dedicate more time listening and providing emotional support.

**Justification**

This scenario demonstrates how the system directly supports Kento’s needs as an implementer stakeholder by addressing both the **Efficiency and Time** and **Developing Habits** themes. It shows how automating repetitive tasks (reminders) removes extra admin burden, allowing Kento to focus on more important things like patient interaction which fulfils the efficiency aspect of the job to be done.

The ability of the patient to be able to manage reminders discreetly shows how the system supports independent, habitual care, linking to the **Developing Habits** and **autonomy** themes. The calm interface and the reassuring tone address the **Reassurance** and **Comfort** theme, which Kento values to reduce stigma and nervousness.

By adding these features, the scenario confirms a design that is time efficient for professionals and
empowering for users, meeting the goal of helpful,supporting healthcare staff and helping patients regulate their own sexual health through discretion and reinforcement.

### Scenario 3

**People:** Josephene (Jo) Wilkinson, and her best friend

**Setting:** Best Friend’s house, then at home

**Action:** Jo watches a video about an unfamiliar sexual health topic in front of her friend, and wants to discreetly find out more without her friend knowing she didn’t understand.

Josephene (Jo) is at her best friend’s house. They’re scrolling through TikTok when a video pops up, discussing a specific sexual health topic that Jo has heard her friends vaguely mention before, but she’s never understood. The video uses slang and makes some confusing claims, leaving Jo feeling out-of-the-know and anxious. She glances at Maya, who seems to understand the video, and she feels a familiar sense of embarrassment; she doesn’t want to look uncool or uneducated in front of her friend.

Jo wishes to discreetly fill her knowledge gap. She pulls out her old iPhone. Instead of opening TikTok again, she opens a new app she had downloaded because it felt safe and unassuming to install - not at all like a "medical" app.

Upon opening it, the system feels comfortable and engaging, not clinical or boring like a school website. It invites her to ask a question. Jo hesitates, then types in the slang term she overheard. The system immediately provides a concise, simple answer in "normal words," and she feels relief. It’s not scary or overwhelming. The system offers to explain more, and she engages with the topic for a few minutes, feeling like her curiosity is being rewarded, not judged.

Now that her initial anxiety is gone, she explores the app a bit more. She finds a section that explains what to do if someone needs help for this topic. It doesn’t present her with forms or clinic lists, which would scare her off. Instead, it simply walks her through the process in a clear, nonmedical way.

For the first time, Jo understands the steps involved in getting help, including how she could use the same app to know what tests are for or how appointments work - and how she could manage them. She’s not ready for any of that, but just knowing the process makes her feel a new sense of control. She closes the app, not only knowing the slang term but also understanding the entire pathway to care, and how it can be managed in the app, all without talking to anyone.

**Justification**

This scenario for Josephene demonstrates how a hypothetical system fulfils our JTBD by addressing her needs as a vulnerable, newly-curious user. By portraying Jo’s progression from anxiety due to misinformation to a newfound sense of control, the scenario directly validates the need for a fundamentally discreet system that is safe and non-judgmental, as evidenced by her choice of a safe and unassuming app and her private engagement. It showcases the provision of tailored, relevant information that respects her individual contexts and identities by delivering concise, simple answers that match her Level of Detail and Comfort and Engagement needs, thereby countering her frustrations with conflicting, scary TikTok content and overwhelming medical jargon. Furthermore, the scenario illustrates how the system removes administrative burden; not by forcing her into booking, but by demonstrating the pathway to care in a clear, nonmedical way, enabling her to self-direct her learning about services without fear. This scenario provides need for design choices that prioritise discretion, engaging and accessible educational content, and a disclosure of administrative information that empowers the user at their own pace, ensuring our solution genuinely supports users like Jo in taking their first steps towards understanding and managing their sexual health.

### Scenario 4

**People:** Mary Hargate

**Setting:** Inside her care home

**Action:** Mary is suffering from symptoms that she does not recognise. She feels a bit embarrassed to ask the workers at the care home for specific information regarding her symptoms, but it is very difficult for her to physically go to a clinic and get checked out.

She finds a service that helps her do a rough self-diagnosis, so that she can understand what kind of disease she has. She can even order medication through it that arrives at where she lives.

When first opening the service, she is prompted for some details about herself, like her age and sexuality. She is then primarily exposed to images and information that match her own age and predisposition, avoiding triggering her with exposure to things she isn’t comfortable with.

This personal information is also only stored locally on the device, so users do not need to be concerned with how their data is being stored or shared in the cloud, because it never leaves the device.

**Justification**

This scenario explores how our service may be used by an older person, which the other scenarios do not cover.

It explores the specific situation of difficulty physically going to a clinic, due to reduced mobility as people age. So, in lieu of being able to easily go to a clinic in person, it shows how the app still allows people to take control of and self-direct their sexual health, as stated in our Job To Be Done.

It also explores themes of data privacy, by showing what happens to their data after it is collected. By storing this data only on the local device where possible, we ensure our objective of keeping user data private and secure is met.

### Scenario 5

**People:** Jamie

**Setting:** Sexual health clinic reception

**Action:** Jamie is filling out a intake form with their personal details

At their local sexual health clinic for a routine screening, Jamie is given an intake form. They fill it out until they reach the "Gender" section that only has "Male" and "Female" checkboxes. There is no "Other" option, no blank line for a write-in, and no separate field for any other gender identity. Jamie knows that leaving it blank will only result in the receptionist pointing out the "error" publicly. This binary choice immediately forces and difficult dilemma: either misgender themselves for an easier process, or risk confrontation. Not wanting to misgender themselves, Jamie firmly crosses out both options and writes "NON-BINARY" in the margin. They walk back to the desk and hand in the form, whispered discussion follows from the administrative staff. The medical part of their visit hasn’t even begun, and they are already overwhelmed.

**Justification**

This scenario is an example of a lived experience of how the system can fail someone like Jamie, a non-binary person. Jamie is RN and deals with the health care system daily, yet this scenario demonstrates that dealing with something that could be brushed off as a small over site by designers; can put someone, even like Jamie, in a vulnerable position and feeling overwhelmed. This highlights the importance of inclusivity, showing that when it is not considered in the design process it creates stigma and embarrassment for unrepresented users. This scenario ensures we consider all potential users,like Jamie and other members of the LGBTQ community, when implementing design choices to help people manage their sexual health.

## Design Concept 2

**Sketch of Design**

<img src="D2/figures/D2 Kiosk Design.png" style="width:14cm" alt="image" />

**Key Features**

- Two-part solution: includes a Kiosk App, and a Phone App. They both share a back-end server allowing users to sign in with the same account on both, and changes made in one will affect the other.

- Placing the app on a kiosk inside a clinic makes it really convenient for patients to quickly book follow up appointments after they’ve just had a check-up. Otherwise, it can be easy to forget to do this. Then, these appointments can be retrieved by their phone app, and reminders can be set for it, all automatically.

- Easy sign at the kiosk using a code provided by the app. The default sign in method using personal information is also available.

- Merges with current clinic kiosks, retaining their behaviour, allowing users to sign in and see information.

- Also allows users to book their next appointment, which they might want to do on their way out of the clinic. Pulls in all available dates and times so patients only see available times and the process of booking is smoother.

- The red circle on the book appointments button indicates that they have been requested to make a follow up appointment. This is a subtle way of nudging and reminding the user to set up an appointment instead of putting it off and forgetting about it.

- Allows for medication management, so patients can check where to pick up their prescriptions from, and how long until they can be collected, as well as information such as dosages and common side effects.

- The kiosk screens could have privacy protectors on them, making it harder for strangers to shoulder-surf patients while they use the kiosk.

- The phone app will also allow you to do everything you can at the kiosk. The kiosk is there primarily for convenience and to act as a physical reminder to users to not put off booking more appointments.

**Justification**

Placing the app on a kiosk helps with the problem we identified of there not being enough reminders to book appointments, by creating a tangible physical place to do that. It also integrates with a process that is already fairly standard in clinics, which is having check in kiosks.

Using a two-part solution allows us to also take advantage of the convenience of mobile phones, enabling users to access their appointments and prescriptions from anywhere. Syncing between the kiosk app and mobile app also makes it very easy and straightforward for users, instead of having a bunch of different apps and services that they have to use to manage their sexual health.

**Drawbacks**

Not every clinic will have kiosks, and it costs money to buy them, money which those clinics may not have. In those cases however, the phone app will still work for booking appointments and managing prescriptions.

Merging extra functionality into the check-in system at clinics may cause queues and waiting if there are not enough kiosks. While one person is booking their appointment, someone else may come in and need to check-in, but they can’t because the previous person is using it. This can already happen of course, but the problem is exacerbated by giving users more reasons to spend time using the kiosk.

## Design Concept 3

A touch screen tablet that comes preloaded with a digital instant sexual health information system (DISHIS). Its use is aimed at remote and lower income communities where access to modern smart phones or personal computers is a luxury. The tablet itself is power efficient, resulting in a long battery life, and is able to be charged from alternate energy sources like solar. As the tablet comes preloaded with DISHIS, all information can be accessed without an internet connection. The system allows for multiple users, each with their own log in. This enables each user to have recommended personalised information to them and restrictions for users, where appropriate.

**Key Features**

- **Access to instant sexual health information digitally** is a luxury not everyone is able to take advantage of. This design concept focusses on rectifying this, enabling remote and lower income communities access instant sexual health information digitally. Tablets that are hard coded with DISHIS can be sent to these communities, allowing them to educate themselves about their own sexual health.

- The tablets are designed around the assumption that **access to electricity and there internet connection is limited or sometimes unavailable**. Tablets are designed to be as energy efficient as possible, ensuring that all unnecessary bloatware is removed, maximising battery life. All sexual health information is stored locally on the tablet, allowing instant access without an internet connection.

- The DISHIS aims to **limit the use of heavy medical language**, ensuring that users can actually understand and act on the information they read. When users turn to the DISHIS, they may already have an issue causing a state of anxiety, vulnerability, or confusion. Complex medical jargon would act as a further barrier, and most likely amplify these feelings.

- The tablet and DISHIS can be accessed without an **account**, enabling complete anonymity to users. Without an account, users can search for information, view community posts, and change settings. However, users with an account will be able to use the system to its fullest:

  - All information articles can be **"bookmarked"** for later use. The user’s most recent saved articles are shown on the homepage. The full list of saved articles can be found in the bookmarks section of the sidebar.

  - The DISHIS has **integrated targeted information and imagery**. User’s are recommended and shown appropriate articles and images based off personal information related to their age, location, gender, sexual orientation, and previously read articles.

  - Within the targeted information and imagery system there are **Content filters**. Content filters allow users to personalise further manually what articles are recommended and can be viewed.

  - Users with access to an internet connection will be able to take advantage of the **communities tab**. This tab is a forum where users can ask questions and share advice with others who need it. There are also polls that users can answer about how they are finding using the DISHIS and if they have learnt something new.

- The **settings** can be found under a tab in the sidebar. They can be accessed by any user, with or without an account.

  - The DISHIS has a wide range of **languages** available by default and can be changed instantly. In addition, the languages spoken in and around the area to which a tablet is going to be sent are checked to be available on the DISHIS before departure.

  - There are a wide range of **accessibility** settings available on the DISHIS. These include, but are not limited to: Screen reader compatibility, high contrast mode, scalable text & layout.

**Justification**  
This design concept is a direct solution to our design challenge, and takes into account potential users like Jo (Scenario 3) and Mary (Scenario 4), who face psychological and physical barriers when managing their sexual health. The DISHIS design is "fundamentally discreet". This directly impacts users like Jo’s needs: she is anxious and fears judgement. The system’s use of simple, non medical language and default anonymity (no account needed to access information) defuses here anxiety, allowing her to explore sensitive topics without fear.

Simultaneously, the design also delivers "tailored, relevant information that respects their individual contexts" to users like Mary. In her care home, she is embarrassed and physically limited. The optional accounts allow the DISHIS to filter content based on her age, avoiding confusing her with irrelevant information. Crucially, accessibility features such as scalable text make the tool truly usable to her.

Finally, the concept "removes administrative burden" by tackling the root issues of access. By providing energy efficient, local storage tablets aimed at remote and low income communities, we can bypass the barriers of device cost, internet connection, and even electricity. This physical medium is what will truly enable users to "self-direct their own sexual health routines."

This approach allowed us to efficiently complete all the tasks while ensuring we stayed aligned with the objectives.

**Simplicity**

Simplicity is *maximizing the amount of work **not** done* instead of over-engineering or adding complex features that aren’t immediately required. The way we incorporated simplicity for this deliverable is by making the conscious decision to **not** use complicated systems such as Figma for our design concepts. Figma is more appropriate for producing a prototype, and right now we only need to focus on consolidating our ideation instead of making something completely polished. This ensures we can make good progress without focussing on the wrong thing.

**Self-Organisation**

The best designs and plans emerge from teams that manage themselves. We did this by ensuring there was no **leader** of the group, we acted as collectively as was reasonable - without one person orchestrating how everything is going to go. This was done by bringing forward ideas with the expectation of them being challenged, and not taking our own opinions as the correct opinion without discussion.
