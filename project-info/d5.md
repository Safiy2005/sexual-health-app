# Updated Sketches this Sprint

In response to the feedback received in Deliverable 3 regarding overly broad scope and homepage design, er refined both the feature set and navigation structure for this increment. We deliberately reduced scope by removing the forum subsystem, Login subsystem and other secondary features in order to prioritise the implementation of core functionality, specifically article search and content accessibility which is in line with the Agile principles of delivering a small but high quality increment. Additionally, we redesigned the application entry point. Instead of opening on a homepage consisting of isolated buttons, users are now taken directly to the article search screen, reflecting common phone design conventions where applications open on a primary use case. To further improve the usability and also reduce cognitive load, we introduced a bottom navigation bar to support **intuitive movement** between screens. These design updates demonstrate our response to feedback and our focus on usability and incremental delivery. This refinement also aligns with the principle of **Minimum Viable Product (MVP)** ensuring that development effort is concentrated on delivering the highest user value within the sprint constraints.

# Updated Scenarios

## Managing Appointments and medication reminders

**People:** Jamie Kendrick (29) - non-binary, queer/pansexual, nurse. **Setting:** Jamie’s bedroom at home, Sunday Evening. **Action (goal):** Jamie wants to discreetly review and organise upcoming sexual health events, including PrEP reminders and a follow-up test.

Jamie is preparing for a busy work week and wants to ensure their sexual health related information are organised in one place. As discretion is important, Jamie opens the app which initially appears as a **calculator**. They enter their **secret equation**, and the app transitions to the **Main Page**, which functions as an article search screen.

Using the **bottom navigation bar**, Jamie taps the **calendar icon** to access the **Event Feed and Calendar**. The event feed displays a chronological list of upcoming appointments, medication reminders (including PrEP) and tests. Jamie realises a follow-up clinic test was never added.

From the event feed, Jamie navigates to the **Add test** screen and enters the test name, due date, and a short optional note in non-medical language. After saving, the new test appears immediately in the event feed in the correct chronological position.

Jamie switches to the **monthly calendar view** then selects a future date, and confirms the feed updates accordingly. When finished, Jamie uses the app’s **escape feature** to instantly return to the calculator interface, ensuring privacy.

### Justification

This scenario supports the following user stories:

- **SDDP30-37** (event feed for appointments, medication, PrEP)

- **SDDP30-22** (adding tests with dates and notes)

- **SSDP30-40** (linking medication reminders to the main event feed)

- **SDDP30-19** and **SDDP30-20** (calculator disguise and escape feature)

- **SDDP30-42** and **SDDP30-11** (discreet reminders)

Demonstrates complete organisation while reinforcing privacy.

## Medication reminders and accurate reminders

**People:** Mary Hargate (68) - retired, little confidence with technology **Setting:** Mary’s room in her care home, morning. **Action (goal):** Mary wants to add medication with the correct dosage and reminder times without confusion.

Mary has been prescribed a new medication and wants to help remembering when to take it. She opens the app, but forgets the secret equation she previously set. Mary enters the predefined recovery equation (990/0), which opens the **configuration screen**, allowing her to safely reset her equation before continuing to the main page. From the **bottom navigation bar**, she selects the **calendar icon** to open the **Event Feed and Calendar**

Mary navigates to the **Medication Reminders** section and taps **Add Medication**. On the **Add medication** screen, she enters the medication name and dosage. She selects two daily reminder times and enables **Link to timetable** so the medication appears in her calendar.

After saving, the medication appears in the reminders list and is visible in the **calendar view** in the correct chronological order. Mary feels happy and reassured everything is clearly organised and exists the app.

### Justification

This scenario supports the following user stories:

- **SDDP30-39** (accurate medication reminders)

- **SDDP30-43** (appointment/reminder notifications)

- **SDDP30-40** (linking reminders to event feed)

It shows Mary’s need for simplicity and minimal cognitive load while ensuring calendar consistency and reminder creation.

# Updated Stories + Project Scope Change

Below is the revised list of user stories. The background colour coding scheme highlights the MoSCoW priorities as follows: <span style="background-color: green!8">Green</span>: Must Have, <span style="background-color: blue!5">Blue</span>: Should Have and, <span style="background-color: yellow!15">Yellow</span>: Could Have. Additionally, the story points and ID number listed with each story serves as a cross-reference to the specific ticket on our Jira board.

The major specific change from the previous iteration of our stories is the removal of those related to our forum epic.

In direct response to our feedback from D3 (which we could not act on before D4 as we received the feedback late), which said that our project scope was too large, we decided to remove all functionality related to forums. It created the most complications and had the most unknowns with regards to the approach to its implementation, on top of being the least relevant of our epics to our Job To Be Done. Finally, it was difficult to justify the value it would provide to our client, as it would just be a forum with placeholder data and no actual people using it for discussion. All this combined meant it was an obvious choice for removal.

This decision was further affirmed after one of our group members left the group, meaning we have less man-hours available to complete the project.

After the removal of our forum stories, this gave us room to split up our largest remaining story into smaller ones that are individually actionable (staying within the rough 25 story limit), allowing us to assign different parts to different people and work on it concurrently. We split our large 40 point story (ID 37) into 4 smaller stories (IDs 47-50).

# D5 Tests

To verify the correctness of our application, we have adopted a systematic testing approach using **JUnit 5** as our test framework.

Our test suite currently comprises **260 tests** across **13 test classes**, organised to mirror the production source structure. Tests target both the **model** and **service** layers of each feature module, verifying behaviour at the unit level (e.g. individual calculator operations, article parsing, section extraction) as well as at the integration level (e.g. end-to-end article search across the full 334-article dataset).

Where possible, our tests are grounded in the design artefacts presented in earlier deliverables: the acceptance criteria from our user stories inform what each test verifies, and the class diagram above defines the interfaces and relationships under test.

For example, the `SecretAuthService` tests exercise the full authentication flow described in the calculator disguise user story (SDDP30-19), while the `ArticleSearchIntegrationTest` validates the search behaviour expected by user story SDDP30-23.

## Summary of Work Completed

In D5, the project transitioned fully into the **Deliver phase of the Double Diamond Model**, moving from abstract design and planning into the implementation of working software. Building on the groundwork created in D4, this increment focused on delivering a functional, testable piece of the application that provides value to the user.

Based on the feedback received in D4, we refined our scenarios to be written from the perspective of a persona instead of a generic ’user’ which insured our decisions were driven by real user needs. As part of this refinement, we made a significant design decision to remove the **forum and login subsystems** from the initial scope. We concluded that these features added unnecessary complexity without providing sufficient value.

Instead, we refocused the application around **content accessibility and discreet navigation**, adding a **bottom navigation bar** to allow switching between core screens. This design change simplified user interaction, reduced cognitive load and better aligned with our personas.

Technically, we implemented a functioning JavaFX application that compiles and runs without errors. The increment includes a structured backend architecture using the **Model-View-Controller (MVC)** pattern, unit tests and a foundation for future development.

## Report on Specific Tasks that Make Up D5

### Design and Implementation

In this increment, we focused on implementing a version of the article navigation and search subsystem alongside the calculator based authentication mechanism. These features were made from the user stories planned for Sprint 1 and shows the transition from design to a working, testable system.

The application compiles and runs without errors and all implemented features meet their acceptance criteria. The system follows the Model-View-Controller (MVC) pattern ensuring separation between data models, logic, and controllers.

Core domain logic is contained within the **model** and **service** packages, while controllers act as intermediaries between the user interface and application logic. This separation improves maintainability, testability and scalability.

Throughout implementation, design decisions were influenced by principles discussed in lectures, including modularity, low coupling,, high cohesion and incremental development. Feedback from previous deliverables was incorporated to improve clarity, reduce unnecessary complexity, and align the system better with user needs.

### Testing

Testing was a core component of this deliverable and was conducted using a combination of **unit testing and integration testing**. Automated tests were implemented using JUnit to verify the correctness, robustness, and reliability of the system.

Unit tests were used to a large degree to validate individual components in isolation. These tests covered critical functionality such as markdown article parsing, search result scoring ,calculator operations, authentication logic and text normalisation. Boundary case, invalid input and error conditions were explicitly tested to ensure predictable system behaviour.

Integration tests were used where component interaction was important, especially within the article search subsystem. These tests verified that the system correctly loads real article data, applies keyword based and semantic search techniques and returns results sorted by relevance. Hybrid search functionality was tested using realistic user queries to validate the system’s ability to support casual users.

Tests were also designed to reflect design artefacts made earlier in the project making sure there was traceability between requirements, design decisions and implementation. The tests suite provides confidence that the increment meets its acceptance criteria which will help us in future sprints.

## Tools and Techniques used

**Agile and Scrum techniques** were used to structure development. Jira provided a shared artefact for sprint planning, backlog refinement, progress tracking and retrospective analysis.

The application was structured using the **Model-View-Controller (MVC)** architectural pattern. MVC supports separation of concerns by isolating logic (model), user interface (View), and interaction logic (Controller). This reduces coupling, improves maintainability and enables independent testing of system components.

Automated tests were written using Junit and organised according to testing theory, including:

- **Unit tests** to verify individual classes and methods in isolation

- **Integration tests** to validate interactions between components such as article loading, search services, and scoring mechanisms.

- **Boundary and edge-case testing** to ensure robust handling of invalid or unexpected input.

Tests were designed to reflect design artefacts and acceptance criteria, making sure there was traceability between requirements, implementation and verification. This aligns with the testing pyramid model, prioritising fast, reliable unit tests while using them with integration tests.

For information retrieval we established **information retrieval and search theory**. Keyword based search uses TF-IDF weighting to measure term importance across the article, while semantic search uses vector embeddings to identify contextual similarity between queries and documents. A hybrid scoring approach combines these techniques, which reflects modern search system design and improves robustness for non expert queries.

Version control was managed using **Git** with a feature branch workflow. This technique supports parallel development and safe integration. Merge requests were used for peer reviews which reduced the risk of integration defects.

## Reflection on the Process

### What Went Well

Our **Communication and preparation between sprint sessions** was the most successful aspect of this increment. We completed allocated tasks prior to the meetings, which meant sprint meetings were used effectively for:

- Reviewing completed work

- Demonstrating implemented features

- Planning next tasks

- Discussing issues

This shows effective application of **Scrum Principles**, especially the emphasis on transparency and inspection. By ensuring that work was completed before meetings, we were able to treat discussions like a sprint review and retrospective.

Our workflow also improved compared to previous deliverables as we:

- Used clearly defined Jira tasks

- Assigned ownership to each story

- Broke large stories into smaller subtasks

- Tracked progress via the burndown chart

This aligns with the Agile principle of delivering **working software frequently** and maintaining a sustainable pace. The incremental development of the calculator disguise and article system demonstrated our sprint plan was realistic and achievable.

We also stuck to the **MVC pattern** which separated concerns and improved maintainability and reduced integration risk.

### What Could Be Improved

During this increment, we lost a team member. This significantly affected our planned Scrum workflow and team velocity. Scrum assumes stable team capacity within a sprint so having lost a member required us to:

- Reallocate story ownership

- Rebalance workload

- Adjust expectations regarding velocity

We adapted effectively but this highlighted a weakness in our **capacity planning and risk management strategy**. In future sprints, we should:

- Include greater buffer capacity

- Avoid assigning many critical tasks to a single person

- Identify backup owners for high priority stories

Another area for improvement is our task estimation accuracy. Some tasks required more effort than anticipated. In future sprints we could allocate story points for integration and testing.

Also our burndown chart reflects steady progress but a lot of the work was completed closer to the deadline. While this is realistic, we should aim for a more even burn rate to reduce pressure at the end of the sprint.
