# Oral Sex: Staying Safe

Oral sex is any type of sexual activity involving the stimulation of genitals with someone’s mouth. This includes using your mouth to stimulate someone’s penis, vulva, or anus, or having your genitals stimulated by someone using their mouth.

For people with penises, oral sex usually involves stimulating the head (the ‘glans’) and the shaft of the penis, and sometimes the testicles. It is sometimes called ‘a blowjob’, ‘giving head’, or ‘going down’.

For people with vulvas, oral sex usually involves stimulating the clitoris. Oral sex on a vulva is sometimes called ‘giving head’ and ‘going down’.

Oral sex on the anus is sometimes known as ‘rimming’ or ‘analingus’.

## Your Choice and Consent

Not everyone has, or wants to have, oral sex. Some people find it enjoyable, others may have tried it and found it doesn’t turn them on, and some may simply not like the idea of giving or receiving it. If you don’t want to give or receive oral sex, it is important to communicate this clearly with your partner.

It's important that you and your partner feel comfortable and are not pressured into doing anything you don’t want to do. If you feel uncomfortable at any point, either before or during sex, you can change your mind and stop.

## Risks and Sexual Health

If you do want to give or receive oral sex, it is important to understand the risks to your sexual health and how to have it safely.

### Oral Sex and STIs

You can get or pass on STIs through oral sex. The risk is present if you are exposed to genital fluids or blood (for example, if your partner is on their period or if you have bleeding gums). If genital fluids come into contact with sores, cuts, or inflamed areas on the lips, mouth, genitals, or anus, this can allow infections to enter your bloodstream.

STIs that can be transmitted through oral sex include:

* Chlamydia
* Gonorrhoea
* HIV
* Syphilis

Pubic lice can also be passed between pubic hair or facial hair (such as beards) during oral sex.

The herpes simplex virus, which causes genital herpes, also causes cold sores. This means the virus can be passed on through oral sex and can result in cold sores on the mouth or blisters on the genitals.

When using the mouth on the anus, there is an additional risk of digestive bacteria and parasites that pass through the anus, such as E. coli and Salmonella. These can cause illness if ingested.

### Oral Sex and HPV

There are over 100 types of Human Papillomavirus (HPV), and a number of these can be passed on during sexual contact, including oral sex. Some types of HPV are known to cause cervical cancer, and HPV can also increase the risk of some types of head, neck, and throat cancer.

## Keeping Yourself Safe

To stay safe when having oral sex, ensure you:

* **Never engage in oral sex if there are open sores or lesions** on the mouth, anus, or genitals.
* **Communicate honestly and openly** with your partner(s) about the potential risks of STIs and other infections.
* **Get tested for STIs regularly** and each time you have sex with a new partner.
* **Maintain good personal hygiene** to minimize the risk of transferring bacteria. This is particularly important for analingus.
* **Use barrier methods**, like condoms (or internal condoms) and dental dams.

Barrier methods may not protect against all STIs, such as pubic lice, or bacteria. If you or your partner have any sores around the mouth, anus, or genital area, avoid oral sex even with protection.

* **Condoms** provide a barrier between the genitals and mouth, helping to prevent the exchange of fluids.
* **Dental dams** are squares of latex or polyurethane that you can use to cover the anus or female genitals during oral sex. While dams may be useful in preventing STIs, there is currently no research into their specific effectiveness.

If you are using sex toys, avoid sharing them. If you do share them, clean them thoroughly between uses by different people.

Avoid brushing your teeth soon after or before giving oral sex, as there is some evidence this can increase your risk of STIs due to tiny cuts that can allow infections into the bloodstream.

## Pleasure and Communication

As with any type of sex, to enjoy oral sex, both partners need to be turned on. As you become more aroused, your body will relax and you may feel more sensitive to touch. Everyone enjoys different things and has different ‘turn-ons’. Understanding what you enjoy during sex and communicating this to your partner will help you have enjoyable and pleasurable sex for both of you.

Make sure to check in with your partner, ask them what feels good and what they like, and tell them what you like. You should never feel like you have to have oral sex, or any other type of sex, if you don’t want to. You also should not force or try to persuade anyone else into doing something they don’t want to do. You might enjoy giving one type of oral sex but not another that your partner suggests, and that’s okay. You don’t have to do anything you don’t want to do; it doesn’t make you selfish. Talk it through with them and let them know how you’re feeling.
