# Internal (or female) condoms

Internal (or female) condoms are barrier contraceptives that fit inside the vagina instead of covering the penis. They are typically made of polyurethane and line the vagina to prevent pregnancy and protect against sexually transmitted infections (STIs).

## At a glance: Key facts

* **Effectiveness:** More than 95% effective if used without mistakes.
* **STI Protection:** Yes, protects against STIs.
* **Hormones:** None.
* **Mood/Emotions:** No effect.
* **Periods:** No change to your period.
* **Acne/Skin:** No change.
* **Visibility:** Only visible to those involved in the sexual activity.
* **Usage:** Single use only; use a new one each time you have sex.
* **Accessibility:** Available for free from many sexual health services and GPs, or available for purchase at pharmacies and online.

## How internal condoms work

Internal condoms line the vagina and protect against pregnancy by stopping sperm from coming into contact with the vagina. They also act as a physical barrier that prevents sexual fluids from being transferred between partners, providing protection against STIs. 

Because they are made of polyurethane, they are a suitable alternative for people with latex allergies.

## Pros and cons

### Pros
* Protects against both pregnancy and STIs.
* Can be inserted up to eight hours before having sex.
* Only needs to be used during sexual activity.
* No hormonal side effects.
* Can be used alongside other methods (like the pill or implant) for extra protection.
* Latex-free (made of polyurethane).
* Still effective when using genital medications like creams or pessaries.
* Can be used with any type of lubrication, including oil-based products like body oils or petroleum jelly.

### Cons
* Can slip or get pushed up into the vagina if not used properly.
* Less widely available and can be more expensive to buy than external (male) condoms.
* Must be replaced after every use.

## Accessing internal condoms

You can get free condoms from Brook services, young people’s services, contraception clinics, GUM clinics, and some GP surgeries. 

* **C-Card Scheme:** You may be able to access free condoms via a local C-Card provider at pharmacies, youth services, and shops.
* **Buying Online/In-store:** They are available to buy at pharmacies, supermarkets, vending machines, and online.
* **Under 16s:** There is no age restriction on buying or accessing free condoms.

If you feel embarrassed, remember that sexual health staff are professionals. You can bring a friend or partner to the clinic for support.

## How to use an internal condom

1. **Check the package:** Feel for the condom and push it to the side before tearing the packet open to avoid damaging it.
2. **Inspect:** Take the condom out and check for any visible rips.
3. **Positioning:** Lie down, squat, or put one leg up on a chair.
4. **Insertion:** Separate the labia with one hand. Squeeze the flexible inner ring at the closed end of the condom and gently insert it into the vagina as far as it will go.
5. **Adjust:** Use your middle finger to push the inner ring up so it rests above the pubic bone.
6. **Outer ring:** The large ring at the open end should remain outside the body, covering the area around the vaginal opening.
7. **Guidance:** Ensure the penis enters the condom and not the space between the condom and the vaginal wall.
8. **Removal:** After sex, twist the outer ring to keep semen inside and gently pull the condom out.
9. **Disposal:** Wrap it in tissue and throw it in the bin. Do not flush it down the toilet.

## Dos and Don'ts

### Do
* Check the expiry date before use.
* Check for the European CE mark or the UKCA mark on the packaging.
* Use a brand new condom every time.
* Take care with sharp nails or jewellery that could rip the material.
* Use lube if needed.

### Don't
* Don't use an internal condom if the pack is damaged.
* Don't use two condoms together (e.g., an internal and an external condom), as friction makes them more likely to tear.
* Don't reuse a condom.
* Don't let the penis touch the vaginal area before the condom is inserted, as "pre-come" can contain sperm.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is it difficult to insert?
It may take a few tries to get used to the process, especially if you aren't used to using tampons or menstrual cups. Using extra lubricant can make insertion easier.

### Can they be used for anal sex?
Yes. Internal condoms can be used during anal sex to protect against STIs.

### What if an accident happens?
If the condom breaks, slips, or is used incorrectly, do not panic. Visit a sexual health service as soon as possible for STI testing and emergency contraception if there is a risk of pregnancy.
