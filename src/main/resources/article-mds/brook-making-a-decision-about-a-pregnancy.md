# Making a decision about a pregnancy

If you have done a pregnancy test and found out you are pregnant, you have three basic choices. You need to decide what you want to do next.

* **Choice A**: Go through with the pregnancy and become a parent.
* **Choice B**: Go through with the pregnancy and place the baby for adoption.
* **Choice C**: End the pregnancy by having an abortion.

## How do you feel about being pregnant?

In order to decide what you want to do, it is important to think about how you feel about being pregnant. Perhaps you planned to get pregnant and having a baby is what you want most at this time. If so, you will likely choose to continue the pregnancy and keep the baby.

If that is no longer what you want, or if you didn't intend to get pregnant, look closely at your feelings. An unintended pregnancy can bring many different or conflicting feelings. You might feel:

* Worried about being able to look after a baby.
* Afraid you’ll have to give up other things that are important to you.
* Concerned about how other people may react.
* Happy to learn that you can get pregnant.
* Pleased to have the opportunity to have a baby.
* Excited by a new and unique event in your life.

It may help to list the different feelings you have right now about being pregnant.

## Your hopes and plans for the future

It is also important to think about what you want from your future. Ask yourself these questions about your life right now:

* What are two or three things that matter most to me in my life right now?
* What are two or three things that I hope to have or achieve in the next five to ten years?
* In order to achieve those things, how would having a baby, adoption, or abortion help?
* What would I lose or give up right now if I have a baby, place the baby for adoption, or have an abortion?

## Thinking about your beliefs

Consider your thoughts, values, and beliefs about your situation and the different choices. Below are some statements people often make regarding their options:

### Choice A: Having a baby and keeping it
* I feel ready to take on the tasks of being a parent.
* Some people have said they will help me.
* I want a child more than I want anything else.
* My partner and I both want to have a baby.
* I think I am too young (or too old) to have a baby.
* I don’t believe I can manage to raise a child properly.
* Having a child now would stop me from having the life I want for myself.
* I don’t feel ready to take on the tasks of being a parent.

### Choice B: Having a baby and placing it for adoption
* I could continue the pregnancy and give birth without having to raise the child.
* I could help the child have parents who want it and can care for it.
* I could postpone being a parent myself until later in my life when I feel ready.
* I like the idea of giving someone else the baby they can’t create themselves.
* I don’t think I could give up the baby after nine months of pregnancy and delivery.
* I would not like living with the idea that someone else has my baby.
* I would worry about whether the baby was being well treated.
* My family would rather have the baby stay in the family than go to strangers.

### Choice C: Having an abortion
* I would like to postpone being a parent until my situation is better (older, finished school, more financially secure, in a stable relationship).
* I don’t want to be a single parent.
* My partner doesn’t want a baby, and I want to consider their feelings.
* An abortion is a safe and sensible way to take care of an unwanted pregnancy.
* My religious beliefs are against abortion.
* I’m afraid I might not be able to get pregnant again.
* My family (or someone else who is important to me) opposes abortion.

## Understanding abortion

If you don’t want to have a baby or go through with an adoption, you can choose to have an abortion. People choose to end a pregnancy for many reasons, including financial stability, personal goals, or medical safety.

It is your choice and your right to have an abortion. The only person who can decide whether an abortion is right for you is you. 

### Common myths about abortion
Some people who oppose abortion claim it has long-term negative health effects. However, medical studies have shown:
* Abortion does **not** cause long-term mental health problems.
* Abortion does **not** make it harder or impossible to get pregnant in the future.
* There is **no link** between abortion and breast cancer.

## Talking it over

Whether the decision is simple or hard for you, you can talk it over with someone you trust. 

* **Privacy:** It is fine to keep your decision private. You can have an abortion without your parents’ or carers’ permission, even if you are under 16 years of age.
* **Partners:** If you are in a relationship, you might want to talk to your partner, but they cannot tell you what decision to make. It is your body and your choice.
* **Counselling:** There are specially trained pro-choice counsellors who can help you come to a decision without judgment. Your GP can refer you, or you can contact organisations like BPAS or MSI Reproductive Choices UK.

## Crisis pregnancy centres

Be careful when looking for support. Some places, known as "crisis pregnancy centres," pretend to offer impartial advice but actually use counselling to persuade people not to have abortions. They may provide false medical information regarding mental health or breast cancer. Brook encourages seeking help from accredited, pro-choice medical services.
