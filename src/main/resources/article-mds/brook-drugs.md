# Drugs

Drugs can change the way your body functions, how you feel, and how you act. A drug is a chemical substance that, when taken, alters physical or mental processes. While there are many different types of drugs, they all have the potential to be harmful if used incorrectly.

## Categories of Drugs

Drugs are generally divided into three main categories:

* **Prescription or ‘over-the-counter’ drugs:** Medications used to manage or treat health issues. These are bought in pharmacies or shops, or prescribed by healthcare professionals.
* **Legal recreational drugs:** Drugs taken to alter mood or behavior that are legal to buy and possess for those over 18 (e.g., alcohol and nicotine).
* **Illegal recreational drugs:** Drugs taken to alter mood or behavior that are illegal to sell, buy, or possess.

### Prescription and Over-the-Counter Drugs
Prescription drugs must be obtained from a pharmacist with a prescription from a GP. It is vital to follow medical instructions and only take medication prescribed specifically for you, as incorrect doses can be unsafe. Over-the-counter drugs (like painkillers or cold medicine) can be bought in supermarkets or pharmacies, but taking more than the recommended dose or mixing them with other substances is dangerous.

### Side Effects
All medications can cause unwanted symptoms known as side effects. These range from minor to serious and depend on factors like age, weight, sex, and overall health. Always read the packaging or consult a pharmacist to understand potential risks. If you experience severe side effects, contact a healthcare professional to discuss alternative treatments.

### Legal Recreational Drugs
In the UK, alcohol and nicotine (found in vapes and cigarettes) are legal for adults over 18. While legal, they can still have significant negative impacts on your health.

### New Psychoactive Substances (NPS)
Formerly known as ‘legal highs,’ these substances are designed to mimic the effects of illegal drugs like cocaine or cannabis. As of 2016, these are illegal to sell or give away. They are often made of unsafe chemicals and can be just as dangerous as other illegal substances.

## Illegal Recreational Drugs

Illegal drugs are classified into three categories based on their level of harm and the penalties associated with them:

* **Class A (Most harmful):** Heroin (smack), Cocaine (coke, crack), Ecstasy (MDMA, molly), Crystal meth, LSD (acid), and Magic mushrooms.
* **Class B:** Cannabis (weed, skunk), Ketamine (ket), and Amphetamines (speed).
* **Class C:** Benzodiazepines (valium, xanax), and Anabolic steroids.

**Legal Consequences:** Being caught with illegal drugs can lead to arrest, prison, and a criminal record, which may prevent you from traveling to countries like the USA or Australia.

## Peer Pressure

People take drugs for many reasons, including socialising, curiosity, or escaping difficult feelings like anxiety and stress. You may feel pressured by friends to experiment or carry drugs for them.

If you are experiencing peer pressure, remember:
* Real friends respect your decision to say "no."
* The majority of young people do not actually take drugs.
* Focus on your own safety and what feels right for you.
* If the pressure persists, consider if those people are the right friends for you.

## Risks and Effects of Drug Use

### Addiction
Addiction occurs when a person loses control over their substance use, despite it causing harm. Drugs can be chemically and behaviorally addictive, often because they produce a "high" that leads to a powerful craving for more.

### Impaired Judgement and Consent
Drugs and alcohol lower inhibitions and impair decision-making. This can lead to risky behaviors, including unprotected or non-consensual sex. Under the Sexual Offences Act, if someone is drunk or high, they may lack the capacity to consent to sexual activity.

### Physical and Mental Health
Drug use carries various health risks:
* **Short-term:** Breathing problems, heart issues, exhaustion, mood swings, paranoia, and "coming down" (feeling low after use).
* **Long-term:** Asthma, infertility, liver/kidney damage, depression, anxiety, schizophrenia, and memory loss.
* **Appearance:** Steroids can shrink genitals; volatile substances can cause facial rashes; cocaine can destroy the nasal septum; and meth can lead to tooth decay and skin ulcers.

### Overdose and 'Bad Trips'
An overdose happens when the body cannot cope with the amount of a substance taken. A "bad trip" is a distressing psychological experience caused by a drug. If someone is unresponsive, overheated, or having a severe reaction, call 999 immediately.

## Harm Reduction

The safest option is not to use drugs. However, if someone chooses to use them, they should:
* Never use alone; ensure someone knows what has been taken.
* Avoid mixing different drugs or mixing drugs with alcohol.
* Start with a small amount and wait for the effects.
* Stay hydrated and take regular breaks to cool down.

### Drink Spiking
Spiking is the illegal act of adding drugs or alcohol to someone's drink without their knowledge. This puts the victim at high risk of theft, assault, or medical emergencies.

## Seeking Support

### Signs of a Drug Problem
You might have a problem if you:
* Worry about when you will next have drugs.
* Use drugs to cope with stress or daily life.
* Notice your relationships or moods are changing significantly.
* Plan your social life entirely around drug use.

### Where to Get Help
Healthcare professionals in the UK have a duty of care and provide non-judgemental support. 
* **GP:** A doctor can refer you to local treatment services.
* **Talk to Frank:** Call 0300 123 6600 for 24/7 confidential advice.
* **Childline:** Call 0800 1111 (for those under 19).
* **Brook:** Local services can help you access support for drugs, alcohol, and smoking.
