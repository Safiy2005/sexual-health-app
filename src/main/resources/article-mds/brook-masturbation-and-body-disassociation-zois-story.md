# Masturbation and body disassociation: Zoi's Story

Zoi, 21, shares her story of how body disassociation has affected her relationship with masturbation and sexual intimacy.

**Content warning:** This page contains discussions of trauma and mental health issues.

## Understanding Body Disassociation

Body disassociation often manifests as a feeling of living entirely within one’s head, where the physical body feels like a foreign entity rather than a part of the self. For Zoi, this experience has been present for as long as she can remember. She describes her physical form as "functioning but never regarded," much like a piece of furniture in a room. 

For those experiencing this detachment:
* The physical self may feel separate from the mental or emotional self.
* Sensory experiences can feel muted or non-existent.
* Bodily functions, such as a menstrual period, may serve as the only tangible reminder of the body’s sentience.

## The Impact of Trauma and Self-Perception

Societal narratives often link a woman’s value directly to her body. For survivors of childhood trauma, this connection can be damaging. Disassociation can become a defense mechanism, but it also creates a sense of being "never deserving or good enough." This detachment can lead to feelings of being a "fraud" when confronted with modern narratives regarding self-satisfaction and body positivity.

Common internal struggles include:
* Difficulty looking in the mirror or acknowledging the body.
* Feeling unworthy of pleasure or self-derived love.
* Internalizing phrases like "no one will love you until you love yourself," which can create a sense of being sentenced to isolation.

## Navigating Expectations at University

The university environment often emphasizes sexual discovery and experimentation. For someone experiencing body disassociation, these pressures can be alienating. 

* **Social Pressure:** Peers may express confusion over a lack of "curiosity" or "horniness."
* **Mislabeling Identity:** Neutrality toward self-satisfaction or a reluctance to pursue sexual encounters is often mischaracterized as asexuality by others, regardless of one's actual queer identity.
* **The "Bad Feminist" Narrative:** There is often a sense of shame associated with not meeting the "feminist" ideal of masturbation as a tool of emancipation and agency.

## Reconciling Agency and Pleasure

In many feminist narratives, masturbation is viewed as a sign of agency over one’s body—a way to reclaim pleasure from patriarchal constraints. However, for those struggling with disassociation, the concept of self-induced pleasure can feel unreachable. 

Zoi’s journey involves recognizing that:
* Lack of sexual experience does not erase sexual expression.
* Personal desires are valid, even if they don't fit traditional narratives.
* Recovery from trauma is a process of reconciling the self with the body.

## A Message of Self-Worth

It is essential to recognize that everyone is worthy of love and desire, regardless of their relationship with their body or their level of sexual experience. You should not feel pressured to present yourself in a way that does not align with your internal reality. Finding a narrative that validates your experience is a crucial step in navigating life without shame.

## Seeking Support

Approximately 1 in 4 people in the UK will experience a mental health problem each year. If you are concerned about your mental health or your relationship with your body, support is available.

* You do not need a formal diagnosis to seek help.
* You can talk to someone whenever you feel ready.
* Resources are available for those dealing with trauma, disassociation, and sexual health concerns.
