# Thrush: Isabel's Story

Isabel* shares her experience of having thrush from the age of 13 and why we need to speak more openly about it.

## Early Experiences with Thrush

"I think I was probably about 13/14 when I first started to get thrush. I had no idea what it was and I remember feeling quite embarrassed about having problems down there so I just sort of just had to grin and bear it, as I didn’t really want to ask anyone for help. I felt as though I couldn’t talk about it or ask questions to others because it seemed kind of gross having something wrong with your vagina. On top of the embarrassment, I felt rather frustrated because of the physical itching and soreness too."

Isabel recalls not being taught about thrush at any point in her life. She initially discovered what it was through self-research on Google, by typing in her symptoms. Later, conversations with friends and her mum helped confirm her suspicions.

## Frequent Thrush and Coping Mechanisms

"I honestly couldn’t count the amount of times I’ve had it. About once a month, or every two months, between the ages of 14 to 20…so a lot!"

The frequency of her thrush was a significant source of distress. Over the years, Isabel tried various methods to manage it:

* Shaving less frequently
* Avoiding bubble baths
* Using only soaps she believed were suitable for intimate areas
* Not wearing knickers that caused itching or had synthetic gussets

While these adjustments offered some relief, she continued to experience thrush more often than she would have liked. The physical discomfort, combined with a feeling of guilt about not taking proper care of her body, was challenging. She also noted that it impacted her sexual confidence and could worsen if she had sex at the start of an infection, potentially passing it to her partner.

## Seeking Advice and Making Changes

Around the age of 20/21, Isabel decided to take more proactive steps.

* **Stopping Shaving:** She stopped shaving altogether. Her partner at the time was supportive, which helped her feel more comfortable with pubic hair and explore if this was a contributing factor to her thrush.
* **Consulting a Sexual Health Nurse:** She spoke to a sexual health nurse at her workplace and learned that an 'intimate' wash she had been using for years might be a cause. She immediately stopped using it.
* **Medical Advice:** Both the nurse and another doctor advised her to avoid using soaps on her vagina altogether. She also researched that if any product were to be used, it should be free of added perfume and colour and applied only around the labia, not directly near the vagina.

## Positive Outcomes and Broader Implications

"As a result of making all of these changes I now barely ever get thrush anymore."

Isabel now trims her pubic hair occasionally and uses no daily washes on her vagina, opting to rinse with the shower head. She acknowledges the initial struggle to feel sexy with pubic hair, especially given societal norms, but her supportive partner helped her embrace her body and prioritize her health over conforming to perceived beauty standards.

Isabel emphasizes the importance of increased education around thrush to reduce embarrassment. She believes that if thrush had been discussed in RSE lessons at school, she would have felt less ashamed. She stresses that thrush is a common and normal condition, not something to be considered "gross" or a cause for shame.

She also calls for more up-to-date and accurate education on the causes of thrush, criticizing brands that market "intimate" products that may actually cause issues. She advocates for incorporating discussions about over-grooming and its potential negative effects on the body into thrush education.

Isabel notes that she often experienced thrush when feeling run down or after increased sexual activity, suggesting that sharing such common triggers could empower individuals to better manage their bodies and prevent infections.

She concludes by highlighting the need for continued open conversations about sexual health, comparing it to the ongoing efforts to destigmatize discussions around sex and sexual health. While she still experiences some residual embarrassment from her younger experiences, she hopes that future generations will be able to discuss and manage thrush as a common ailment.
