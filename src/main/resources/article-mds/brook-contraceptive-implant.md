# The Contraceptive Implant: What is Nexplanon & How Long does it Last?

## What is the Contraceptive Implant?

The contraceptive implant is a small, flexible plastic rod that is placed just under the skin in the upper arm. It releases the hormone progestogen to prevent pregnancy. Nexplanon is the main contraceptive implant currently in use in the UK.

### How it Works:

The implant releases the hormone progestogen (similar to the hormone progesterone that is produced naturally in the ovaries) into your bloodstream to prevent pregnancy. The implant does this by:

* Stopping ovulation
* Thickening the mucus around the cervix, making it harder for sperm to get through
* Thinning the lining of the womb, so that a fertilised egg is less likely to implant

The implant is a method of long-acting reversible contraception (LARC). These methods do not rely on you remembering to take them, but they do need a trained doctor or nurse to administer or fit them for you.

## Key Information

* **Hormones:** Contains progestogen
* **Mood/Emotions:** Can help with feeling low or anxious
* **STIs:** No protection against STIs
* **Visibility:** Unlikely anyone else will see it
* **Periods:** May become irregular or stop altogether
* **Acne/Skin:** Can cause acne
* **Effectiveness:** More than 99% effective
* **Duration:** Lasts up to three years

## Starting the Implant

### Appointment:

You will need to book an appointment with a contraceptive clinic, your GP, or Brook. This appointment will typically include:

* A discussion about your medical and family history to determine the best method for you.
* A review of any other medications you are taking, as some can make the implant less effective.
* Your blood pressure will be taken, and you will be weighed.
* You will **not** be required to have a vaginal or breast examination for the implant.

### Fitting:

Some services may be able to fit the implant at the same appointment, or you may need a second appointment.

* The implant is about 4cm long (about the size of a matchstick or hair pin).
* It is inserted under the skin in your upper arm by a specially trained doctor or nurse.
* A local anaesthetic injection will be given to numb the area before insertion.
* The procedure takes only a few minutes.
* No stitches are required.
* The area may be slightly tender for a few days, but a small dressing will be applied to protect it and help stop any bruising.

### When to see a doctor:

* If you cannot feel your implant or it appears to have changed shape. (Be careful not to squeeze or play with the implant, as this can occasionally cause the rod to break.)
* If you notice any skin changes or pain around the site of the implant.

## Effectiveness and Protection

* **Preventing Pregnancy:** More than 99% effective.
* **Immediate Protection:**
 * If the implant is fitted during the first five days of your period, you will be protected from pregnancy immediately.
 * If fitted on any other day of your menstrual cycle, you need to use condoms or internal condoms for the first seven days.
 * You may also be required to take a pregnancy test after the implant has been fitted.
* **STI Protection:** The implant offers **no protection** against sexually transmitted infections (STIs). Condoms should be used to protect against STIs.

## Pros and Cons

### Pros:

* Over 99% effective.
* Not visible and no interruption to sex.
* Works for up to three years.
* An option for people who cannot use contraception containing oestrogen (such as the combined pill, contraceptive patch, or vaginal ring).
* Fertility returns to normal as soon as it is removed.
* Unaffected by diarrhoea or vomiting.
* Can help reduce heavy periods and period pain.
* An eco-friendly method of contraception due to its long duration.

### Cons:

* Temporary side effects in the first few months are common, including headaches, nausea, breast tenderness, and mood swings.
* Can cause or worsen acne.
* No protection against sexually transmitted infections (STIs).
* Effectiveness can be reduced by some medicines.
* Periods may become lighter, shorter, or less painful. They can also become irregular, longer, or heavier. It is also common for periods to stop altogether while using the implant.

## Periods and the Implant

Periods may become lighter, shorter, or less painful. They also might become irregular, longer, or heavier. It is also common for periods to stop altogether whilst using the implant.

### Is it harmful for your periods to stop altogether?

For some people, the implant can cause periods to stop completely. This is not harmful, and your fertility will return to normal once the implant is removed. Some people prefer to have a period while others prefer not to, so it’s useful to think about this before getting the implant.

## Implant Fitting After Pregnancy, Abortion, or Miscarriage

### After Pregnancy:

* You can get pregnant from as little as three weeks after giving birth.
* The implant can be fitted any time after giving birth.
* If fitted within three weeks of the birth, you are protected immediately. If not, you will need to use alternative contraception for seven days.
* The implant is safe to use while breastfeeding.

### After Abortion or Miscarriage:

* You can become pregnant from two weeks after an abortion or miscarriage.
* The implant can be fitted straight away after an abortion or miscarriage, and you will be protected immediately from pregnancy.

Speak to your midwife, GP, or a nurse at a sexual health clinic to check if this method is right for you.

## Interaction with Medicines

The effectiveness of the implant can be reduced by some medicines, including those used to treat epilepsy, HIV, and TB, and the complementary medicine St John's Wort. Commonly used antibiotics do not typically reduce the effectiveness of the implant, but it is always best to check with your doctor, nurse, or pharmacist first.

## Implant Removal

The implant can be left in place for up to three years.

The implant has to be removed by a specially trained doctor or nurse. They will:

* Give you a local anaesthetic.
* Make a small cut in your skin.
* Gently pull the implant out.
* Apply a dressing to your arm (which you should keep on for a few days to reduce bruising and to keep it clean and dry).

If you want to continue using the implant, a new one can be inserted at the same time, and you will remain protected from pregnancy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### When can I have sex after my implant fitting?

You can typically resume sexual activity a few days after implant insertion. However, to ensure immediate effectiveness, it’s important to consider the following:

* If you inserted the implant during the first 5 days of your period, it’s immediately effective.
* Otherwise, you’ll need to use a barrier method of contraception, like condoms, for the first week.
* If you were already using another reliable hormonal contraceptive before the implant insertion, it might be immediately effective. It’s best to consult with your healthcare provider to determine the specific effectiveness in your case.

### Does getting the implant hurt?

The level of discomfort during and after implant insertion varies from person to person. While most people experience only mild discomfort or a feeling of pressure during the procedure, some may feel slight pain or discomfort. You may also experience some soreness or bruising at the site afterwards, but this usually subsides after a few days. If you’re feeling particularly anxious about pain, speak to your healthcare provider in advance to discuss pain relief options.
