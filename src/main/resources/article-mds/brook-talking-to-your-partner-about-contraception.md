# Talking to your partner about contraception

Communication is key for having great sex! Talking to your partner about contraception can help make the experience safer and more enjoyable for everyone. Some people feel comfortable talking about sex, while others find the idea uncomfortable, embarrassing, or daunting. However, discussing contraception and STI history before engaging in sexual activity can help relieve anxieties and make the experience better for everyone involved.

## It takes two
The responsibility of preventing unwanted pregnancy should not be placed on one person—it takes two. If you cannot get pregnant alone, you shouldn’t have to prevent pregnancy alone either.

## Making informed decisions around safe sex
Assuming that contraception is solely your partner’s responsibility could lead to unwise decisions and a higher risk of unwanted pregnancy. Talking about what contraception you are using, what that means for your sex life, and whether you want to change methods ensures both of you are aware of the risks and can make joint decisions.

Sharing the emotional burden can reduce anxiety. If you feel comfortable, you may want to share with your partner if you have forgotten to take a pill or change a patch. This helps both of you understand if the risk of pregnancy is higher and work out what needs to happen next, such as seeking emergency contraception or using additional methods like condoms.

**Important:** Talking to your partner about whether you need additional contraception or emergency contraception should not be a replacement for seeking medical advice. If you’re unsure, speak to a nurse at a sexual health clinic, a doctor, or a pharmacist.

## How you can support each other
Depending on your relationship, there are many ways you can support each other regarding contraception:
* **Reminders:** If one person struggles to remember a daily pill, the other can help remind them.
* **Appointments:** You can accompany each other to medical appointments.
* **Side effects:** Some methods have side effects (like headaches, nausea, or mood swings) that usually disappear after a few months. Explaining these effects helps your partner understand what you are going through.
* **Involvement:** Some methods rely on clear communication and partner involvement to be effective, including the fertility awareness method, condoms, diaphragms, and caps.

## Your body, your choice
You should always respect your partner’s choice of contraception, and they should always respect yours. You should never feel pressured to avoid using contraception or condoms.

* **Timing:** It is best to talk about safe sex before "the heat of the moment."
* **Environment:** Try to have the conversation somewhere private where you both feel comfortable.
* **Medium:** If a face-to-face conversation feels like too much, you could talk on the phone or via message, provided you are sure you both understand each other.
* **Sobriety:** Conversations should happen when you are both sober. Alcohol and drugs can lead to misunderstandings and affect your ability to consent to sex.

## Discussing STI history
A conversation about contraception is also a good opportunity to discuss STI history. This helps you make informed decisions about using barrier methods (like external or internal condoms). You may decide to wait to have sex until you have both completed an STI test.

Condoms are the only method of contraception that protects against STIs. Unless you are in an exclusive relationship and are being tested regularly, it is recommended to use condoms even if you are using another method like the pill, implant, injection, or coil.

## Conversation starters
If you’re not sure how to bring up the topic, try these approaches:

**Say it directly:**
* “I’m on the / I use the [insert method here].”
* “I’m not on any contraception.”
* “My method of contraception isn’t 100% effective, can we use condoms?”
* “It’s really important to me that I have safe sex—that way I enjoy it more.”

**Ask them about prevention:**
* “Are you on any contraception?”
* “I always use condoms, but are you on any contraception as well?”
* “I’m not comfortable with pulling out—is there a reason you don’t like using condoms?”
* “I’m exploring my contraception options at the moment. What do you use?”

**Work it into conversation:**
* “I don’t know how people manage to look after a child, I’m so glad I’m on contraception!”
* “I’m going to the shops, should I get some condoms?”
* “Can you remind me tomorrow to start on a new ring/patch/pill pack?”

## Common questions and concerns

### I don’t like condoms and my partner is on contraception, is it ok not to wear them?
This is a conversation you must have with your partner. They may have reasons for wanting to use condoms, such as "doubling up" methods to maximize protection. You also need to consider STIs. If your partner wants to continue using condoms, you must respect that choice. You might explore different types or sizes of condoms to improve the experience.

### I’m changing my contraception, do I need to tell my partner?
While it depends on your relationship, it is a good idea to keep them informed. The risk of pregnancy might be higher while your body adjusts, and some changes require using alternative contraception for seven days.

### My partner doesn’t like condoms and wants me to go on a different method, what should I do?
If you want to use condoms, you have every right to do so. You should never feel pressured to change your contraception method; it is your body and your choice. Talk to your partner about why they dislike condoms—sometimes changing the size or type can help—but do not feel forced to switch to a hormonal or long-acting method if you don't want to.

### I’m struggling to bring up the topic, what can I do?
Talking about contraception shows responsibility. If you are nervous, try practicing the conversation with a friend first. If you find it impossible to discuss with a potential partner, it may be a sign that you aren't quite ready to have sex with that person yet.

### We’ve had sex without condoms before, but I want to use them again. Is that unreasonable?
Not at all. You have the right to choose to use a condom every single time you have sex. Consenting to sex without a condom in the past does not mean you cannot choose to use them next time.
