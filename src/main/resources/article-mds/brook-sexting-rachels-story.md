# Sexting: Rachel's Story

Rachel, 20, shares her personal experiences with sexting, exploring how it can be a source of validation and enjoyment, as well as the risks associated with digital intimacy and harassment.

> **Content warning:** This page includes discussions of harassment and stalking.

## Early Experiences and Validation

Rachel began sexting early in her relationships, often before using encrypted messaging platforms. For her, the experience was initially centered on self-indulgence and validation:

* **New Perspectives:** Having not felt like a "hot girl" in school, receiving positive attention through sexting was a powerful and validating experience.
* **Relationship Building:** In her first relationship, sexting was used to build excitement and stay connected throughout the day.
* **The "Buzz":** After her first relationship, Rachel used dating apps like Tinder to initiate contact. Sending photos to older men provided a rewarding "buzz" and a sense of appeal.

## The Risks of Digital Objectification

Over time, Rachel began to notice the downsides of constant external validation. She describes becoming "hooked" on the attention, which eventually led to a shift in how she viewed herself and her interactions:

* **Loss of Spark:** The excitement of sending photos to strangers eventually faded, leaving her feeling like an object rather than a person.
* **Harassment:** After rejecting a man she had been talking to on Snapchat, Rachel experienced a year of persistent harassment. The individual tracked her across Facebook and Instagram, created multiple fake accounts to bypass blocks, and even contacted her friends to reach her.
* **Consent and Control:** Rachel encountered issues with boundaries, such as individuals screenshotting her photos without mutual enthusiasm and refusing to confirm if they had deleted the images after the relationship ended.

## Lessons Learned and Setting Boundaries

Reflecting on her journey, Rachel has developed a healthier approach to her digital presence and self-image:

* **Prioritizing Trust:** She is now much more comfortable holding back and only sending photos to people she knows and trusts.
* **Self-Validation:** Rachel realized that her need for external validation stemmed from insecurity. She has since learned to see herself as "good naked" without needing others to confirm it.
* **Personal Agency:** While she still expresses her sexuality online (e.g., posting bikini or lingerie photos), she does so after "checking in" with herself to ensure she is doing it for her own enjoyment rather than for someone else's approval.
* **Rejecting Blame:** Rachel emphasizes that regardless of whether someone sends nudes, harassment and "creepy" behavior are never the fault of the person being targeted.

## Summary of Advice

* **Check-in with yourself:** Ask why you are sending a photo and if it is for your own benefit or someone else's.
* **Trust is key:** Ensure you have an established level of trust before sharing intimate images.
* **Understand the risks:** Be aware that you cannot always control what happens to an image once it is sent, but remember that harassment is never your fault.
* **Value your own opinion:** Internal validation is more sustainable than seeking constant external approval.
