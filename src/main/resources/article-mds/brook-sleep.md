# Sleep

Sleep is a regular period of rest which allows the brain and body to slow down and engage in processes of recovery. It promotes better physical and mental performance the next day and in the long term. When you don’t sleep, these important processes are short-circuited, affecting thinking, concentration, energy levels, and mood.

Sleep is a very individual experience; every person is unique and will have a sleep cycle and preferences that suit them. As Thomas Dekker (1572-1632) said, "Sleep is the golden chain that ties health and our bodies together."

## How Sleep Works

Internal body clocks control when you are awake and when your body is ready for sleep, typically operating on 24-hour cycles. As you fall asleep, your body temperature drops, brain activity ramps down, and heart rate and respiration slow.

There are four stages of sleep divided into two categories:

### Stages 1 and 2 (Non-REM)
* **Stage 1:** The initial "dozing off" period, usually lasting 1-5 minutes. You drift in and out of sleep and can be awoken easily.
* **Stage 2:** Lasts between 10-60 minutes. This involves further slowing of brain and body activity.

### Stage 3 (Non-REM Deep Sleep)
Also known as slow-wave sleep or delta sleep, this stage lasts 20-40 minutes. This is the deepest part of non-REM sleep where muscles relax and brain waves slow significantly. It is essential for the recuperation of the body, effective thinking, and memory.

### Stage 4 (REM Sleep)
REM (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep is the most essential stage for the brain, enabling key functions like memory and learning. During this stage, brain activity picks up significantly, and most of the body experiences temporary paralysis. Most intense dreaming occurs during REM sleep.

## The Importance of Sleep

### Physical Health and Development
Sleep contributes to the effective function of virtually every system in the body. It empowers the immune system, regulates hormones, and enables muscle and tissue recovery. A lack of sleep is linked to health problems such as heart disease, type 2 diabetes, and high blood pressure.

### Thinking and Cognitive Function
Sleep promotes attention, memory, and analytical thought. It helps the brain recognize important information to consolidate learning and facilitates creativity, which is essential for studying or learning new skills.

### Mental Health and Wellbeing
There is a close relationship between sleep and mental health. Poor sleep can lead to irritability, exhaustion, and long-term consequences like depression and anxiety. Sleep deprivation can make you feel lonely, isolated, and make it difficult to concentrate or make decisions.

### Decision Making
Lack of sleep can affect the development of the part of the brain that controls impulsive behavior. This makes it more likely for an individual to engage in high-risk behaviors and can negatively affect academic performance and relationships.

## Development During Puberty

The teenage years are a formative period of significant development. A minimum of 8-10 hours of good sleep is recommended for young people.

During adolescence, the brain produces melatonin (the sleep-regulating hormone) later at night than in children or adults. This causes the circadian rhythm to reset, naturally leading young people to fall asleep later and wake up later in the morning. Deep sleep is also an important factor in the onset of puberty.

## Tips for Improving Your Sleep

Improving sleep quality is often about making small, realistic changes to your habits.

* **Have a good routine:** Do the same things in the same order an hour before bed. Keep this consistent, even on weekends.
* **Limit screens in the bedroom:** Avoid mobiles, tablets, or TVs in the bedroom. Aim for at least one hour of screen-free time before sleep.
* **Exercise regularly:** Physical activity helps you sleep more soundly. Teenagers should aim for at least 60 minutes of exercise daily.
* **Relax before bed:** Try a warm bath, quiet music, or gentle yoga.
* **Cut out caffeine:** Reduce intake of cola, tea, and coffee, as caffeine can prevent you from falling asleep.
* **Avoid smoking and vaping:** Nicotine is a stimulant that disrupts sleep patterns.
* **Manage food and drink intake:** Avoid eating too much or too little close to bedtime. Avoid alcohol, as it interferes with sleep quality.
* **Create a sleep-friendly environment:** Ensure your room is dark, cool, quiet, and comfortable.
* **Don't toss and turn:** If you can’t sleep, get up and do something relaxing until you feel sleepy again.
* **Write away worries:** Set aside time before bed to make plans for the next day to clear your mind.
* **Talk through problems:** Support from friends or trusted adults can help you feel less overwhelmed.
* **Get outside in the morning:** Sunlight within the first hour of waking boosts cortisol, helping you stay awake during the day.

## Seeking Support

If you have been struggling with sleep and self-help tips do not work, it is important to talk to your GP. They may recommend a sleep study to look at your nighttime patterns or recommend treatments for underlying disturbances. Treating sleep issues early is vital for protecting both physical and mental wellbeing.
