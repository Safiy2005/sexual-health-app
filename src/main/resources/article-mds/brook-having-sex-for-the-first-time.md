# Having Sex for the First Time

When you decide to have sex for the first time, there’s no right or wrong way to do it. It's important to feel comfortable and do what makes you feel good. Many people feel a little nervous before their first sexual experience, which is natural. These nerves should be linked to excitement rather than fear or panic.

Good sex is more about feeling comfortable with someone, trusting them, and being able to communicate than it is about having the perfect technique.

## What We Mean by ‘Sex’

Often, when people talk about having sex for the first time, they mean penetrative vaginal sex or anal sex. At Brook, we define sex as all forms of sexual contact between two or more people, which can include penetrative sex, oral sex, and fingering, among other activities. There are many ways to be sexually intimate and experience pleasure. This means that having ‘sex’ and having sex ‘for the first time’ can mean different things for different people.

Our number one tip for great sex is to ensure everyone involved is consenting at all times. No one should ever feel they have to do something they are not comfortable with or don’t want to do. Consenting to one thing does not mean consenting to something else, and it is completely okay to say no or stop at any point.

If your partner changes their mind, it’s okay to feel disappointed. However, if you feel so unhappy that your partner feels they have to have sex to cheer you up, this is not consensual.

There can be pressure to have sex, but take your time and ask yourself if you're ready. You might be thinking about sex because your friends have (or say they have), because your partner wants to, or because characters in your favorite shows are doing it. There is no specific age by which you should have had sex for the first time; the right time is when you are ready and want to. Remember, you can change your mind at any point. You should never feel pressured by anyone else to have sex.

## Have a Conversation (or Lots of Them!)

Talk about what you want to do, your boundaries, your worries, how you will ensure you have safe sex, and anything else you want to discuss with the person you are going to have sex with. Communication is key to consensual and enjoyable sex.

### I’m Worried What They Will Think About My Body

Worrying about your partner's thoughts on your body is common, especially during a first sexual experience. Remember:

* We are constantly bombarded with ideas about how our bodies should look. In reality, everyone’s body is different and all bodies are wonderful.
* You and your partner are in the same position, and they may also have concerns about their body and what you will think. Talking about this beforehand can help both of you feel more relaxed.
* If you both want to have sex together, your focus will be on enjoying the moment and each other.
* Your partner should always be respectful of you and your body, and you should be respectful of theirs. If they are doing or saying things that make you feel uncomfortable about your body, let them know. If they continue after you've asked them to stop, they are not showing you the respect you deserve, and you should consider whether they are someone you want to be having sex with.

### Safe Sex

It is important to keep in mind that you can still get a sexually transmitted infection (STI), even if it's your first time. If one of you has a penis and the other a vagina, pregnancy is also possible, so it's crucial to have contraception sorted first. It’s also harder to get excited about sex if you're worried about pregnancy or STIs.

Many people like to visit a sexual health clinic before they have sex to get free condoms and become comfortable with a type of contraception before engaging in sexual activity.

* **Condoms:** Condoms are the only method of contraception that also protects against STIs. We recommend getting familiar with condoms before sex so you feel confident using them. You should never feel pressured to have sex without a condom, just as you should never feel pressured to have sex at all. You have the right to choose to use condoms every time.

## Managing Nerves and Readiness

The more relaxed you are, the better the whole experience will be. Feeling a little nervous before your first sexual experience is natural, but you shouldn’t feel stressed or tense. Having sex for the first time should be something you want to do and look forward to, not something that causes anxiety or distress.

If nerves are overwhelming:

* Try slowing things down. Take time kissing and touching each other, exploring each other’s erogenous zones.
* Step back from intercourse and try something else, like giving each other a massage or taking a bath or shower together.

### What are Erogenous Zones?

An erogenous zone is an area of your body that is very sensitive and can produce a sexual response when touched. Learning which parts of your body feel good to be touched is an important part of discovering your sexuality.

* **Common examples for people with penises:** Penis, scrotum, anus, prostate, and nipples.
* **Common examples for people with vulvas:** Vulva, vagina, anus, and nipples.
* **Other areas can include:** Neck, ears, back, hips, and thighs.

If the nerves are too much or you decide you’re not ready, that’s completely fine. There is no rush, and you can always try again another time if you want to. If your partner changes their mind, is too nervous, or doesn’t feel ready for sex, you should respect their decision, even if it's disappointing for you. Forcing or pressuring them to continue is not consensual.

Sometimes, even if you really want to have sex, your body may not respond as expected. Erections, vaginal wetness, and relaxed muscles might not happen. Trying to have sex when your body isn’t ready could result in pain or discomfort, making the experience unenjoyable. Be comfortable with the possibility that even if you’ve been looking forward to it, you might not be able to proceed, and that’s okay. There is no rush.

## Sex Shouldn’t Hurt

Penetrative sex for the first time can feel really good and, as long as you take your time and go gently, it shouldn’t be painful or uncomfortable (though it might feel a bit strange at first). If it is, it might be a sign that you are not aroused enough or are feeling nervous.

* Using a water-based lube can help with penetrative sex by making things more slippery.
* If you are having anal sex, you will need to use lots of lube because the anus and rectum are not self-lubricating.

It is common for people with vaginas to worry that their vagina will be stretched or damaged from penetrative sex. In reality, penis-in-vagina sex does not change the size or shape of the vagina. The vagina is like an elastic tube that stretches to accommodate a penis, whatever its size, and then returns to its original size and shape afterwards.

Sex in real life isn’t always like we see in movies or porn. Things don’t always go smoothly; there can be moments of awkwardness, amusement, and messiness. This is why feeling ready, comfortable, and communicating with your partner is so important. If something is awkward, talk about it. If something is funny, laugh together. Ultimately, this will help you feel more relaxed and make the whole experience more enjoyable.
