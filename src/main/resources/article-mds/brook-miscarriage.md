# Miscarriage: What Are The Signs of a Miscarriage & How Common Is It?

Miscarriage is when a pregnancy is lost naturally in the first 23 weeks. It is different from a stillbirth (when a baby is born after the 23rd week with no signs of life) or an abortion (when a pregnancy is ended through medical means). 

Miscarriage is common, with about one in every five pregnancies ending naturally. Most miscarriages happen within the first 12 weeks of pregnancy; sometimes, a person may miscarry before they even realize they are pregnant.

## Causes of Miscarriage

It can be difficult to determine the exact cause of a miscarriage, but it is very unlikely to be caused by anything you have done or not done. 

* **Chromosomal Problems:** Most miscarriages are thought to be caused by problems with the development of the baby (embryo or foetus). Problems with chromosomes—the genetic building blocks—can prevent the baby from developing normally.
* **Medical History:** Risk factors include having had previous miscarriages.
* **Health and Lifestyle:** Being very overweight, using certain drugs, consuming a lot of alcohol, or smoking during pregnancy can increase risk. Reducing these habits helps support a healthier pregnancy.
* **Recurrent Miscarriage:** If someone has more than two miscarriages in a row, they are usually offered tests to find the cause, though often no obvious cause is found.

## Myths About Miscarriage

It is important to know that the following activities do **not** increase your chance of having a miscarriage:
* Having sex during pregnancy
* Exercising
* Having an argument
* Continuing to work

## Stillbirth

If a pregnancy loss happens after 24 weeks, it is classified as a stillbirth. This occurs in approximately one in every 200 births and is often caused by problems with the placenta.

## Ectopic Pregnancy

An ectopic pregnancy occurs when the pregnancy starts to develop outside the womb, usually in one of the Fallopian tubes. It occurs in 1 to 2 out of every 100 pregnancies and can be dangerous as the pregnancy cannot continue to grow there.

Symptoms that require immediate medical attention include:
* Irregular vaginal bleeding
* Pain low down in the abdomen
* Pain where the shoulder and arm meet
* Diarrhoea or pain when using the toilet
* Collapsing, feeling faint, or a fast heartbeat

## Molar Pregnancy

A molar pregnancy is a rare cause of pregnancy loss (1 in 600 pregnancies) where cells do not develop properly and instead become a tumor-like mass. It is usually removed via surgical suction.

Symptoms can include:
* Vaginal bleeding or brown-red discharge
* Severe nausea/vomiting (more than a normal pregnancy)

## Signs and Symptoms of Miscarriage

The most common sign of miscarriage is vaginal bleeding. While light spotting is common in early pregnancy, you should contact your GP if you experience:
* Vaginal bleeding
* Cramping (similar to period pains)
* Back pain

**Go to Accident and Emergency (A&E) or call 999 if:**
* Bleeding is very heavy.
* Pain is unbearable or located in the shoulder.
* You feel very faint or dizzy.

## Managing a Miscarriage

If a scan confirms that a miscarriage has occurred but the tissue remains in the womb, there are three main ways to manage the process:

* **Expectant Management:** Waiting for the tissue to pass naturally. This can take several weeks and involves pain and bleeding.
* **Medical Management:** Taking pills that cause the cervix to open and the tissue to pass. This usually involves heavy bleeding and pain, requiring painkillers.
* **Surgical Management:** A minor operation (suction) under local or general anaesthetic to remove the pregnancy tissue. This is often recommended if there is heavy bleeding, infection, or if other methods fail.

## Emotional Support and Wellbeing

Feelings following a miscarriage can range from grief and sadness to relief or confusion. There is no "right" way to feel, and the time it takes to feel better varies for everyone.

* **Talking about it:** It can be hard to tell people, especially if you hadn't shared the pregnancy yet. However, reaching out to partners, friends, or family can help.
* **Support for Partners:** Partners may also feel deep sadness and should not feel they have to "bottle up" emotions to be strong for the other person.
* **Informing Children:** If you have other children, it is often helpful to talk to them openly about what has happened in an age-appropriate way.

## Remembering Your Baby

Many people find comfort in marking the life of the baby, even if the pregnancy was short.
* **Memorials:** You may choose to have a burial, a memorial service, or use songs and poems to remember them.
* **Certificates:** While deaths before 24 weeks are not officially registered, many hospitals can provide a commemorative certificate upon request.
* **Naming:** Some parents find it helpful to give the baby a name.
