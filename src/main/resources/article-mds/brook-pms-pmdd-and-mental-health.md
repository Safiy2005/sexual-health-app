# PMS, PMDD and Mental Health

## Understanding PMS and PMDD

Everyone experiences PMS (pre-menstrual syndrome) differently. PMS refers to the symptoms people can experience in the week or so before their period starts. While many people experience PMS, its intensity and the specific symptoms can vary greatly.

### Key Differences in PMS Experience:

* **Different Symptoms:** Symptoms can be psychological, emotional, or physical. Some individuals experience a mix, while others may only have one type of symptom.
* **Different Intensity:** The severity of symptoms varies. Some may notice a clear link between hormone changes and their feelings, impacting their ability to work or study, while others' symptoms may be barely noticeable.
* **Changes Over Time:** Symptoms can evolve with age, such as during perimenopause or after pregnancy. You might also experience different symptoms each month, or some months may be more challenging than others.
* **Different Genders:** Transgender or non-binary individuals who menstruate can also experience PMS, which may cause additional discomfort related to their gender identity.

This page focuses on the mental health aspects of PMS. For information on the physical changes during the menstrual cycle, please refer to [relevant external resources].

## Causes and Symptoms of PMS

The exact cause of PMS is unknown, but it's believed to be linked to changing hormone levels during the luteal phase of the menstrual cycle, between ovulation and your period. During this phase, oestrogen levels decline while progesterone levels rise. Progesterone's role in preparing the body for pregnancy can naturally lead to a desire to withdraw socially and take things easier.

PMS can manifest with various mood-related symptoms, including:

* Tiredness and difficulty sleeping
* Irritability
* Anxiety
* Poor concentration
* Feeling moody and/or tearful

### Premenstrual Exacerbation

PMS can coincide with existing mental health conditions, potentially worsening those symptoms. This is known as premenstrual exacerbation. Unlike PMDD, PMS symptoms typically resolve during or shortly after your period.

## Lifestyle Changes to Ease PMS Symptoms

If PMS is affecting your mood, certain lifestyle adjustments can help manage symptoms:

### Track Your Cycle

Understanding when PMS is likely to occur can help you prepare. Knowing when you might experience low energy or anxiety can empower you to manage your experience:

* Be gentle with yourself on days you don't feel your best.
* Allow yourself time to rest.
* Plan social activities or important projects during periods of higher energy.

You can track your cycle using a diary or apps like Flo, which can estimate your period start date.

### Diet

* **Cut down on sugars:** Sugars can cause rapid energy fluctuations, potentially worsening tiredness and anxiety.
* **Prioritise protein:** Protein helps slow sugar absorption, aiding in sustained energy levels.
* **Eat regularly:** Increased appetite during this phase is common. Eating smaller, frequent, healthy meals can help manage PMS.
* **Avoid alcohol:** Alcohol can temporarily increase testosterone and oestrogen, potentially leading to mood swings and increased anxiety.
* **Limit caffeine:** While tempting for energy, caffeine can disrupt sleep patterns and may worsen period cramps.

### Exercise

* Exercise can boost energy levels and improve sleep quality. It doesn't need to be high intensity; gentle activities like yoga, swimming, or walking are beneficial.

### Rest

* Prioritise rest and sleep, as they are crucial during this phase of your cycle. It's okay to have days where you simply need to relax.

### Minimise/Manage Stress

* Stress can exacerbate PMS symptoms like anxiety and irritability, and may also increase period pain. Exercise is a good stress reliever, as are quiet weekends and dedicated free time.

### Quit Vaping or Smoking

* Nicotine in e-cigarettes and cigarettes can worsen PMS symptoms by increasing your sensitivity to stress.

### Talk About It

* PMS can impact relationships and social interaction. Discussing your feelings with loved ones can foster understanding and support. Open communication can help others offer assistance, help you re-prioritise, or provide understanding.

### Pay Attention to Your Feelings

* While it's easy to attribute all feelings to PMS, these emotions may be signalling underlying issues. Use this time for reflection to address any negative emotions or concerns. Explore deeper reasons behind feelings of tearfulness or irritability.

## Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD)

If PMS symptoms drastically impact your daily life, work, education, relationships, or mental health, even with lifestyle changes, you may have Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD).

PMDD is a severe form of PMS and a mental health condition affecting approximately 3-8% of people with periods. It's thought that individuals with PMDD may be more sensitive to hormonal changes.

### Symptoms of PMDD

People with PMDD often describe feeling like a different person during the premenstrual phase, with severe mood swings that significantly affect all areas of their life and a feeling of being unable to cope.

**How PMDD Differs from PMS:**

PMDD shares many symptoms with PMS, but they are more intense and have a more profound impact on daily life and relationships.

Some common PMDD symptoms include:

* Depression
* Extreme irritability and/or anxiety
* Suicidal thoughts, feelings of hopelessness
* Feeling overwhelmed or out of control
* Panic attacks
* Appetite changes
* Significant mood changes

PMDD can also present with physical symptoms like muscle pain, breast tenderness, bloating, cramps, and headaches. Symptoms typically begin after ovulation and usually subside by the week after your period.

### Seeking Support for PMDD

There is a lack of awareness and understanding surrounding PMDD, which can make it challenging to receive adequate support. However, you don't have to endure these symptoms as a normal part of life. Treatments are available to ease symptoms.

If you believe PMS is significantly affecting your life, whether or not you suspect PMDD, contacting your GP is recommended.

**Organisations Specialising in PMDD:**

* **The International Association for Premenstrual Disorders:** Offers a question and knowledge bank, peer support, and support groups.
* **National Association for Premenstrual Syndromes:** Provides information and advice.

### Managing PMDD and Severe PMS

Keeping a symptom diary, tracking your menstrual cycle, and noting your daily feelings can be beneficial. This diary can help you prepare for symptom fluctuations, identify potential triggers, and provide valuable information to your GP.

**Potential Treatments and Support:**

* **Lifestyle Changes:** Small adjustments to diet, exercise, and sleep can help alleviate symptoms.
* **Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT):** This talking therapy helps identify and modify negative thought patterns affecting emotions and behaviours, aiding in managing anxiety and depression. Your GP can refer you for NHS talking therapies, or you can find private therapists online.
* **Combined Pill:** For some, the combined pill can help by preventing ovulation, thus reducing hormonal fluctuations. However, for others, it may worsen PMDD symptoms. It's a process of finding what works for you.
* **Medication:** Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs) can be prescribed to manage symptoms during the most challenging periods and are often used alongside therapy.
