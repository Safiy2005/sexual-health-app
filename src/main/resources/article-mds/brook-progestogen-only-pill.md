# Progestogen-Only Pill: The Mini Pill (POP)

## What is the Mini Pill (POP)?

The progestogen-only pill, also known as the 'mini-pill' or POP, prevents pregnancy by using the hormone progestogen.

## How Does It Work?

The progestogen-only pill prevents pregnancy in two main ways:

* **Thickening cervical mucus:** This makes it difficult for sperm to reach an egg.
* **Preventing ovulation:** Sometimes, it stops your ovaries from releasing an egg. This is the primary action of POPs containing the hormone desogestrel.

## Effectiveness

* **More than 99% effective** if used without mistakes.

## Pros

* No interruption to sex.
* Over 99% effective if taken correctly.
* No long-term effects on fertility.
* An option for individuals who cannot use contraception containing oestrogen.
* Can help with painful periods and premenstrual symptoms.

## Cons

* No protection against STIs.
* May experience spotting or irregular periods.
* Must be taken around the same time every day (within three or 12 hours, depending on the pill type).
* Effectiveness can be reduced by certain medicines, types of antibiotic, vomiting, and diarrhoea.
* Temporary side effects in the first few months may include spots, headaches, weight change, and breast tenderness.

## Periods

When taking the progestogen-only pill, your periods may:

* Become irregular.
* Become lighter.
* Become less painful.
* Stop altogether.
* You may also experience spotting between periods.

## Getting the Progestogen-Only Pill

You can obtain the progestogen-only pill in the following ways:

* **Prescription:** From a doctor or nurse at a contraceptive clinic, GP surgery, or Brook.
* **Over-the-counter:** From some pharmacies.

Contraception and sexual health services like Brook are free and confidential for everyone, including those under 16.

## At Your Appointment

An appointment to get the progestogen-only pill will typically include:

* Questions about your medical and family history to determine the best method for you.
* A discussion about any other medications you are taking, as some can affect the pill's effectiveness.

You will not be required to have an internal or breast examination for the progestogen-only pill.

## Starting the Pill

* **During your period:** If you start taking the pill within the first five days of your period, you will be protected against pregnancy immediately.
* **Short menstrual cycle:** If you have a short menstrual cycle (23 days or less), starting on the fifth day of your period might mean you are not immediately protected. You may need to use additional contraception (like condoms) for the first two days.
* **Other times in your cycle:** If you start at any other time in your menstrual cycle, it will take two days to become effective. You will need to use condoms for the first two days.

## Starting After Pregnancy

* **After giving birth:** You can start the progestogen-only pill any time after birth. If you start within the first three weeks (before day 21), you are protected immediately. If you start 21 days or later, you will need additional contraception for the first two days. It is safe to use while breastfeeding.
* **After an abortion or miscarriage:** If you start within five days of the procedure, you are protected immediately. If you start later than day five, you will need additional contraception for the first two days.

Always speak to your midwife, GP, or nurse at a sexual health clinic to confirm the right method for you.

## How to Take the Progestogen-Only Pill

* Choose a time of day that suits you best and take it at the same time every day.
* Start the next packet immediately after finishing the previous one.
* Getting into a routine of taking the pill at the same time each day is crucial for effectiveness.

There are two types of progestogen-only pill:

* **The three-hour progestogen-only pill:** Contains levonorgestrel or norethisterone. Must be taken within three hours of the same time each day. Examples include Femulen, Micronor, Norgeston, and Noriday.
* **The 12-hour progestogen-only pill:** Contains desogestrel (e.g., Cerazette). Must be taken within 12 hours of the same time each day.

Refer to the advice provided on what to do if you forget your pill.

## Factors Impacting Effectiveness

Your contraception may be less effective if:

* You take it outside the designated 3 or 12-hour window.
* You vomit within two hours of taking it.
* You have very severe diarrhoea lasting more than 24 hours.
* You are taking certain medicines, including those for epilepsy, HIV, TB, and the complementary medicine St John's Wort. Always inform your doctor or pharmacist if you are taking any other medication.
* You are taking certain antibiotics. (Common antibiotics generally do not reduce effectiveness, but always check with a healthcare professional).

If any of these factors apply, talk to a pharmacist, doctor, or nurse and use additional contraception, such as condoms, until you are sure your contraception is protecting you.

## Coming Off the Pill

* **How quickly it leaves your system:** Hormones generally leave your body within a couple of days.
* **How quickly you can get pregnant:** This depends on when ovulation restarts, which can vary from days to months. Fertility levels usually return rapidly.
* **What happens to your periods:** If you have concerns about irregular periods or a long time for periods to return, speak to a nurse or doctor.
* **Physical changes:** Most side effects are temporary and your hormone levels will self-regulate over a few weeks or months. Remember to use another method of contraception if you do not wish to become pregnant after stopping the pill. Condoms also protect against STIs.

## If You Don't Like the Pill

* Side effects are often temporary. It's recommended to trial a method for at least three months.
* There are different types of progestogen-only pills, so another type might suit you better. Discuss your options with a medical professional.

## Progestogen-Only Pill vs. Combined Pill

* **Combined Pill:** Contains oestrogen and progestogen. Usually taken for three weeks followed by a one-week break, resulting in a withdrawal bleed.
* **Progestogen-Only Pill:** Contains only progestogen. Taken every day, and periods may stop or become irregular.

The combined pill is not suitable for everyone, and the progestogen-only pill is a good alternative if you cannot take the combined pill. Discuss your individual circumstances with your GP.

## Impact on Libido

Contraceptive methods can affect libido differently for individuals, potentially increasing or decreasing it. If you are unhappy with the effect, you can stop and try another method. For some, knowing they are protected from pregnancy can reduce anxiety and increase their libido.
