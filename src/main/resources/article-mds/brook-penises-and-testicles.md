# Penises and Testicles

The penis and testicles are essential parts of the male reproductive system, each serving distinct biological functions. The penis has two main functions: urination (carrying urine out of the body from the bladder) and ejaculation (delivering semen out of the body). The testicles also have two primary roles: they are where sperm is produced and stored, and where the hormone testosterone is produced.

## The External Parts

The external anatomy of the penis and surrounding area includes:

* **The Shaft:** The main body of the penis.
* **The Glans:** Also known as the head of the penis.
* **The Corona:** The ridge that separates the shaft from the glans.
* **The Foreskin:** A hood of loose skin that surrounds the glans (present at birth).
* **The Frenulum (or Frenum):** A sensitive piece of skin on the underside that connects the foreskin to the base of the glans.
* **The Scrotum:** The sac of skin hanging at the base of the penis that holds the testicles. It regulates temperature via the cremaster muscle, which pulls the testicles closer to the body when cold and allows them to hang further away when warm.
* **The Anus:** The opening to the rectum located behind the penis and testicles. It is highly sensitive and can be an area of sexual pleasure.

## The Internal Parts

Several internal organs and tubes facilitate the reproductive process:

* **Urethra:** The tube running through the penis that carries both urine and semen.
* **Testicles:** Located inside the scrotum, these produce sperm and testosterone. It is normal for them to be different sizes or hang at different heights.
* **Epididymis:** A tube where sperm matures; it connects the testicles to the vas deferens.
* **Vas Deferens:** Long, narrow tubes that carry sperm from the epididymis to the seminal vesicles during ejaculation.
* **Seminal Vesicles:** Two small organs below the bladder that produce the fluid that makes up semen.
* **Prostate Gland:** A walnut-sized gland that produces fluid to help sperm move. It is sensitive to pressure and can be a source of pleasure.
* **Cowper’s Glands (Bulbourethral Glands):** These produce pre-ejaculate (precum) to lubricate the urethra and reduce friction before ejaculation.

## Erections

An erection occurs when the spongy tissue of the penis fills with blood and becomes firm. This can happen due to sexual thoughts, physical stimulation of erogenous zones, or even spontaneously.

### Erogenous Zones
An erogenous zone is an area of the body that is particularly sensitive and can produce a sexual response when touched. Common erogenous zones include the penis, scrotum, anus, prostate, and nipples, as well as the neck, ears, back, hips, and thighs.

## Ejaculation

Ejaculation is the release of semen from the penis through the urethra, usually as a result of an orgasm. This can occur during masturbation, sex, or while asleep (nocturnal emissions or "wet dreams"). 

Because ejaculation can lead to pregnancy if semen enters the vagina, it is important to use contraception. Condoms are the only form of contraception available for people with penises that protect against both unwanted pregnancy and sexually transmitted infections (STIs).

## How to Wash Your Penis and Testicles

Maintaining hygiene is essential for sexual health:

* Wash the penis and testicles gently with warm water every day.
* If you have a foreskin, pull it back gently to wash underneath.
* Failure to clean under the foreskin can lead to a buildup of **smegma**, a yellowish-white substance. While smegma is a natural lubricant, its buildup can cause unpleasant odors and lead to **balanitis** (redness and swelling of the head of the penis).
* Use un-perfumed soap to avoid irritation.

## Pubic Hair and Grooming

Pubic hair is natural and provides protection from bacteria while reducing friction during sex. Whether you choose to keep, trim, or remove it is a personal preference. If you choose to groom, follow these safety tips:

* Ensure the area is clean and use warm water to soften the hair.
* Use a mirror to see clearly.
* Trim long hair with scissors or an electric razor (with a guard) before shaving.
* When using a manual razor, ensure it is new and sharp. Use a sensitive shaving cream and shave in the direction of hair growth.
* Clean and moisturize the area afterward with unscented lotion.
* If you cut yourself, stop immediately, clean the area, and apply pressure.

## Lumps and Bumps

Most lumps on the penis or scrotum are harmless, but you should be aware of:

* **Fordyce spots:** Enlarged oil glands.
* **Milia:** Tiny bumps caused by keratin buildup.
* **Molluscum contagiosum:** A viral skin infection.
* **STIs:** Some infections cause sores or bumps.

If you notice changes involving discomfort, pain, unusual discharge, or persistent sores, consult a GP, school nurse, or sexual health clinic.

## How to Examine Your Testicles

It is important to know what is "normal" for your body so you can spot changes early. It is best to check your testicles after a warm bath or shower when the scrotum is relaxed:

1. Support your scrotum in the palm of your hand.
2. Get to know the size and weight of each testicle.
3. Roll each testicle gently between your fingers and thumb.
4. Feel for lumps, swellings, or changes in firmness. The surface should be smooth.

### When to See a Doctor
Consult a healthcare professional if you notice:
* A lump on the testicle.
* Sharp pains or a dull ache in the testicles, lower abdomen, or groin.
* A sensation of heaviness in the scrotum.

While testicular cancer is rare, it is the most common cancer in younger men and people with penises. Early detection through regular self-examination is key.
