# About Enduring Love?

Brook’s section on relationships is based on findings from a research project called **Enduring Love?**, conducted by researchers at The Open University. Led by Professor Jacqui Gabb, the project explored how couples experience, understand, and sustain their long-term relationships.

## Defining "Enduring" Love

The project was titled "Enduring Love?" to reflect two aspects of long-term partnerships:
* **Duration:** The positive way love "endures" and lasts over time.
* **Perseverance:** The reality that love can sometimes be something that has to be "endured," particularly during difficult periods or "endurance tests."

The study does not suggest that long-term relationships are inherently better than being single or having successive shorter-term relationships. Instead, it focuses on the reality of how people maintain connections over time.

## Challenging the "Recipe for Success"

Mainstream media and relationship guidance often suggest there is a single "right" way to have a successful relationship. Common societal expectations include:
* Couples should live together.
* Parenthood and "starting a family" are the ultimate goals of a relationship.
* Monogamy is the only sign of true love.
* Constant, direct communication is the only way to resolve issues.

However, the findings from the *Enduring Love?* study show that there is no universal recipe for success. What works for one couple may not work for another.

## Diversity in Relationships

The research highlights that there are as many ways of "doing" relationships as there are people. Key variations identified in the study include:

* **Living Arrangements:** Some couples live together, while others live apart. This may be by choice or due to practicalities like shared housing or living with parents.
* **Children:** Some choose to have children, some have unplanned children, and others remain childless by choice or circumstance.
* **Gender and Identity:** Relationships include same-gender and different-gender partners.
* **Monogamy:** While many prefer monogamy, others find that open or non-monogamous arrangements work better for them.
* **Priorities:** Some people prioritize their partner above all else, while others view the partnership as one important connection among many, including friends and family.

## Finding What Works for You

The information gathered from the study reflects a "pick and mix" approach to relationship advice. Because the research included people from diverse ages, classes, and cultural backgrounds, some examples may feel familiar while others feel alien.

The ultimate conclusion of the research is that there are no right or wrong ways to conduct a relationship. As long as the arrangement works for the individuals involved, it is valid.

## About Professor Jacqui Gabb

Jacqui Gabb is a Professor of Sociology at The Open University with over 20 years of academic experience. Her research focuses on intimacy and family life, exploring the contemporary dynamics of personal relationships. 

She has authored several works on the subject, including:
* *Couple Relationships in the 21st Century* (2015)
* *The Secrets of Enduring Love* (2016)

The *Enduring Love?* project was funded by the Economic and Social Research Council (ESRC).
