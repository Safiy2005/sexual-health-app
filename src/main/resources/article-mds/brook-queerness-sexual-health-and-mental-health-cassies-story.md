# Queerness, sexual health and mental health: Cassie’s story

Cassie, 27, shares her experience regarding how society’s perceptions of her queerness significantly impacted both her mental health and sex life.

"I’m genuinely proud of who I am today. I’m an openly queer woman in a long-term relationship with another woman, and I wouldn’t change it for the world. If I had the choice to be straight, I wouldn’t take it. However, this doesn’t mean being queer has been without its challenges. Navigating the world as a queer person has impacted my mental health in ways I never expected. I knew coming out would be challenging at times, and I knew that queerphobia was always going to be something I’d have to deal with, but I didn’t realise how much being queer would affect my mental health. More accurately, I didn't foresee how society’s reaction to and unpreparedness for queerness would affect my mental health, and consequently, my sex life."

## The Impact of Societal Heteronormativity

"At first, I didn’t make the connection, but in hindsight, it’s obvious. Society’s perception of my queerness has a direct impact on how I view myself, my body, and what I choose to do with it. Growing up in a world that assumes everyone is straight made me feel 'othered'. Heteronormativity was pervasive—on TV, in books, in schools, and in casual conversations. It wasn’t always that people explicitly stated being queer was wrong (though some did); it was the simple fact that queerness wasn’t even considered."

"This widespread heteronormativity led to deep internalised queerphobia. I tried to ignore my feelings, but I couldn't shake the fact that I was attracted to girls, and it felt like the world was telling me this was wrong. I became ashamed of who I was and carried that shame into every aspect of my life: how I approached relationships, how I saw my body, and how I engaged in intimacy. I'd even go so far as to say it hindered the depth of some of my friendships. My friends were obsessed with boys, constantly talking about kissing and dating, and I would sit there questioning what was wrong with me. 'Why can’t I do that? How do they have the confidence to do this and I don’t?'"

"Looking back now, I realise that all that shame and confusion had led me to hate myself. The mental impact of that self-hatred is still with me. Even though I now love my queerness, I find myself being overly critical of everything I do. It’s been difficult to unlearn that feeling of not being 'normal' and always feeling like who you are is 'wrong'."

## Gaps in Sex Education and Misinformation

"The lack of queer inclusion in my sex-ed classes didn’t help with this feeling either. I remember sitting in my media classroom, learning about STIs and contraception, but absolutely nothing about what sex could look like for someone like me. I thought, 'Well, if school thinks it’s wrong, then it must be.' No one was teaching me how to navigate my own sexual health or my own relationships. The result? I was terrified of sex."

"Without any real guidance, I turned to the only source I could find: porn. Now at 27, I want to scream at younger Cassie and say, 'This isn’t going to help! PORN IS FOR ENTERTAINMENT, NOT EDUCATION!' but we live and we learn, I guess. I was less afraid of my sexuality at this point and felt like I had to prepare myself for sex with a woman, but watching porn only made it worse. I saw these perfect bodies and exaggerated performances, and it made me feel insecure about sex, my body, and my queerness. The poor body representations and the unrealistic portrayal of intimacy took a toll on my mental health and left me feeling unworthy and isolated."

## Finding Community and Healing

"In my late teens, I somehow managed to find other queer people who helped me get past the self-hatred and the internalised queerphobia. But it’s no wonder I felt the way I did; nothing was made for us! Education didn’t include us, films didn’t tell our stories, and even porn didn’t accurately represent us; in fact, they often fetishised us! Thankfully, I gradually learned to love my body and love sex, but it took time and a lot of patience."

## Ongoing Challenges and Resilience

"However, society’s view of queerness can still impact me and my sex life today. Regularly facing queerphobia and the constant fear of being hate-crimed is terrifying. Mentally battling harmful myths like ‘lesbian bed death’ is exhausting. The pressure to constantly ‘prove’ my sexuality is frustrating, and feeling like queer love is always under scrutiny or needs to be justified takes a real toll. All of this has impacted my mental health, leading to times when my libido is extremely high or almost non-existent. This can be hard, but I’m lucky to be surrounded by other queer people who understand me and who will support me whenever I need it."

"I wish I had found my people sooner, but I’m grateful I eventually did. I’m not sure where I’d be without them. With their support, I’ve been able to heal parts of myself I didn’t even realise were hurting. And without all that self-hatred, it turns out I’m actually quite a sexual person. I’m thankful I get to be that person despite everything."

## Conclusion

"I don’t have all the answers, and I probably never will. But one thing I’m certain of: my queerness was never ‘wrong’. It was society that was wrong."
