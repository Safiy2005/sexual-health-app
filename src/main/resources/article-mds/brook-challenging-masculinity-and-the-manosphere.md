# Challenging Masculinity and the Manosphere

Stereotypical masculinity can be harmful for boys and men. Understanding where these ideas come from is the first step in changing them. We receive ideas about gender stereotypes from many different sources, including:

* TV shows and films
* Video games
* Toys from childhood
* Role models
* The people we hang out with or grew up around

One of the primary ways ideas about masculinity are reinforced today is through social media and the "manosphere."

## Understanding the Manosphere

The manosphere refers to a range of websites, blogs, and online forums that promote stereotyped versions of masculinity, misogyny, and opposition to feminism. This environment can be particularly appealing if you are not feeling good about yourself, are spending a lot of time online, or are lacking a sense of community.

Harmful online communities often operate by:
* Expressing prejudice under the guise of building community or reaching a specific goal.
* Promising success if you follow specific rules or a charismatic leader.
* Providing oversimplified answers that blame specific groups of people (usually women) for men's problems.
* Fostering a false sense of superiority and an "Us vs. Them" mentality.

Over time, exposure to this content can distort your worldview and how you perceive women.

## Misogyny and Incel Culture

**Misogyny** is the hatred or dislike of women and girls. It involves sexism, aggression, viewing women as inferior, and reducing them to their bodies or appearance.

**Incel culture** (involuntary celibates) is an online community of men who believe they are unable to find romantic or sexual partners and blame women for this. This culture often involves misogynistic hate speech and can lead to the radicalisation of young people into extreme views.

It is important to remember:
* No one owes you sex.
* You do not have rights to anyone else’s body.
* Relationships should be equally fulfilling for all parties.
* Making a specific group responsible for your happiness is not a healthy way to live.

## Ways to Challenge Harmful Stereotypes

While gender stereotypes exist, we have the power to challenge and eventually dismantle them. Here are six ways to avoid being influenced by the manosphere:

### 1. Challenge your own ideas
Masculinity is unique to the individual. While there is nothing wrong with wanting to be strong or successful, these aren't the only options. Think about what it means to be a "good human" rather than just a "man." You are more than your gender; you are a unique individual with your own passions and interests.

### 2. Analyse what you see online
Not everything online is true. If content makes you feel better by putting others down, question why you like the idea. Is it because it’s true, or just because of how it makes you feel? Talk to someone you trust about content that makes you feel uneasy.

### 3. Curate your social media
You are in control of your consumption. If you see misogynistic content, report it or mark it as "not interested" to train the algorithm. Follow positive role models who don't necessarily fit stereotypical masculine molds and unfollow accounts that make you feel inadequate.

### 4. Be an active bystander
Responding to harmful behaviour can happen online or offline. If you see misogynistic content, you can:
* Report it anonymously via the platform's reporting function.
* Downvote or show disapproval.
* Show support for the person being targeted.
* *Note: If you need to step away for your own mental health, it is okay not to get involved.*

### 5. Prioritize offline activities
Spending too much time online can distort reality. For example, the "perfect" bodies seen on social media do not reflect the diversity of people in real life. Seek out communities, clubs, and hobbies in the physical world to find shared values and genuine connection.

### 6. Talk to people around you
Language matters. Instead of using phrases like "man up," try opening up to friends about your feelings. Supporting a friend or calling out inappropriate comments shows that you aren't afraid to be yourself. Your friends are likely feeling the same pressures to conform, and talking about it can help everyone feel less alone.
