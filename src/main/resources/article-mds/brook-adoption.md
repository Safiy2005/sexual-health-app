# Adoption: How the Process Works and Support with Making the Decision

Adoption is one of the choices available to a person who is pregnant and does not wish to keep the baby. It provides an opportunity for the child to be raised by another person or couple, who then become the child’s legal parents.

Once an adoption order is made, it is a final and legally binding decision. The adopters become the child’s legal parents, and the birth parents no longer have any legal rights regarding the child, though arrangements may be made for the birth parents to receive news of the child’s progress.

## Reasons for Considering Adoption

The decision to place a child for adoption is highly personal. Some people make the decision quickly, while others find it more difficult. There are many reasons why someone might choose adoption, including:

* Lack of a supportive family network to assist with childcare.
* The feeling that they cannot raise a child by themselves.
* Cultural or family beliefs regarding unmarried parenting.
* A personal opposition to abortion, while not wishing to become a parent.

Choosing adoption is a valid and responsible decision made in the best interests of the child and the birth parent.

## Talking it Through

It is helpful to discuss your feelings and options with someone you trust. This might be a friend or family member, or a neutral professional who can offer support without judgment.

### Professional Advice and Support
If you decide that adoption is the right path, it is important to speak with an expert as soon as possible. Advice can be obtained from:

* Social workers from your local authority’s social services department.
* Voluntary adoption agencies.
* Hospital social workers attached to maternity clinics.

A social worker will help guide you through the decision, balancing your best interests with those of the child. You will be asked for personal and medical information about yourself and your family to share with the adoptive parents for the child’s benefit later in life.

## The Legal Process

Adoptions are arranged by agencies but made legal through the courts.

### Your Decision
Even if you are under 18, the final decision regarding adoption is yours, not your parents'. A social worker will ensure that the decision is yours alone.

### The Role of the Father
* **If the father is married to the mother or named on the birth certificate:** His legal agreement to the adoption is required. If there is a disagreement, a court may need to decide what is in the best interests of the child.
* **If the father is not married to the mother and not on the birth certificate:** Formal permission is not legally required, though the agency will likely try to contact him for medical history. You cannot be forced to reveal the father’s identity.

### The Adoptive Family
All prospective adopters undergo rigorous interviews and background checks to ensure they can provide a safe and happy home. You can discuss the type of family you want for your child with your social worker. You may also be able to request specific levels of future contact, such as:

* Regular letters and visits.
* Occasional updates.
* No contact at all.

## After the Birth

Preparations can begin during pregnancy, but nothing is finalized until after the birth. You remain free to change your mind throughout the pregnancy.

When you leave the hospital, the baby may go to a temporary foster carer or directly to the adoptive parents, depending on the agreed plan.

### Finalizing the Order
1. **Application:** Once the baby has settled with the adoptive parents, they apply to the court.
2. **Timing:** A baby must live with the adopters for at least 10 weeks before they can apply for an adoption order.
3. **Formal Consent:** You cannot give formal legal consent to the adoption until the baby is at least six weeks old. A court representative will visit you to ensure you fully understand the implications before you sign the documents.

## Changing Your Mind

The ability to change your mind depends on where you are in the process:

* **Before placement:** You can ask for the immediate return of your baby.
* **After placement but before court application:** The baby is normally returned to you unless there are significant concerns regarding the child's welfare.
* **After application to the court:** You can still change your mind, but the court must be convinced that returning the child is in their best interests. Legal advice should be sought immediately.
* **After an adoption order is made:** The decision is final. The legal relationship is severed, and the child cannot be returned.

## Emotional Wellbeing

Placing a child for adoption can trigger a wide range of emotions, including relief, sadness, or guilt. It is important to remember the reasons why you made your decision.

Physical and hormonal changes after birth can also affect your mood. If you experience persistent low mood or think you may have postnatal depression, contact a midwife, doctor, or counsellor. Support is available and you deserve care regardless of your decision to place the child for adoption.
