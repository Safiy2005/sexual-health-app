# Abortion: Advice for Partners

A pregnancy has consequences for everyone involved, and people may have different ideas about what they would like to happen. Whether or not to continue with a pregnancy is always the choice of the person who is pregnant. This is because they have bodily autonomy, which is the right for a person to decide what happens to their body without influence or coercion from others.

Whenever possible, it is important that both people are involved in the choices and decisions about continuing or ending a pregnancy.

## What do we mean by partner?

On this page, we use the word ‘partner’ to mean either of the people involved in a pregnancy; this could range from a couple who are in a long-term relationship to two people who have had sex once.

If you don’t want to have a baby, the best thing is to avoid an unplanned pregnancy by using contraception. Brook also recommends using condoms, especially in a casual relationship or hook-up, as they help to protect against STIs.

## Rights and responsibilities

* **Financial Responsibility:** If a sexual partner becomes pregnant and decides to have the baby, you may be held financially responsible for the upbringing of the child. This may apply even if you are no longer in a relationship or choose not to have an active part in the child’s life.
* **Legal Rights to Abortion:** If your partner decides to have an abortion, they may do so without your agreement. Under UK law, a pregnant person’s partner has no legal right to make them have an abortion or to prevent them from having one.
* **Medical Approval:** If someone decides to have an abortion, this decision must be approved by two doctors who agree with the pregnant person that it is in their best interests.

## Involvement in decisions

Partners’ experiences vary widely. Some may be very supportive and reach a mutual agreement. Some may have no involvement because their partner chooses not to inform them. In some cases, partners may become sole decision-makers through coercion or abuse.

### Healthy involvement
People in happy, committed relationships may feel more able to talk about their feelings and anxieties. Common discussion points include:
* Current circumstances and how a child could impact their lives.
* Hopes for the future and how children fit into that picture.
* Acknowledging that while the pregnant person has the final decision, both sides have a right to express thoughts and feelings.

### Lack of involvement
A pregnant person may choose not to inform their partner for several reasons:
* The pregnancy resulted from casual sex.
* The person believes it is their body and therefore solely their choice.
* Fear of being blamed or punished for a failure in contraception.
* The pregnancy was a result of rape.

### Unhealthy involvement
Sometimes family members or partners may try to force or prevent an abortion due to personal beliefs or within the context of an abusive relationship. Abusive partners may use coercion, such as threatening to end the relationship or using intimidation. This is an unsafe situation. Every person deserves to be in a relationship where they feel safe making decisions that are right for them.

## If you are worried about your partner’s reaction
If you feel vulnerable to pressure or are undecided, it is a good idea to talk to a professional to clarify your thoughts before talking to your partner. You can talk to a professional at Brook, another sexual health service, or a specialist organization.

## Supporting your partner through the decision
Most people accept that the final decision rests with the pregnant person but need an opportunity to express their feelings. To support your partner:

* **Listen:** Give them space to express thoughts and process feelings.
* **Evaluate:** Help them think through the pros and cons of the pregnancy.
* **Communicate:** Let them know what you think, but understand they may not feel the same way.
* **Seek Professional Help:** Help them get in touch with an appropriate agency or accompany them to a GP or sexual health clinic. Remember that professionals will likely want to speak to the pregnant person alone for part of the appointment.
* **Inform:** Find relevant information about options and be realistic about what support you can provide.
* **Self-Care:** Look after yourself by talking to your own support network.

## How you might feel after an abortion
Post-abortion feelings often relate to original feelings about the decision:
* **Sadness or Resentment:** If a partner strongly wanted to continue the pregnancy, they may feel disappointed or resentful.
* **Relief:** Some may feel relieved that a decision has been made and the period of uncertainty is over.
* **Reflection:** Even when both agree, couples may still think about "what might have been."
* **Breathing Space:** If the decision was made due to financial or educational concerns, it may allow the couple space to plan their future together.
