# Period Products

There are many different options for collecting period blood, each with its own pros and cons. Understanding these various methods can help you find the right solution for managing your period. It's common to try a few different products and brands to discover what works best for your individual needs, and many people use a combination of methods depending on their cycle and daily activities.

Everyone with a period should have access to period products and the choice of which product to use. Periods can be expensive, but free period products are available for those who need them.

## Tampons

Tampons are small tubes of compressed cotton that are inserted into the vagina. They have a string attached for easy removal. Tampons can be used with or without an applicator (a plastic or cardboard tube that aids insertion). Used tampons should be disposed of in a bin, never flushed, as they can cause blockages and pollution.

**Pros:**

* Comfortable once inserted; often unfelt.
* Easy and quick to use with practice.
* Invisible and discreet.
* Can be worn while swimming.
* Compatible with any type of underwear.
* Disposable, no cleaning required.
* Widely accessible.
* Relatively inexpensive in the short term.
* Available in various absorbency levels.

**Cons:**

* Can be difficult or uncomfortable to insert and remove.
* No visual cues to indicate when changing is needed, increasing the risk of leaks.
* Not the most environmentally friendly option due to being disposable.
* Cost can add up over time.
* Can leave behind residue or fibres, potentially causing irritation or infection.
* Risk of Toxic Shock Syndrome (TSS) if left in too long; change every 4-8 hours and use the lowest absorbency suitable.

Tampons can be left in for up to eight hours and come in various sizes for different flow levels.

## Pads (Sanitary Towels)

Pads are absorbent liners placed inside underwear to collect menstrual blood. They are available in various absorbencies, suitable for light to heavy flows. Like tampons, unscented versions are recommended to avoid irritation. Pads should be disposed of in a bin, not flushed.

**Pros:**

* Easy to use as they don't require insertion into the vagina.
* Convenient for the end of a period when the vagina may be drier.
* Easy to store and carry.
* Disposable, no cleaning required.
* Widely accessible.
* Relatively inexpensive in the short term.
* Reusable pads are available, offering an eco-friendly and cost-effective long-term solution.

**Cons:**

* Can feel bulky or messy, especially when saturated.
* Prone to leaking if they shift or come unstuck.
* Cannot be worn for swimming.
* Conventional pads are not environmentally friendly as they are disposable.
* Cost can accumulate over time.

**How to use pads:**

Pads are generally simpler to use than tampons. They are stuck to the inside of underwear, often with adhesive wings that wrap around the gusset for a secure fit. Regular changes are recommended, especially for heavy flows.

## Menstrual Cups

Menstrual cups are flexible containers made of rubber or silicone that are inserted into the vagina to collect blood. They create a seal to stay in place. When full, the base is pinched to break the seal, the collected blood is emptied into the toilet, and the cup is washed and reinserted. While they may take practice to use, they are a comfortable, easy, and waste-free option. Cups are reusable and can last up to 5 years, making them an environmentally friendly choice.

**Pros:**

* Comfortable once inserted.
* Easy and quick to use with practice.
* High capacity (around 25ml), requiring less frequent changes than tampons or pads.
* Reduced risk of leaks.
* Reusable, leading to lower environmental impact and long-term cost savings.
* Invisible once inserted.
* No external items needed.
* Do not leave residue or fibres.
* Can be worn while swimming.
* Compatible with any type of underwear.
* Increasingly accessible.

**Cons:**

* Can be difficult to master initially, taking several cycles to get used to.
* No visual cues for fullness, potentially leading to leaks if cycle is not well understood.
* Higher upfront cost compared to disposable products.
* Can be messy, and cleaning in public restrooms can be challenging.
* Risk of TSS if left in for too long; change at least every 12 hours.
* May not be suitable for individuals using certain types of IUD contraception.

**How to use a menstrual cup:**

It's recommended to practice using a menstrual cup at home first. The stem may need trimming for a comfortable fit.

1. **Clean your cup and hands:** Sterilize by boiling between periods. During your period, wash with warm water and soap. Ensure the small holes around the rim are clear to maintain the seal.
2. **Fold the cup:** Various folding techniques exist.
3. **Insert the cup:** Get into a comfortable position (e.g., sitting on the toilet or squatting) and insert the folded cup into the vagina.
4. **Open the cup:** Once inserted, allow the cup to pop open. It should sit as low as comfortably possible.
5. **Check the seal:** Gently run a finger around the base of the cup to ensure it has opened and created a seal. Adjust by gently pushing the base or pulling the stem while engaging pelvic muscles.
6. **Wear for up to 12 hours:** Change sooner if it fills up.

**Pain or discomfort:** If you experience pain or pressure, the cup may be inserted too low or the stem may be too long. Remove and reinsert, or trim the stem gradually.

**To remove:**

1. **Wash your hands.**
2. **Bear down:** Use abdominal and vaginal muscles to lower the cup.
3. **Pinch the base to break the seal:** If the base is out of reach, pull the stem gently while bearing down to bring it closer.
4. **Pull out the cup:** Remove by the stem or base.
5. **Empty into the toilet.**
6. **Clean and reinsert:** Wash with warm, soapy water. If unable to wash, use water from a bottle or tissue.

## Period Pants

Period pants are underwear or swimwear with a built-in absorbent layer that can be washed and reused.

**Pros:**

* Comfortable and do not require insertion.
* Designed to feel dry during wear.
* Easy to use.
* Higher absorbency than tampons and pads, reducing the need for frequent changes and leakage.
* Reusable, environmentally friendly, and cost-effective long-term.
* No additional products needed.
* Period swimwear can be worn while swimming.
* Available in various styles.
* Easy to purchase online.

**Cons:**

* Require cleaning and drying, meaning multiple pairs are needed.
* Higher maintenance.
* Can be inconvenient to change when out and about, requiring spares and a way to store used pants.
* Higher upfront cost compared to disposable products.

**Accessing Free Period Products:**

Everyone should have the option to choose and use period products that best suit them. Initiatives are in place to support those struggling with the cost of period products:

* **Schools and Education:** In England, students in schools and 16-19 education organisations can access free period products. Inquire with a school nurse, teacher, or trusted staff member if you are unaware of how to access them.
* **Scotland:** Local councils provide free period products in many locations. The PickupMyPeriodApp or local council websites can provide information on where to find them.
* **Universities:** Many Students' Unions offer free period products. Check with your local Students' Union.
* **Morrisons "Package for Sandy":** Ask for a 'package for Sandy' or 'period product pack' at any Morrisons customer service desk for a discreet package.
* **Food Banks:** Many food banks offer free period products, though a voucher may be required. Search The Trussell Trust's website for your nearest location.
* **Local Councils (England):** While not a legal requirement, some local councils may provide free products. Check your local council's website.
* **Charities:** Charities dedicated to tackling period poverty in the UK may offer assistance.
