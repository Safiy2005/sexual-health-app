# Genital Warts

Genital warts are small growths or bumps that appear on or around the genital or anal area. They are caused by the human papillomavirus (HPV). While there are over 100 different types of HPV, about 30 types affect the genital skin, and 90% of genital warts are caused by just two types (types 6 and 11).

It is important to note that genital warts are not caused by the same virus that causes common warts on the hands or verrucas on the feet.

## Symptoms

Most people with an HPV infection do not have visible symptoms. It can take months or even years after infection before warts appear. When they do appear, they may be single growths or clusters with a cauliflower-like appearance. While usually painless, they can be itchy, inflamed, or prone to slight bleeding.

Common locations for genital warts include:
* Around the opening of the vagina (vulva)
* Around the cervix (the neck of the womb)
* Inside the vagina
* Around or inside the anus
* On the upper thighs
* Anywhere on the penis
* On the scrotum
* Inside the urethra (the tube where urine comes out)

## How Genital Warts are Passed On

The most common way genital warts are transmitted is through skin-to-skin contact with someone who has the HPV virus. They can be passed on even if warts are not currently visible.

Transmission most commonly occurs through:
* Vaginal sex
* Anal sex
* Non-penetrative genital-to-genital contact
* Sharing sex toys
* Oral sex (though this is rare)

Genital warts are not passed on through kissing, hugging, sharing towels, clothing, cutlery, or toilet seats.

## Prevention

While you cannot fully protect yourself against genital warts, you can significantly reduce your risk:
* **Condoms:** These help protect you, but because the virus can live on the surrounding skin not covered by a condom, they do not offer 100% protection.
* **HPV Vaccine:** The vaccine protects against the types of HPV that cause most cases of genital warts and cervical cancer.

## The HPV Vaccine

A vaccination programme is offered to young people aged 12 to 13. The current vaccine offered by the NHS, Gardasil 9, protects against nine types of HPV (6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, and 58). This covers the strains most commonly responsible for both genital warts (6 and 11) and cervical cancer (16 and 18). 

Full protection requires two doses, usually administered at least six months apart.

## Diagnosis and Treatment

If you suspect you have genital warts, you should visit a Brook service, a sexual health (GUM) clinic, or your GP.

### Diagnosis
There is no specific swab or blood test for genital warts. A doctor or nurse diagnoses them through a physical examination of the area. In some cases, a more detailed internal examination of the vagina or anus may be required.

### Treatment
Treatment is only offered if visible warts are present. Options include:
* **Creams or lotions:** Applied directly to the warts (usually better for softer warts).
* **Physical removal:** Destroying the tissue by freezing (cryotherapy), heating, or cutting them away (better for hard or rough warts).

It may take several months for treatment to be successful. Do not use over-the-counter treatments intended for hand warts or verrucas, as these are not suitable for genital skin.

### Important Considerations
* **Pregnancy:** Tell your healthcare provider if you are pregnant or could be, as this affects which treatments are safe to use.
* **Sexual Activity:** It is recommended to avoid sex until the warts have fully healed to prevent transmission and aid recovery.
* **Untreated Warts:** If left untreated, warts may eventually go away on their own, but they may also grow larger or multiply. They are rarely harmful to overall health but can cause discomfort.
