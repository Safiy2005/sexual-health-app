# The Hormonal Coil: What it is and How it Works

The hormonal coil, also known as the Mirena coil (the most popular brand), is a small, T-shaped plastic device. It contains the hormone progestogen to control fertility and is inserted into the uterus (womb) through the vagina by a specially trained doctor or nurse.

## Key Facts

* **Hormones:** Contains progestogen (low dose).
* **Mood/Emotions:** Can help with feeling low or anxious for some.
* **STIs:** Provides no protection against sexually transmitted infections.
* **Visibility:** It is not visible to others.
* **Periods:** Excellent at making heavy periods lighter and less painful.
* **Lasts for:** Up to 8 years, depending on the brand.
* **Acne/Skin:** Can cause acne in some users.
* **Preventing Pregnancy:** More than 99% effective.
* **Starting this method:** Can only be started if there is no chance of pregnancy. Requires an appointment with a specialist nurse or doctor for fitting. You may need additional contraception for seven days depending on when in your cycle it is fitted.

## How the Hormonal Coil Works

The hormonal coil works in three ways to prevent an egg from being fertilised by sperm:

* **Thickens cervical mucus:** The mucus around the cervix becomes thicker, making it harder for sperm to pass through and reach an egg.
* **Thins uterine lining:** It makes the lining of the womb thinner, preventing a fertilised egg from implanting.
* **May stop ovulation:** It can also stop ovulation (the release of an egg from the ovaries), although most people who use it continue to ovulate.

The hormonal coil is a method of long-acting reversible contraception (LARC). These methods do not rely on daily remembrance but require a trained doctor or nurse for administration or fitting.

## Pros of the Hormonal Coil

* Over 99% effective at preventing pregnancy.
* Not visible and does not interrupt sex.
* Prevents pregnancy for up to three, four, or eight years, depending on the brand.
* Excellent at reducing heavy, painful periods.
* Fertility returns to normal quickly after removal.
* Can be used by individuals who cannot use contraception containing oestrogen.
* Contains less progestogen than some other hormonal contraception methods, leading to fewer hormonal side effects.
* Its effectiveness is not affected by vomiting, diarrhoea, or other medicines, unlike some other contraception methods.
* Considered an eco-friendly method due to its long duration of use.

## Cons of the Hormonal Coil

* Offers no protection against sexually transmitted infections (STIs).
* May cause temporary side effects such as skin problems, headaches, irregular bleeding, or breast tenderness.
* Can cause small, fluid-filled cysts on the ovaries. These usually disappear without treatment and often without symptoms.
* Carries small risks of infection after insertion, the coil being pushed out, or displacement.
* There is a very small risk of tearing the uterus during fitting, and a small risk of ectopic pregnancy if you do become pregnant while using the hormonal coil.
* Fitting can be painful for some people.

## What to Expect Regarding Periods

It is common for periods to become lighter, less painful, shorter, or stop altogether while using the hormonal coil. However, you may experience some temporary irregular bleeding during the first few months. If you have periods while on the hormonal coil, your provider may recommend not using menstrual cups or tampons for the first few weeks after fitting.

## Getting the Hormonal Coil

The coil must be fitted by a doctor or nurse who has been specially trained. It is available free from a range of services, including contraceptive clinics, your GP, and organisations like Brook.

Sexual health services such as Brook are free and confidential, including for people under the age of 16. Health professionals work to strict guidelines and will not tell anyone else about your visit unless they believe you are at serious risk of immediate harm.

## The Fitting Process

### Step 1: Appointment

You will need to book an appointment at a contraceptive clinic or with your doctor. This appointment typically includes:

* **Medical history review:** Questions about your medical and family history to determine the most suitable method for you.
* **Internal examination:** A doctor or nurse will perform an internal examination to check the position and size of your uterus before fitting the coil. They will also check for any signs of infection, and you may be asked to do an STI test or be given antibiotics.
* **Pregnancy confirmation:** There must be no chance that you are pregnant, so you may need to do a pregnancy test before the coil can be fitted.

Some services may be able to fit the coil at the same appointment, while others may require a second appointment.

### Step 2: Fitting the Coil

The coil fitting usually takes no longer than 5 minutes:

* You will be asked to remove the lower half of your clothing and lie on the bed with your legs open and knees bent.
* The doctor or nurse will use a speculum to gently widen your vagina. This might feel uncomfortable but should not be painful.
* The coil will then be inserted into the uterus through the cervix. You may feel some discomfort, which might last a minute or two after insertion, or longer for some individuals.
* The doctor or nurse will trim the strings and remove the speculum.

The coil fitting can be uncomfortable for some people. The doctor or nurse may discuss painkiller tablets and/or using local anaesthetic to make the fitting more comfortable.

Currently, there are four main brands of hormonal coil: Mirena, Kyleena, Levosert, and Jaydess. Some brands work for eight, four, or three years.

### Step 3: After the Coil is Fitted

* You may experience period-type pain and possibly some light bleeding for a few days afterwards.
* The coil has two threads that hang through the opening of your uterus (cervix). You should check these threads a few times during the first month and then at least once a month to ensure the coil is in place.
* You will only need to go back for a replacement when the coil needs to be changed (every 3 to 8 years, depending on the type).

## When to Talk to a Doctor

Contact a doctor if you experience any of the following:

* You cannot feel the threads of the coil.
* You can feel the coil itself.
* You experience severe or prolonged abdominal pain, especially if you also feel unwell, hot, and clammy.
* You experience heavy vaginal bleeding with or without clots.

## When Can the Hormonal Coil be Fitted?

The hormonal coil can be fitted at any time in your menstrual cycle if it is certain that you are not pregnant.

* **Immediate protection:** If fitted within the first seven days of your menstrual cycle, you will be immediately protected against pregnancy.
* **Delayed protection:** If fitted at any other time, you will need to use an additional method of contraception (such as condoms) for the first seven days.
* **Short cycles:** If you have a short menstrual cycle (where your period normally comes every 23 days or less), you will need to start the coil in the first six days of your cycle because you might ovulate earlier.

## Getting the Hormonal Coil After Pregnancy

You can get a hormonal coil fitted after a birth, abortion, or miscarriage, but the timing may vary.

### After giving birth

You can become pregnant as early as three weeks after giving birth.

* The hormonal coil can sometimes be fitted 48 hours after giving birth.
* However, in most cases, you will have to wait four weeks. This means you will need to use alternative contraception, such as condoms, from week three (21 days) after the birth until the coil is fitted.
* The hormonal coil is safe to use while breastfeeding.

### After an abortion or miscarriage

You can become pregnant from two weeks after an abortion or miscarriage.

* The hormonal coil can be inserted straight away after an abortion or miscarriage, and you will be immediately protected from pregnancy.

Speak to your midwife, GP, or a nurse at a sexual health clinic to find out if this method is right for you.

## Removal of the Hormonal Coil

The coil must be removed by a trained doctor or nurse. The procedure should be less painful and quicker than when the coil was inserted.

If you are not planning to have another coil inserted and do not want to become pregnant, you will need to use additional contraception, such as condoms, for the seven days before the coil is taken out. Your fertility should return to normal as soon as the coil is removed.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does having the coil fitted hurt?

Some people may find the fitting painful, but you can request pain relief, including local anaesthetic, to help. It's a good idea to discuss this with the nurse or doctor beforehand. For most people, pain only lasts a couple of minutes, offering long-term contraception benefits.

### Can I only get a coil fitted during my period?

No, you can get a coil fitted at any time during your cycle. There is no medical evidence to suggest that having it fitted during your period offers any additional benefits.

### Can you get the hormonal coil while you are pregnant?

No, the hormonal coil is used to prevent pregnancy and should not be used if there is any chance of pregnancy, as this may cause an ectopic pregnancy. At your contraception appointment, you may be asked to do a pregnancy test to confirm you're not pregnant. If you've recently had unprotected sex, you may need to wait until a pregnancy test can accurately determine that pregnancy has not occurred before the coil can be fitted.
