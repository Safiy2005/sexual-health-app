# Menorrhagia: Heavy Periods

## Symptoms of Heavy Periods

It can be hard to know if you have a heavy period. Here are some common symptoms:

* **Your period regularly lasts more than 7 days.**
 * Period bleeding usually lasts for 2-7 days, with the first couple of days being the heaviest and gradually getting lighter.
 * People with menorrhagia (heavy periods) will often bleed for longer than 7 days or experience a few days of the heaviest flow.
* **You soak through pads/tampons more than every 2 hours.**
 * On average, about one to five tablespoons of period blood leaves the body, but for people with heavy periods, it can be much more.
 * Heavy periods are generally classed as a period of more than 80ml of blood. However, this is hard to measure and can feel like much more.
 * It's easier to recognize if you soak through pads/tampons quickly (every 1-2 hours) or if you use pads and tampons at the same time to avoid leaks.
* **You have blood clots bigger than 2.5cm.**
 * Blood clots are clumps of blood that can leave the vagina during a period.
 * If they are over 2.5cm or experienced very often, it may be a sign of heavy periods and you should see a doctor.
* **Your periods are stopping you from going about normal life.**
 * Heavy periods can add to the burden of managing your period, potentially making you feel down.
 * This can manifest as:
 * Worrying about leaking onto your clothes when out.
 * Planning your day around toilet access for changing period products.
 * Lower self-esteem and confidence.
 * Avoiding activities like school or work due to fear of leaks.
* **You flood or leak onto your underwear or clothes regularly.**
 * Flooding is a gush of period blood that may be hard for period products to absorb quickly, leading to leaks.
 * Heavy periods can cause regular leaks onto clothes, underwear, or bedding.

## Other Symptoms (Sleep, Pain, Dizziness)

* **Feeling dizzy, breathless, and getting tired easily:** This may be a sign of anaemia (low iron) due to excessive blood loss. If you experience these symptoms, it's important to see a doctor for a blood test and potential iron supplements.
* **Painful periods:** Heavy periods are often painful. If pain is severe and not relieved by painkillers, impacting your daily life, seek medical help.
* **Affecting sleep:** Heavy periods can disrupt sleep due to frequent changes of pads, bedding, or pain throughout the night.

**Important:** You may experience all or only a few of these symptoms. If your periods are worrying you or stopping you from doing normal activities, it's a good idea to see a doctor. They can provide peace of mind and discuss treatment options.

## Potential Causes of Heavy Periods

Heavy periods are common in certain life stages and can also be linked to specific medical conditions:

* **Teenagers:** Often due to "anovulation" (when ovaries don't release an egg), which is normal in the first few years after periods start. This can lead to irregular, heavier, and longer periods.
* **Approaching Menopause:** Anovulation is also more common as you approach menopause, potentially causing irregular and heavy periods. See a doctor if your periods become significantly heavier and you suspect perimenopause.
* **Bleeding Disorders:** Other symptoms include bruising easily and prolonged bleeding from cuts and wounds.
* **PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome):** Infrequent periods with PCOS can be heavy due to a thickened uterine lining caused by hormone imbalances. It's important to have at least four periods a year; consult your doctor if you don't.
* **Endometriosis:** A condition where uterine lining-like cells grow elsewhere in the body, which can be associated with heavy periods.
* **Adenomyosis:** The uterine lining grows into the uterine muscle, causing very painful and heavy periods.
* **Fibroids:** Non-cancerous growths in the uterine muscle, more common in ages 30-50. An ultrasound scan can confirm them.
* **Polyps:** Small, non-cancerous growths in the uterus that can cause heavy periods. They may be visible on an ultrasound.
* **Pelvic Infection:** Sexually transmitted infections (STIs) can sometimes make periods heavier and may be a sign of pelvic inflammatory disease. Consult your doctor if you are concerned.
* **Certain Medications:** Blood thinners can make periods heavier.

## Managing Heavy Periods

### Track Your Periods

Tracking your periods helps you understand them better and provides valuable information for healthcare appointments. You can use period tracking apps or Brook's period tracker, designed with healthcare experts.

### Use Heavy Flow Period Products

If fear of leaking affects your daily life, consider these options:

* **Doubling up on period products:** Use a tampon/menstrual cup with a pad/period pants for extra protection.
* **Use heavy flow period products:** Products are designed for different flow levels. Night-time pads are larger and longer.
* **Period pants and menstrual cups:** These can hold more blood than typical pads or tampons and are reusable, making them cost-effective long-term.

**Get free period products:** Free products are available at schools, colleges, universities, sexual health clinics, and food banks. You can also ask for "Package for Sandy" at Morrisons customer service desks.

* 

### Nutrition

* **Include iron-rich foods:** To help maintain iron levels. Your GP may recommend iron supplements if needed.
* **Maintain a healthy weight:** This can help regulate your periods.
* **Avoid trigger foods:** Salty foods can worsen bloating.

### Get Support from Those Around You

* **Talk about it:** Sharing your experiences can reduce stigma and help you find others who experience heavy periods.
* **Inform others:** Let friends, family, or colleagues know about how your periods affect you to foster understanding and support.
* **Seek accommodations at school/work:** Talk to teachers, supervisors, or managers about making your environment easier during heavy period days, such as:
 * Working from home.
 * Using a chair if working in customer service.
 * Moving your desk closer to toilets.
 * Being allowed to sit out of P.E.
* 
* **Mental health support:** If your mental health is affected, various organizations can help.

## When to See a Doctor

If you think you have heavy periods and they are affecting your life, don't hesitate to seek medical advice. Your GP can help identify any underlying causes and suggest treatments.

When you see your GP, they will ask about your periods and general health. Be prepared to share a tracker for at least the last three periods. A blood test may be done to check for anaemia. A vaginal examination might be suggested, though it's less likely for teenagers. Your GP will discuss options and may refer you to a specialist.

### Treatment Options

If your periods started recently and are still irregular, they may become lighter over time.

* **Medication:**
 * **NSAIDs (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs):** Medicines like Ibuprofen, naproxen, and mefenamic acid can reduce bleeding by 20-60% and also help with period pain. Start taking them 24-48 hours before your period for optimal pain relief. Always follow instructions on the label.
 * **Tranexamic acid tablets:** These help blood clot and can reduce blood loss by 40-60%. They are prescribed by a doctor and taken during the heaviest days of your period for up to 4 days.
* **Non-contraceptive Hormones:**
 * **Medroxyprogesterone acetate and Norethisterone:** These can be taken short-term to stop prolonged bleeding or long-term to lighten periods by preventing the uterine lining from becoming too thick. They are usually taken for 21 days with a 7-day break. These do not prevent pregnancy, so contraception is still needed.
* **Hormonal Contraception:**
 * These methods can help manage periods, control timing, reduce pain, and make periods lighter. Improvements may take up to three months.
 * **Progestogen-only methods:**
 * **Progestogen-only pill (mini-pill):** Taken daily, can reduce bleeding by up to 60%.
 * **Hormonal coil (IUS):** Very effective at making periods lighter, reducing bleeding by up to 95%, with some people experiencing no periods at all.
 * **Injection:** Given every 8-13 weeks.
 * **Implant:** Inserted into the upper arm, lasts up to three years.
 * **Combined contraception methods:** Contain oestrogen and progestogen and can reduce bleeding by up to 45%. They also allow you to plan when to have a bleed by taking them back-to-back.
 * **Combined pill:** Taken daily.
 * **Ring:** Inserted vaginally, lasts 21 days.
 * **Patch:** Applied to skin, lasts 7 days.
* **Other Options:** For certain conditions or if other treatments haven't worked, a specialist may consider surgical options.
