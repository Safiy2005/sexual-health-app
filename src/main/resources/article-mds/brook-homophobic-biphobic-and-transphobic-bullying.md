# Homophobic, Biphobic and Transphobic Bullying

Homophobic, biphobic and transphobic bullying are the official terms used to describe bullying motivated by prejudice against LGBT+ people. Bullying is always wrong, whatever form it takes, and it is never your fault.

## Key Definitions

* **Homophobia**: Fear or dislike of someone based on the fact they are gay or homosexual.
* **Transphobia**: Fear or dislike of someone based on the fact they are trans.
* **Biphobia**: Fear or dislike of someone based on the fact they are bisexual.

## Common Signs of Bullying

Bullying can include anything from name-calling and spreading rumours to physical, sexual, or emotional abuse. If an interaction makes you feel upset or threatened, it is likely bullying. Common examples include:

* Making comments about a person’s gender or sexuality to deliberately make them uncomfortable.
* Making threats, putting someone down, or humiliating them.
* Inappropriate hand gestures or groping.
* Being ignored, excluded, or made to feel left out.
* Physical violence, such as hitting, pinching, biting, pushing, kicking, or punching.
* Stealing belongings or money.
* Making things up to get you into trouble.
* Threats and intimidation, including making silent or offensive phone calls.
* Posting or sharing insulting messages, rumours, or photos online.
* Sending offensive text messages.
* Threatening to "out" you by telling friends or family about your sexuality or gender.
* Comparing you to LGBT+ celebrities or characters to portray particular stereotypes.

## Your Rights

Behaving in an insulting or intimidating way towards someone because of their sexual orientation or gender identity is unacceptable. You have the right to report such incidents to the police. If you experience abuse or violence, there are many places where you can receive support.

## Understanding the Bully

It is important to remember that bullying is not your fault. Bullies often look for a target and use a "difference" to make the victim feel responsible for the abuse. Often, bullies feel bad about themselves and attack others to distract from their own problems or feelings of inadequacy. While this does not excuse their behavior, remembering this can help you realize that the bullying is not actually about you, your gender, or your sexuality.

## The Impact of Bullying

Bullying can significantly affect your mental and physical health, leading to:
* Feelings of worry, anxiety, or depression.
* Loneliness and low self-esteem.
* Avoiding school, college, or specific social locations.
* Internalised homophobia, where a person may hide their sexuality, ridicule themselves to gain acceptance, or feel shame and bitterness toward themselves or other LGBT+ people.

## How to Handle Bullying

### Practice Assertiveness
If it is safe to do so, being assertive can sometimes stop bullying before it escalates. This might involve challenging remarks or dismissing the bully so they see you are not an "easy target." Organizations like Kidscape and ChildLine offer resources on building confidence and practicing assertive body language.

### Talk to Someone
* **Friends**: They can provide emotional support and distraction.
* **Parents or Guardians**: Their support can be vital, even if you aren't ready to tell anyone else.
* **Teachers**: Schools have a legal duty to help you. By law, they must have anti-bullying and equal opportunities policies.

### Stay Safe
* **Physical Safety**: Travel in groups or take different routes home to avoid bullies.
* **Online Safety**: Block bullies on social media and phone contacts. Do not reply to abusive messages, as this can escalate the situation.
* **Keep Records**: Keep a diary of what is said and when. Take screenshots of online bullying to show to a trusted adult.

## Get Help

If you are being bullied and need support, you can contact:
* **ChildLine**: Call **0800 1111** for free to speak with a counselor, or use their online chat service.
* **Mental Health Support**: Many organizations specialize in supporting LGBT+ youth dealing with the effects of bullying.
