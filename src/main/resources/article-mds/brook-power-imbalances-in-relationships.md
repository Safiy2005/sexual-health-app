# Age Gaps and Power Imbalances in Relationships

When one person in a relationship has more power than the other, this is called a power imbalance. Power imbalances can come in many different forms, including:

* **Age differences:** If one partner is older or younger than the other.
* **Financial:** If one person earns more money than the other.
* **Authority:** If one person has more authority than the other that is given to them through their job (e.g., celebrities, civil servants, or if they work together).

## When Power Imbalances Can Be Problematic

Not all relationships with power imbalances are a cause for concern. They can be healthy depending on the nature of the imbalance, the age of the people involved, and their motives and personalities. However, sometimes they can be problematic and lead to abuse.

Some people deliberately seek out relationships where they have more power. This can stem from a lack of self-esteem, control, or power in other areas of their life, but often it comes down to wanting control over their partner.

## How Power Imbalances Can Make Someone Vulnerable

Power imbalances can make one person vulnerable in a relationship. For example, someone in such a relationship may:

* Feel they have less experience than their partner.
* Be reliant on their partner for certain things.
* Feel that their partner knows best about relationships and sex due to their greater experience.
* Feel the need to "prove" their maturity to their partner.
* Feel they cannot speak their mind because their partner has the ability to affect their job or career.

## The Abuser's Advantage

An abuser might use these feelings and vulnerabilities to their advantage, coercing or manipulating the other person into doing things they do not want to do or do not think are right. The abuse can be obvious, or it can be subtle and occur over time through actions like:

* Criticising and undermining.
* Telling them to delete social media or not go out with friends.
* Putting them down through negative comments.

An abuser might explain their behaviour as acts of love or normal relationship dynamics. What they describe as caring gestures can be more sinister methods of control, making it difficult for the victim to speak out and get help. This is particularly true if the abuse is used to isolate them from support systems like friends, family, or financial resources, which is known as coercive control.

## Age Gaps in Relationships

Age gaps in relationships are common. They are often more significant and noticeable when younger compared to when older. For example, a ten-year age gap in your 30s-40s is less significant than a two-year age gap in your teens.

Dating someone older or younger than you doesn't automatically mean the relationship is or will become unhealthy. However, it's important to consider:

* How does the age difference impact the relationship?
* Are you dating them because of the age difference?
* Does one person seem to make all the decisions?
* Do you both have the same maturity level?
* Does it feel like you are at the same stage in life and want the same things?
* Are there any signs of coercive control or abuse?

## The Law and Consent

The legal age of consent in the UK is 16, meaning individuals of this age can legally consent to sexual activity. This age rises to 18 if the other person is in a position of trust, such as a teacher or social worker. However, just because something is not illegal does not mean it is acceptable. Regardless of age, it is never okay to make someone do something they are uncomfortable with.

## Recognizing Coercive or Abusive Relationships

It can be difficult to identify if you are in a coercive or abusive relationship, as the behaviour may be defended as looking out for you or as acts of love. However, abuse is never okay. Signs to look out for include:

* You feel you cannot speak your mind.
* You modify your behaviour and actions because of them.
* You feel you don't have control over the relationship.
* You feel uncomfortable telling family or friends because they "won't understand."
* They ask you to do things that make you uncomfortable.
* You feel reliant on them for your basic needs.

If you experience anything that makes you uncomfortable, it is important to seek help. Domestic abuse laws protect anyone over the age of 16, not just those living together. Your safety is paramount, so never confront your partner if it would put you at risk.
