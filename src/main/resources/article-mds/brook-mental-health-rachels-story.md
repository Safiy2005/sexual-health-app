# Mental Health and a Birthday to Remember: Rachel’s Story

> **Content Warning:** This page contains discussions of mental illness and suicide.

Rachel shares her mental health journey spanning her 18th, 20th, and 21st birthdays. Her story illustrates the reality of living with severe mental health challenges and the eventual path toward stability and recovery.

## The 18th Birthday: Dissociation and Body Image
At 18, Rachel experienced frequent "dissociative spells"—episodes where she felt as though she were dreaming while awake. These episodes were characterized by:
* Physical symptoms: Skin prickling and a racing heart.
* Perceptual shifts: Surroundings appearing simultaneously bright and dim.
* Triggers: Emotional stress, specifically following a breakup and a period of intense obsession with a partner.

During this time, Rachel also struggled with severe cystic acne, which led to significant body dysmorphia and self-harm as a form of "punishment" for her appearance.

## The 20th Birthday: Crisis and Hospitalization
Rachel’s 20th birthday resulted in hospitalization following a severe panic attack. The admission was necessitated by a combination of factors:
* Being under the influence of alcohol.
* Threatening suicide.
* Extreme paranoia and feelings of humiliation.

This crisis followed six months of emotional instability, including the end of a difficult relationship and a series of unstable social interactions that left her feeling overwhelmed. Despite believing she was "better" than she had been at 18, her mental health had reached a breaking point.

## The 21st Birthday: Recovery During a Pandemic
By her 21st birthday, Rachel was navigating the global COVID-19 pandemic and a family crisis. However, this birthday marked a positive shift in her journey. Key factors in her improved wellbeing included:
* **Medical support:** Starting a course of Fluoxetine (an antidepressant) two weeks prior.
* **Support systems:** Strong emotional support from her mother and consistent communication with friends and family via video calls and socially distanced visits.
* **Healthy outlets:** Engaging in hobbies and small, meaningful celebrations.

## Reflections on Four Years of Recovery
Rachel’s path to recovery was not linear and involved significant professional intervention. Over the course of nearly four years, her journey included:
* Consulting with ten different counselors from the NHS, private practices, charities, and university services.
* Managing recurring suicidal ideation and panic attacks.
* Experiencing prolonged periods of dissociation.

Despite the intensity of these struggles, Rachel emphasizes that with time, medication, and professional support, circumstances can improve.

## Seeking Support
Approximately 1 in 4 people in the UK will experience a mental health problem each year (NHS England, 2020). It is important to remember:
* You do not need a formal diagnosis to seek help.
* You can talk to someone whenever you feel ready.
* Help and support are available through various organizations and healthcare providers.
