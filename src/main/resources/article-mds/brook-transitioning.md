# Transitioning: Gender Identity (Social, Medical or Legal)

Transitioning refers to the process of a person changing their gender expression (how they present their gender to the world) to match their gender identity (how a person sees or feels about themselves), rather than the sex they were assigned at birth.

Every person’s transition journey is unique and personal. It is an inner journey of reflection that often begins with a person coming out to themselves. Because this process can take time to process, individuals should take all the time they need.

There are three main types of transition:
* Social
* Medical
* Legal

A person may choose to transition in one, some, or all of these ways. Some trans people experience **gender dysphoria**, which is the distress or discomfort felt when sex assigned at birth does not match gender identity. However, not all trans people experience dysphoria, and not everyone chooses to transition. Some may refrain from transitioning due to safety concerns, cost, or personal preference. All choices are valid and deserve respect.

## Social Transitioning

Social transitioning involves changing how a person is perceived in society. This typically includes changes to social identity and gender expression.

### Social Identity
Some people change their social identity by using a different name or different pronouns (e.g., he, she, they, ze, xe). 
* It is common to experiment with names and pronouns before making a decision.
* Pronouns should be respected as they are a reflection of a person's gender identity.
* If unsure, it is helpful to ask which pronouns a person uses. "They/them" is a common gender-neutral option.

### Gender Expression
People may change their physical presentation to reflect their identity. This can include:
* Changing clothing or hairstyles.
* Starting or stopping the use of makeup.
* Voice training to soften or deepen the voice.
* Modifying body appearance, such as "binding" the chest to make it look flatter.

## Medical Transitioning

Medical transitioning involves physical changes to the body through medical intervention.

### Hormone Therapy
Hormone therapy uses sex hormones to change the body physically. 
* **Oestrogen (for transfeminine people):** Can lead to breast growth, increased fat on hips, reduced facial/body hair, and reduced muscle mass.
* **Testosterone (for transmasculine people):** Can lead to periods stopping, increased facial/body hair, deepening of the voice, and muscle growth.

Some changes caused by hormones are irreversible. Most doctors recommend living in your gender identity for at least three months before starting therapy, and counseling is often provided. In the UK, a referral to a gender specialist via a GP is required for NHS treatment.

### Puberty Blockers
Puberty blockers are medications used to suspend the physical changes of puberty. 
* **Current UK Law:** As of June 2024, new prescriptions for puberty blockers are generally not provided for trans or gender-questioning young people.
* Those prescribed them before June 2024 can continue their use.
* New access will primarily be through controlled research studies assessing risks and benefits.
* Referrals for support must come through an NHS paediatric service or Child and Young People’s mental health service.

### Gender Affirming Surgery
Surgery is a personal choice and may include various procedures:
* **For transfeminine people:** Breast implants, orchidectomy (removal of testicles), penectomy, vaginoplasty, or facial feminisation surgery.
* **For transmasculine people:** Chest recontouring (top surgery), hysterectomy (removal of the womb), phalloplasty, or scrotoplasty.

In the UK, standards of care generally require a person to live in their gender identity for 24 months before undergoing gender affirmation surgery.

## Legal Transitioning

Legal transitioning involves officially changing name, gender, or sex on legal documents.

### The Gender Recognition Act 2004
This act allows trans people to:
* Apply for a Gender Recognition Certificate (GRC).
* Obtain a new birth certificate.
* Marry or enter a civil partnership in their acquired gender.

To apply for a GRC, a person must be over 18 and prove they have (or have had) gender dysphoria, have lived in their gender identity for at least two years, and intend to live in that identity permanently.
