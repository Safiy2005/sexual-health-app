# Anxiety

Anxiety is feeling worried or scared, often in relation to something that might happen or is going to happen in the future. Although most people will experience anxiety at some point in their life, everyone’s experience is different. It is one of the most common mental health concerns and is often experienced alongside depression or low mood.

Anxiety is often caused by life events or situations like sitting exams, money problems, moving house, or changing jobs. Some people are more likely to experience anxiety than others; for example, LGBT+ people are more vulnerable due to bullying, stigma, and discrimination.

## When to Seek Help
If anxiety is making it hard to live your day-to-day life, it may be considered a mental health problem. You should consider seeking professional help if anxiety:
* Causes you to avoid social activities or situations.
* Leads you to withdraw from the outside world.
* Makes you struggle with everyday tasks.
* Stops you from enjoying hobbies or free time.
* Causes you a significant amount of distress.

You do not need to be diagnosed with an anxiety disorder for your experience to be valid and to seek support.

## Physical Symptoms of Anxiety
Although anxiety is an emotional state, it brings several physical symptoms. These may include:
* Feeling sick or nauseous.
* Restlessness and an inability to relax.
* Headaches.
* A fast heartbeat.
* Sweating.
* Trouble sleeping.
* Panic attacks.

## Panic Attacks
Panic attacks are bursts of intense anxiety. During a panic attack, you might feel overwhelmed, fearful, shake, or feel unable to breathe. They are temporary (usually lasting 10–20 minutes) and are not life-threatening. Focusing on the present moment is one way to overcome them. 

Unlike panic attacks, which may have no obvious cause, "anxiety attacks" tend to last longer and are usually caused by a specific trigger.

## Types of Anxiety Disorders
Anxiety can take many different forms, including:
* **Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD):** A long-term condition where you feel anxious most days, often without a specific trigger.
* **Social Anxiety Disorder:** A long-term fear of social situations, such as meeting new people or going to busy places where you feel watched.
* **Obsessive Compulsive Disorder (OCD):** Experiencing unwelcome thoughts or images (obsessions) and dealing with them through repetitive actions (compulsions), like washing hands or checking doors.
* **Panic Disorder:** Experiencing frequent panic attacks without an obvious cause.

## Anxiety and Sexual Health
Mental health and sexual health are intricately linked. Anxiety can impact your sex life in several ways:

### Desire and Libido
If you are struggling with racing thoughts, you may not have the mental space for sex. When your body feels under threat, your libido (desire for sex) often decreases. You might also find it difficult to be present during sex, making it harder to enjoy or reach orgasm.

### Performance Anxiety
You may experience anxiety specifically around sex, such as:
* Worrying about your first time or if it will hurt.
* Worrying about getting or staying hard (erections) or ejaculating too quickly.
* Worrying about performance or "being good at it."
* Feeling shame due to your upbringing.

Communication with your partner is essential to managing these expectations. Sex should always be pleasurable and something you want; you should never feel that you owe anyone sex.

### Physical Manifestations
Anxiety can cause physical barriers to sex even if you feel mentally aroused:
* **Erection Issues:** Anxiety can make it difficult to get or stay hard.
* **Vaginismus:** For people with vaginas, anxiety can cause the vagina to tighten involuntarily when trying to insert a finger, tampon, or penis, which can make penetration painful.

## Worries about STIs
The possibility of catching an STI makes some people anxious, often due to social stigma. Most STIs are easily treatable and will not impact long-term health if caught early. You can reduce this anxiety by practicing safe sex:
* Using condoms.
* Avoiding contact if a partner has tested positive.
* Taking PrEP if you are at high risk of HIV.

## Self-Care and Management
Many people find that self-care techniques help them take control of their anxiety:
* **Talk to others:** Speak with friends, family, or trusted adults. Sharing your feelings reminds you that you are not alone.
* **Journaling:** Writing down worries can help put them in perspective.
* **Mindfulness:** Techniques to focus on the present, such as focusing on your senses, body scanning for tension, and deep belly breathing.
* **Exercise:** Physical activity releases "feel-good" hormones and helps you focus on your movement and breath.

## Professional Help and Support
If you are struggling, several professional avenues are available:
* **Visit your GP:** They can assess your anxiety levels using a standardized test, prescribe medication, or refer you to specialist services.
* **Counselling and Talking Therapies:** You can access these via the NHS (referral or self-referral) or privately.
* **Support Organisations:**
 * **Anxiety UK:** Information, therapy, and helplines.
 * **Mind:** Support for all mental health conditions.
 * **NoPanic:** Specializes in panic disorders and OCD.
 * **Young Minds:** Guidance specifically for young people.
