# Orgasms: What Happens When Someone Orgasms?

Orgasms are the release of built-up physical and/or psychological sexual tension and arousal. An orgasm is a powerful feeling of physical pleasure and sensation, and can bring feelings of happiness or wellbeing. 

However, great sex does not need to include orgasms. While orgasms are fun, making them your only aim can take the enjoyment out of sex. Putting pressure on yourself or your partner can lead to performance anxiety. Our advice is to let go of "scripts" about how sex should be, be flexible with expectations, and go with the flow. 

## What happens when you orgasm?
An orgasm is created by the tensing and releasing of certain muscles, along with the release of feel-good hormones such as endorphins. This combination creates feelings of physical and emotional pleasure. 

Orgasming is also known as "coming" or "climaxing." While the experience is personal, there are biological differences in how it occurs:
* **People with a penis:** An orgasm serves a biological purpose of expelling semen (ejaculation), which can lead to conception if sperm meets an egg.
* **People with a vagina:** The orgasm is for pleasure and is not necessary for conception.

## People with a penis

### The build-up to an orgasm
* When aroused by touch or excitement, blood flows into the spongy tissue of the penis, causing an erection.
* The testicles move closer to the body and increase in size due to blood flow.
* Thigh and buttock muscles tense.
* Blood pressure, pulse, and breathing quicken.

### During an orgasm
* The pelvic floor muscles, prostate gland, vas deferens, and seminal vesicles contract.
* These contractions push semen into the urethra and out of the body (ejaculation).
* The average orgasm lasts 10–30 seconds.

### After an orgasm
* There is a "refractory period" during which further orgasms are not possible.
* This period can last from a few minutes to a few days and generally lengthens as people age.
* The penis and testicles return to their original size.

## People with a vagina

### The build-up to an orgasm
* Blood vessels within the genitals expand, causing the vulva to swell.
* Fluid passes through the vaginal walls, making the area wet.
* The vagina becomes longer.
* Heart rate, breath, and blood pressure increase.
* The person may look flushed on the neck and chest.
* Breasts may increase in size by up to 25% and nipples may appear less erect due to surrounding blood flow.
* The clitoris pulls back against the pubic bone.

### During an orgasm
* Pelvic floor muscles contract roughly every 0.8 seconds.
* Orgasms typically last an average of 13–51 seconds.
* Most people with vaginas do not have a refractory period and can experience multiple orgasms if stimulated again.

## The process of arousal
Arousal is the state of becoming physically and emotionally excited. Physical signs include an increased heart rate, tingling sensations, vaginal lubrication, or an erection. Arousal is the body’s way of preparing for sex, but it is pleasurable in itself and does not have to lead to an orgasm.

Most people with vaginas cannot orgasm through vaginal penetration alone; most require stimulation of the clitoris, which contains many nerve endings similar to the head of a penis.

## Masturbation and orgasms
Reaching an orgasm can take practice. Many people experience their first orgasm through masturbation. It is often easier to reach orgasm when you are relaxed, turned on, and communicating with a partner about what you like and dislike. Orgasms can vary based on mood, menstrual cycle, position, and communication.

## Orgasms in porn
Porn is generally not representative of real sexual experiences. It often suggests that people with penises can last a very long time and that people with vaginas orgasm quickly and easily. In reality:
* Porn uses editing, retakes, and staging tricks.
* Actors are at work and may not be naturally aroused; they often pretend to have orgasms.
* Real orgasms often look and feel very different from how they are portrayed on screen.

## Premature ejaculation
Premature ejaculation is when semen is discharged earlier than desired by the person or their partner. Most people with penises will experience this at some point. It is nothing to be ashamed of, but if it becomes a persistent concern, you can seek support from a GP or sexual health clinic.

## Female ejaculation
People with vulvas can also ejaculate, commonly referred to as "squirting." This is not experienced by everyone and is not always linked to an orgasm.

## Factors that affect the ability to orgasm

### Drugs and alcohol
While some use substances to feel more confident, they carry significant risks to sexual function:
* **Arousal:** Alcohol and drugs can decrease lubrication for people with vaginas and make it difficult for people with penises to maintain an erection.
* **Sensation:** Being high or drunk can reduce physical sensation, making it harder to reach orgasm.
* **Consent:** Alcohol and drugs impact the capacity to consent. Someone should be fully coordinated and responsive before engaging in sexual activity.
* **Risk:** Substances can impair judgment regarding contraception and STI protection.

### Mental health
Stress, anxiety, and depression can make it difficult to enjoy sex or reach orgasm. Additionally, some anti-depressant medications can lower sex drive and make orgasming harder.

### Hormones
Hormonal fluctuations, particularly during the menstrual cycle, can impact sex drive and physical response. Changes in hormones can lead to:
* Reduced sex drive.
* Reduced natural lubrication.
* Difficulty maintaining an erection.
* Reduced genital sensitivity.

It is natural for sex drive to fluctuate. However, if these changes are difficult to manage, it is helpful to speak with a GP or counselor.
