# Arguments: How to Manage Arguments with a Partner

Arguments are a normal part of any relationship; it is not possible to agree on everything. However, how you deal with arguments affects whether they have a positive or negative impact on your relationship and wellbeing.

Arguments often occur when one person feels strongly that they are right and the other is wrong. This can lead both partners to defend their positions while dismissing the other person’s feelings. Conflict can also arise when one person feels their needs are being neglected—for example, if a partner spends too much time on their phone instead of providing quality time.

## Common Reasons for Conflict
Research from *Enduring Love?* suggests that couples argue for many reasons, including:
* How to spend time together and how much time to spend together
* Differing views on the future of the relationship
* Jealousy and trust issues
* Cheating
* Money
* Distribution of housework and childcare (common in couples who live together)

## The Role of Arguing in Relationships
Arguments are a natural part of relationships. If you never argue, it may mean someone is bottling up their feelings, which can lead to unhappiness. The frequency of arguments varies between healthy relationships; the most important factor is that the positive interactions significantly outweigh the moments of bickering or conflict.

## Strategies for Managing Arguments

### Don’t focus on who’s right and who’s wrong
Try to move away from the idea of "winning" or "losing" an argument. Accept that there can be multiple valid points of view rather than one categorically right or wrong perspective.

### Talk about how YOU feel
Take responsibility for your own emotions. Consider if your reaction is being influenced by past experiences or other stresses in your life. Instead of saying, “You make me so angry,” which places blame, try using "I" statements.
* **Tip:** Try starting sentences with “It makes me feel [emotion] when…”

### Take time out
If you feel yourself becoming overwhelmed—experiencing breathlessness, a sick feeling, or a loss of control—take a break. It is difficult to reach a resolution when emotions are high. Take deep breaths or go for a walk, and agree to resume the conversation when you are both calmer.

### Be kind
Arguments do not have to be hurtful. You can explain your feelings without resorting to insults. If your partner says something that hurts you, explain why it was hurtful rather than trying to retaliate. Remember that everyone has different comfort levels with confrontation; raised voices can be frightening for some.

### Listen to how they feel
Give your partner space to talk without interruption. Try to put yourself in their position to understand their perspective. Being prepared to listen and consider their view makes it more likely that you will reach a compromise.

### Let it go
Conflict is often prolonged when partners are unwilling to forgive minor issues. Being forgiving can make a relationship much easier. This does not mean allowing someone to mistreat you; it means letting go of issues that have been talked through and no longer serve a purpose.
* **Tip:** If you find you cannot let an issue go (for example, after a breach of trust like cheating), the same argument may repeat indefinitely. If you cannot move past an issue, you may need to reconsider the relationship.

### Be self-aware
Understand the root of your feelings. Often, an argument about a small thing is actually about a hidden issue or a past experience. Exploring your triggers and emotions is known as "emotional intelligence," which helps build stronger, more satisfying relationships.

## Communicate Openly
Honest communication can often head off an argument before it begins. Do not expect your partner to read your mind; tell them what is wrong before feelings become too intense. Arguments can be a starting point for learning how to communicate more effectively.

## When Arguments Become Abuse
There are certain behaviors that should never be accepted in a relationship, as they are signs of abuse. Seek help if your partner:
* Raises a hand to you or hits you.
* Forces you to go further sexually than you want to.
* Gets angry when you do not drop everything for them or fails to respect your boundaries (e.g., criticizing what you wear or who you spend time with).
* **Gaslighting:** Makes you question your own reality, feelings, or interpretation of events.
* **Stonewalling:** Shuts down and refuses to communicate when you try to bring up a topic.

If you have experienced any of these, it is important to get help. Abuse is never your fault.
