# Cyber Bullying

Bullying is a distressing experience regardless of how it happens. Because of the internet and mobile phones, cyber bullying can occur at any time, giving bullies constant access to their targets. You may be bullied by someone you know in person, or by people you don’t know, often referred to as "trolls"—individuals who deliberately set out to upset others or start arguments.

Bullying online can feel "safer" to the person doing it because they do not have to see your reaction and can often hide behind an anonymous profile. Cyber bullying can take place anywhere online, including social media, video-sharing sites, text messages, online chat rooms, and gaming communities.

## Common examples of online bullying

Cyber bullying can take many forms. Some common examples include:

* Posting threatening or unpleasant messages online.
* Sharing or tagging embarrassing images or videos of people without their permission. (If these are sexual images, this may constitute revenge porn or child abuse imagery).
* Joining in on other people’s rude or abusive posts.
* Leaving sexist, racist, homophobic, transphobic, or biphobic comments on profile pages.
* Making unpleasant jokes or status updates about people.
* Sending messages pushing people to engage in sexual acts (which may be child sexual exploitation).
* Showing someone pornographic pictures or videos, or sending unwanted sexual images.
* Posting sexual comments, pictures, or videos online.

If a behavior is upsetting you, it is unacceptable. While there is no single legal definition of "cyber bullying" in UK law, there are various laws in place to protect against harassment and threatening behavior.

## What you can do if you are being bullied

If you or someone you know is the victim of online bullying, or if you are receiving abusive or sexual messages, here are the steps you should take:

* **Don’t respond:** Do not retaliate or reply to the messages, as this often makes the situation worse.
* **Block and report:** Use the tools on social media platforms to block the bully and report their behavior. You can also report bullying if you see it happening to someone else.
* **Do not share sexual content:** If you receive messages or images that are sexual in nature, do not send them to anyone else. It is illegal to send or possess indecent images of anyone under the age of 18.
* **Keep evidence:** Do not delete the messages. Keep them or take screenshots as evidence to show to a trusted adult.
* **Talk to someone:** Tell an adult you trust, such as a parent, carer, or teacher. Your friends can also provide support.
* **Contact professional services:** If you do not feel comfortable telling someone you know, contact an organization like ChildLine for confidential support.

## Putting your safety and mental health first

Being bullied can make you feel scared, vulnerable, and angry. Online bullying can feel particularly intense because it is hard to escape. 

* **Take a break:** Try to take time away from your phone and social media to clear your head and reduce stress.
* **Remember it is not your fault:** Never believe what a bully says about you. Their behavior is a reflection of them, not you.
* **Rebuild your confidence:** After bullying has been dealt with, it is normal for your confidence to feel low. It takes time to build it back up, and talking about your feelings is a key part of the healing process.

If cyber bullying is affecting your mental health, it is vital to speak to someone. You can call **ChildLine on 0800 1111** anytime (24/7) to talk to a trained counselor in complete confidence.
