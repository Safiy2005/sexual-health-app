# LGBT+ and mental health: What can you do?

Being LGBT+ doesn’t cause poor mental health. However, research shows that LGBT+ people are more likely to suffer from poor mental health than the rest of the population. This is largely due to the different and intersecting challenges the LGBT+ community faces in society.

## Mental health challenges in the LGBT+ community

Research indicates that LGBT+ people are:
* **1.5 times more likely** to experience anxiety and depression.
* More likely to misuse drugs and alcohol.
* More likely to experience eating disorders.
* More likely to suffer from low self-esteem.

These outcomes are often the result of external pressures and social challenges, including:
* Hate crimes, abuse, and violence (LGBT+ people are nearly four times more likely to be victims of violent crime).
* Bullying and cyberbullying.
* Discrimination.
* Challenges around coming out (which is often an ongoing process rather than a one-time event).
* Homophobia, biphobia, and transphobia.
* Rejection or being excluded from social or family circles.
* Additional challenges based on age, religion, ethnicity, and gender.

## 1. Get support from the LGBT+ community

It can feel lonely to be LGBT+, particularly if you haven’t come out or don't have LGBT+ friends. Connecting with people who have shared experiences can provide a vital support network.

**Where to find LGBT+ friends:**
* University social groups.
* Online communities (be careful with personal information and prioritize safety).
* Local events and support groups, such as pride events, book clubs, or sports groups.
* Volunteering for LGBT+ causes.
* LGBT+ bars, restaurants, or clubs.

## 2. Reach out to support organisations

There are many organisations dedicated to supporting the LGBT+ community. Talking to someone outside of your immediate social or family circle can help you process feelings you may have been suppressing.

**Recommended organisations:**
* **LGBT Foundation:** Offers helplines, peer support, and therapy.
* **Gendered Intelligence:** Support for trans, non-binary, and gender-questioning people.
* **Switchboard:** A dedicated LGBT+ helpline.

## 3. Look after your sexual health

Mental health is strongly linked to sexual and physical health. While supporting your mind, ensure you are also taking care of your body by:
* Getting tested for STIs regularly.
* Using contraception and condoms.
* Using PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis) if needed.

## 4. Manage negative news and social media

Negative media coverage and opinions about the LGBT+ community can be distressing. You can manage the impact of this content by:

* **Allowing yourself to feel:** It is okay to feel angry, sad, or frustrated. Bottling up these emotions can negatively impact your mental health.
* **Setting boundaries:** You do not have to stay constantly updated. Take breaks from the news or unfollow accounts that cause distress. You don't have to be an activist 24/7.
* **Seeking positive content:** Focus on inclusive, celebratory, and affirming media, such as films and TV shows that celebrate queer love.
* **Drawing on community:** Talk to friends who feel the same way to share the emotional load.
* **Taking action:** Sometimes taking a small action, like educating someone or supporting an LGBT+ cause, can help you regain a sense of hope and control.
* **Remembering your worth:** Surround yourself with people who validate your identity.

## 5. How to support a friend

If you want to support an LGBT+ friend with their mental health:
* **Be a good listener:** Ask questions and give them space to talk about their experiences.
* **Educate yourself:** Learn about LGBT+ issues and the specific challenges your friend may face.
* **Validate their feelings:** Let them know their emotions are valid.
* **Offer practical support:** Go to LGBT+ events with them or help them make new friends.
* **Be an active bystander:** Speak up against discrimination if it is safe to do so. Correct people if they use incorrect pronouns for your friend.
* **Check in:** If you see negative news, reach out to your friend to let them know you are thinking of them.
