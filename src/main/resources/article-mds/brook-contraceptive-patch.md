# The Contraceptive Patch

The contraceptive patch is a small, beige, 5cm by 5cm patch applied to the skin like a plaster. It prevents pregnancy by releasing two hormones—oestrogen and progestogen—through the skin into the bloodstream. These hormones are similar to those produced naturally in the ovaries.

In the UK, the brand currently available is Evra.

## Key Facts
* **Effectiveness:** More than 99% effective if used correctly without mistakes.
* **Hormones:** Contains both oestrogen and progestogen.
* **Maintenance:** Requires changing the patch once a week for three weeks, followed by a one-week break.
* **STIs:** Does not protect against sexually transmitted infections; a barrier method like condoms should be used for STI protection.
* **Visibility:** The patch is a light beige colour. It may be more visible on some skin tones than others, though it can be placed in discreet areas.
* **Prescription:** Requires a prescription from a doctor or nurse.

## How it Works
The contraceptive patch prevents pregnancy in three ways:
* It stops the ovaries from releasing an egg (ovulation).
* It thickens the cervical mucus, making it harder for sperm to reach an egg.
* It thins the lining of the uterus (womb), making it difficult for a fertilised egg to implant.

## Pros and Cons

### Pros
* Does not interrupt sex.
* You only need to remember it once a week rather than every day.
* Effectiveness is not impacted by vomiting or diarrhoea.
* Can make periods more regular, lighter, and less painful.
* Can help improve acne and premenstrual symptoms (PMS).
* May reduce the risk of certain cancers.

### Cons
* Temporary side effects in the first few months may include headaches, nausea, breast tenderness, mood swings, and spotting.
* Does not protect against STIs.
* Can cause skin irritation for some users.
* It is visible on the skin.

## Bleeding and the Patch-Free Week
During your patch-free week, you will likely experience a "withdrawal bleed." This is not a biological period but is caused by the absence of hormones during that week. You should start your next four-week cycle on time, even if you are still bleeding.

Some users choose to skip the patch-free week to avoid bleeding. You should consult a doctor or nurse to see if continuous use is right for you. If you miss more than two bleeds while using the patch normally, seek medical advice.

## Starting the Patch
You can start the patch at any time during your menstrual cycle.
* **If started during the first five days of your period:** You are protected against pregnancy immediately (unless you have a very short cycle of 23 days or less, in which case you must start within the first four days).
* **If started at any other time:** You must use additional contraception (like condoms) for the first seven days.

### Starting after Pregnancy
* **After giving birth:** If not breastfeeding, you can start on day 21 for immediate protection. If breastfeeding, it is usually advised to wait six weeks as the patch can reduce milk flow.
* **After miscarriage or abortion:** You can start immediately and will be protected against pregnancy right away.

## How to Use the Patch
1. Apply the patch to clean, dry, hairless skin (e.g., upper arm, shoulder, or buttocks). Do not apply to breasts or irritated skin.
2. Change the patch every week on the same day (the "patch change day") for three weeks.
3. On the fourth week, do not wear a patch.
4. After exactly seven patch-free days, apply a new patch to begin a new cycle.

**Tips:**
* Change the location of the patch each time to avoid skin irritation.
* Avoid using body creams or lotions near the patch, as they may cause it to loosen.
* The patch is designed to stay on during showers, baths, and swimming.

## What to do if the Patch Falls Off
If the patch becomes loose or falls off, the required action depends on how long it has been off:

* **Off for less than 48 hours:** Stick it back on as soon as possible. If it doesn't stick, use a new patch. Continue your cycle as normal. No extra contraception is needed if it was used correctly for the 7 days prior.
* **Off for more than 48 hours:** Apply a new patch immediately. This now becomes your new "patch change day" and marks the start of a new four-week cycle. You **must** use additional contraception (condoms) for the next seven days. If you had unprotected sex during the time it was off, you may need emergency contraception.

## Forgetting to Change the Patch
Instructions vary depending on where you are in your cycle:

### Week 1 or 2
* **Less than 48 hours late:** Change it immediately. No extra contraception is needed.
* **More than 48 hours late:** Start a brand new four-week cycle. Use extra contraception for seven days. If you had sex in the previous few days, consult a professional regarding emergency contraception.

### Week 3
* Remove the patch as soon as possible and start your patch-free break. Start a new cycle on your usual day.
* If you are more than seven days late in removing it, you may not be protected. Start a new cycle and use extra contraception for seven days.

### After the Patch-Free Week
* **Less than 48 hours late:** Apply a new patch immediately. You are still protected.
* **More than 48 hours late:** Apply a new patch and use extra contraception for seven days. You may need emergency contraception if you had sex during the break.

## Stopping the Patch
Hormones leave your system within a couple of days of stopping. Fertility generally returns rapidly, though it can take a few weeks or months for your natural cycle to regulate. If your periods do not return after three months, consult a healthcare provider.
