# Gonorrhoea: Symptoms, Tests & Treatments

Gonorrhoea is the second most common bacterial sexually transmitted infection (STI) in the UK. It is a serious condition that, if left untreated, can lead to infertility. 

It is important to note that many people do not experience symptoms:
* Half of women with gonorrhoea have no obvious signs or symptoms.
* 1 in 10 men have no obvious signs or symptoms.

Because of this, it is vital to get tested if you have had unprotected sex, even if you feel fine.

## Symptoms of Gonorrhoea
Symptoms usually develop within 1 to 14 days, though for some people, they may not appear until months later. Common symptoms include:

* Increased discharge from the vagina (often green or yellow)
* Fluid coming out of the penis (often green or yellow)
* A pain or burning sensation when urinating
* Pain or tenderness in the lower abdomen (tummy)
* Bleeding between periods or after sex
* Pain or tenderness in the testicles

Gonorrhoea can also infect other areas of the body:
* **Throat:** Usually shows no symptoms.
* **Rectum:** Can cause pain, discomfort, or discharge.
* **Eyes:** Can cause conjunctivitis.

## Causes and Transmission
Gonorrhoea is caused by bacteria called *Neisseria gonorrhoea* (or gonococcus), which is found in the semen and vaginal fluid of an infected person. It is passed on through:

* Unprotected vaginal sex
* Unprotected anal sex
* Unprotected oral sex
* Sharing sex toys without washing them or covering them with a fresh condom

**You cannot get gonorrhoea from:**
* Kissing or hugging
* Sharing baths or towels
* Swimming pools
* Toilet seats
* Sharing cups, plates, or cutlery

## Risks of Untreated Gonorrhoea
If left untreated, the infection can spread to other parts of the body. In both men and women, this can lead to long-term health complications, including infertility. Gonorrhoea can also be passed from a mother to her baby during childbirth, though it can be safely treated during pregnancy with antibiotics.

## Testing for Gonorrhoea
If you have had unprotected sex or have symptoms, you should get tested. Accurate results are usually available two weeks after the unprotected sexual encounter.

* **The Swab Test:** A small cotton bud is used to collect a sample from the vagina or the tip of the penis. Women can often take this sample themselves. Swabs may also be taken from the throat or rectum if you have had oral or anal sex.
* **Urine Test:** People with a penis may be asked to provide a urine sample instead of, or in addition to, a swab.

Testing is free and confidential at Brook services, GUM clinics, sexual health clinics, and some GP surgeries. You can also purchase self-test kits or access free home testing kits in some areas. Please note that routine blood tests and cervical smear tests do not detect gonorrhoea.

## Treatment
In most cases, treatment involves a single antibiotic injection. If you have symptoms, they should improve quickly after treatment.

**Follow-up care:**
* You will be advised to return for a repeat test 2–4 weeks later to ensure the infection is completely cleared.
* Avoid all sexual contact until you have been given the "all-clear" to prevent reinfection or passing it to others.
* Return to the clinic if symptoms do not improve within a week, if you have unprotected sex again, or if you did not complete the treatment as instructed.

## Partner Notification
It is essential to inform any sexual partners from the last three months so they can be tested and treated. Many clinics offer a "partner notification" service where they contact your partners anonymously to recommend testing without mentioning your name.

## Antibiotic Resistance
There have been reports of a "super-strain" of gonorrhoea that is resistant to certain antibiotics. Health professionals are aware of this and ensure that the most effective antibiotics are prescribed to combat resistance.

## Prevention
To protect yourself and your partners from gonorrhoea and other STIs:
* Use a condom every time you have vaginal, anal, or oral sex.
* Wash sex toys or cover them with a new condom between each use.
* Get regular STI screenings if you are sexually active with new or multiple partners.
