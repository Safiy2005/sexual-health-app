# Trichomoniasis: Symptoms, Tests, and Treatments

Trichomoniasis is a sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by a tiny parasite called *Trichomonas vaginalis* (TV). It commonly infects the vagina and the urethra in both men and women.

Up to half of the people infected with trichomoniasis will not experience any symptoms. When symptoms do appear, they are often easily confused with other STIs and usually manifest within a month of coming into contact with the infection.

## Symptoms

### Symptoms for people with a vagina
* Vaginal itching, swelling, and soreness.
* Unusual discharge that may be frothy, yellow-green in color, and have a fishy smell.
* Lower abdominal (tummy) pain.
* Pain during sex or while urinating.
* Itchy inner thighs.

### Symptoms for people with a penis
* Thin, white discharge from the penis.
* Pain during ejaculation or while urinating.
* Itching, swelling, and soreness of the penis.
* Needing to urinate more frequently.

## Causes and Transmission

Trichomoniasis is caused by the *Trichomonas vaginalis* (TV) parasite. The infection is easily passed on through:
* Unprotected vaginal sex.
* Sharing sex toys.

It is currently unknown whether the infection can be passed on by genital-to-genital contact alone or by transferring discharge via fingers. You cannot contract trichomoniasis from hugging, kissing, sharing cutlery, or using toilet seats.

## Diagnosis and Testing

Diagnosing trichomoniasis can be difficult because its symptoms mimic other STIs. During an examination:
* **For women:** A doctor or nurse will look for red patches on the walls of the vagina and take a swab.
* **For men:** A doctor or nurse will look for discharge, swelling, or soreness of the head of the penis. A swab or urine sample will be taken.

A swab involves using a small cotton bud or plastic loop to collect a sample. While it may be slightly uncomfortable, it should not be painful.

### Test Results
* **Point of care testing:** Results are produced during the appointment.
* **Microscope diagnosis:** Results are provided during the visit after a clinician examines the sample under a microscope.
* **Lab testing:** The sample is sent to a lab, with results typically returning within one week.

If there is a high likelihood of infection (e.g., a partner has tested positive), treatment may be started before the results are finalized.

## Treatment

Trichomoniasis is unlikely to resolve on its own, but it is easily treated with antibiotics. 
* **Medication:** You will typically be prescribed metronidazole, taken for five to seven days.
* **Alcohol:** You must not drink alcohol while taking metronidazole and should wait at least 48 hours after finishing the course before consuming alcohol, as it can cause severe nausea.
* **Pregnancy:** Trichomoniasis can cause complications during pregnancy. It is safe to treat, but you must inform your healthcare provider if you are or think you might be pregnant.

## Follow-up Care

You should avoid having sex until you have completed your treatment and been given the "all-clear" to prevent reinfection or passing it to others. You should return to the clinic if:
* Symptoms do not improve within a week.
* You have unprotected sex before treatment is finished.
* You did not complete the full course of antibiotics.
* You vomited after taking your medication.

## Telling Your Partners

It is important to inform any sexual partners from the last six months so they can be tested and treated. Some clinics offer a "contact slip" service, which allows them to notify your partners anonymously without mentioning your name.
