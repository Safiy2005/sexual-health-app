# Contraception During Perimenopause and Menopause

If you don’t want to get pregnant in the perimenopause, you need to use contraception. Contraception not only prevents pregnancy but can also help with menopausal symptoms. You might find that the best method for you during perimenopause may be different from the one you usually use.

You can still get pregnant until you reach menopause (one year after your periods have stopped) or the age of 55, whichever comes first. So, if you are in perimenopause and don’t want to get pregnant, it’s important to use contraception even if your periods are irregular and only come every few months.

It’s advised that, if you are under 50 but have reached menopause, you use contraception for two years after your last period. If you’re unsure if you’re still fertile, speak to a healthcare professional before you stop using contraception.

You can get a Sexually Transmitted Infection (STI) at any age, so make sure you use condoms and get tested regularly, especially when you have sex with someone new.

## Important Considerations for Contraception in Perimenopause

When thinking about contraception during perimenopause, there are a few things you need to discuss with a healthcare professional regarding your current method or one you want to start:

* **HRT (Hormone Replacement Therapy) Compatibility:** HRT is not compatible with all contraceptive methods. If you are planning on using HRT, some methods will not be recommended. The Mirena coil can also be used as the progestogen element of HRT.
* **Health Conditions:** Some health conditions or risk factors can mean certain methods are unadvisable.
* **Age:** Your age may limit the methods available to you.
* **Symptom Management:** You may be able to use contraception to help with your perimenopause symptoms.

It’s useful to understand more about the different methods and how they interact with perimenopause while exploring your options.

## Combined Hormonal Methods

Combined methods contain progesterone and oestrogen. They include the combined pill, vaginal ring, and the patch.

### What are they?

Combined methods contain progesterone and oestrogen. They include the combined pill, vaginal ring, and the patch.

### Benefits

The oestrogen in these methods can help with hot flushes and other menopausal symptoms, and they can help improve bone density, which declines during perimenopause.

### Use with HRT

These methods cannot be used at the same time as HRT as the hormone levels in these methods are much higher. However, they can act as a replacement for HRT as they can help with symptoms.

### Who can use them?

You can use these methods until the age of 50 if you have no health risks, which you should discuss with a healthcare professional. After the age of 50, it’s not recommended to continue using the combined methods as they can increase your risk of blood clots.

### Impact on Menopause Diagnosis

With combined methods, you will continue to get a withdrawal bleed that looks like a period, which can make it hard to know when you’ve reached menopause. The blood test that is sometimes given to help determine if you are still fertile will not be accurate if you are using combined methods.

## Progestogen-Only Methods

Progestogen-only methods include the injection, the implant, the progestogen-only pill, and the hormonal coil (LNG-IUC). Unlike the combined methods, they don’t contain oestrogen.

### What are they?

Progestogen-only methods include the injection, the implant, the progestogen-only pill, and the hormonal coil (LNG-IUC). Unlike the combined methods, they don’t contain oestrogen.

### Benefits

These methods are a good option if you have heavy periods, which can be a symptom of perimenopause, as it’s common to get no bleeding or light, infrequent bleeding.

### Use with HRT

Progestogen-only methods can be used safely with HRT, and the Mirena coil (a type of hormonal coil) can be used as a form of HRT.

### Who can use them?

You can take progestogen-only methods if you have certain health conditions or risk factors that mean you can’t use combined contraception methods.

### Impact on Menopause Diagnosis

As you don’t get a period with these methods (although you may get some bleeding), it can be hard to know when you’ve reached menopause. If you’re not sure and are using a progestogen-only method, you may be able to take a menopause test at your GP surgery that will measure the levels of follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) in your blood. This is not 100% reliable, particularly if you’re over 45.

### Progestogen-Only Pill

The progestogen-only pill is taken every day. It is safe to use at any age.

### Contraceptive Injection

The injection is a long-acting reversible contraception (LARC) that means you don’t need to think about it every day. The injection can affect some people’s bone density. This is usually not long-term, but as your bones thin as you get older, this may be worth discussing with a healthcare professional. For this reason, the injection may not be recommended to you if you have any risk factors or if you are over 50.

### Contraceptive Implant

The implant is a long-acting reversible contraception (LARC) that means you don’t have to think about it every day. The contraceptive implant is safe to use at any age.

### Hormonal Coil (LNG-IUC)

The hormonal coil is a small, T-shaped plastic device that, once fitted by a specially trained doctor or nurse, prevents pregnancy for 3-5 years. The hormonal coil is safe to use at any age and is particularly good at helping with heavy, painful periods.

The Mirena coil is the only method that can be used as a form of HRT as well as contraception. If the Mirena coil is being used for HRT, it will need to be replaced every five years. If being used solely as contraception, it is possible to keep the coil in for longer than five years, sometimes up to 10 years depending on your age. Your healthcare professional will be able to advise.

## Non-Hormonal Methods

Barrier methods and the copper coil are all methods that don’t contain hormones.

### What are they?

Barrier methods and the copper coil are all methods that don’t contain hormones.

### Benefits

Non-hormonal methods won’t interfere with your hormone levels, meaning you will be able to tell when you enter perimenopause and then menopause. They won’t cause any hormonal side effects but also can’t help with menopause symptoms.

### Use with HRT

As they don’t contain hormones, they are safe to use with HRT.

### Copper Coil (Cu-IUC)

The copper coil (cu-IUC) is a small T-shaped device inserted into the uterus by a trained professional. Copper coils can last up to 10 years and can be left in place until after menopause if fitted after the age of 40. If you suffer from heavy periods, this may not be the method for you as it can cause periods to become heavier or more painful.

### Condoms

You can continue to use condoms as you would before perimenopause, but if this is your chosen method of contraception, it’s really important you use one every time you have sex, even if your periods are very irregular, as it can be hard to predict when your next ovulation will be. A common symptom of perimenopause is vaginal dryness – to help, use lube or buy lubricated condoms. Don’t use oil-based products with condoms, as it can cause them to split.

### Caps and Diaphragms

Caps and diaphragms are silicone domes that are inserted into the vagina before sex and kept in until 6 hours after sex. They can be used at any age, but if you suffer with weak pelvic floor muscles or an overactive bladder, which can be common during perimenopause, these may be more uncomfortable to use.

## Fertility Awareness Methods

Also known as natural family planning, this method involves tracking your temperature, cycle, and other fertility signs to predict your fertile days. This is not recommended during perimenopause if you don’t want to get pregnant, as irregular periods make it hard to predict when your fertile days are.

## Sterilisation

Tubal occlusion (for women) or vasectomy (for men) are permanent contraception methods. You can get these at any point. There are often long waiting lists for these surgical contraception methods, and you will need to use other contraception in the meantime. For this reason, many people use LARC methods such as the hormonal coil, which has the same effectiveness at preventing pregnancy but is less invasive and easier to access.

## Contraception and Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT)

### Methods Safe to Use with HRT

The following contraceptive methods are safe to use with HRT:

* Barrier methods like condoms and caps and diaphragms
* Copper coil
* Hormonal coil
* Implant
* Injection
* Progestogen-only pill

### Mirena Coil as HRT

The Mirena coil is the only method of contraception that also acts as HRT. You can use this method as the progestogen element of your HRT so you only need to take the oestrogen part. One option is to have the hormonal coil fitted as your contraception method; this can then also be used as the progestogen element of your HRT.

### HRT and Contraceptive Effectiveness

The hormone levels in HRT are too low to act as contraception, so you will still need to use contraception during perimenopause. The levels of oestrogen and progestogen found in HRT are not enough to prevent ovulation.

## Hormonal Contraception and Menopause Symptoms

Hormonal contraception cannot delay menopause, but the hormones in these methods might mask or help manage your symptoms so you could enter perimenopause or menopause without realising. For example, you may still get regular bleeding, but this doesn’t mean you are ovulating each month (remember a withdrawal bleed is different to a period). As irregular periods are a key indicator of perimenopause, you won’t know when you’ve entered this stage.

## Emergency Contraception

Yes, you can still use emergency contraception if you have had sex without contraception or you think that your contraception may have failed. This will not be affected by your HRT.
