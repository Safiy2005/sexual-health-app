# Getting Your Cervical Screening (Smear Test)

Cervical screening, also known as a smear test, is a procedure where a doctor or nurse takes a sample of cells from your cervix. The cervix is the narrow part of the uterus (womb) that joins to the top of the vagina. 

These cells are tested for "high risk" types of human papillomavirus (HPV) that could potentially lead to cancer. It is important to remember that cervical screening is not a test for cancer; it is a preventative test to help stop cancer from developing.

## Eligibility and Frequency

Screening is offered to women and people with a cervix aged 25 to 64. 

* **Aged 25 to 49:** Invited every three years (usually starting six months before turning 25).
* **Aged 50 to 64:** Invited every five years.

Even if you have had the HPV vaccine or are not currently sexually active, screening remains important as the vaccine does not protect against all strains, and HPV can remain in the body for a long time without symptoms.

## What Happens During the Appointment?

The screening is normally performed by a female nurse or doctor and follows these steps:

* You will be asked to remove your trousers or skirt and underwear.
* You will lie down on an examination bed with your legs bent and apart.
* You will be given a paper bib to cover your bottom half.
* The practitioner inserts a smooth, tube-shaped tool called a speculum into the vagina. This opens the walls of the vagina so the cervix can be seen.
* A soft brush is inserted through the speculum to the cervix and rotated a few times to collect a sample of cells.
* The sample is placed in a specimen pot and sent to a lab.

You are in control throughout the process and can ask the practitioner to stop the test at any time.

## Preparing for Your Screening

To ensure the best results and your own comfort, keep the following in mind:

* **Avoid certain products:** For two days before the test, do not use vaginal medicines, lubricants, or creams.
* **Sexual activity:** It is best to avoid penetrative sex for at least 24 hours before the screening.
* **Menstrual cycle:** Try to book your appointment for a time when you are not on your period. Ideally, avoid the two days before and after your period.
* **Infections:** If you are being treated for a pelvic infection or unusual discharge, wait until your treatment is finished before having the test.
* **Hygiene:** You may shower before your appointment. Wash your vulva (the outside area) with water or unscented soap only. Never wash inside the vagina, as it is self-cleaning and internal washing can cause infections like BV or thrush.

Do not worry about the appearance of your vulva or pubic hair; medical professionals perform these tests regularly and are focused solely on your health.

## Tips for Reducing Anxiety and Discomfort

While some people find the procedure uncomfortable, it is often quicker if you are able to relax your abdominal and vaginal muscles. To help manage anxiety:

* **Bring support:** You can bring someone with you for company.
* **Communication:** Tell the nurse if you are nervous or have had painful experiences (such as vaginismus or vulvodynia).
* **Adjustments:** Ask for a smaller speculum, ask to put the speculum in yourself, or ask to lie in a different position.
* **Clothing:** Wear a skirt or dress; you can keep this on during the screening and only remove your underwear.
* **Time:** Ask for a longer appointment to allow yourself more time to process the experience.

## Understanding Your Results

Your nurse or doctor will tell you when to expect your results.

* **No HPV/Abnormal Cells:** If no high-risk HPV or abnormal cells are found, you will not need further tests until your next scheduled screening.
* **HPV Positive:** If the sample tests positive for HPV, it is then checked for changes in the cervical cells. These changes can be treated before they have a chance to turn into cancer. 

Being HPV positive does not mean you have cancer. HPV is a very common group of viruses, and the screening is designed to monitor and manage any risks early.
