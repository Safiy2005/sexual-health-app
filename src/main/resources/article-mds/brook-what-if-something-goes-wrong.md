# What if something goes wrong?

## Summary
    *   **Overview** A guide on the immediate steps to take after unprotected sex or contraceptive failure, including information on pregnancy prevention, STI testing, and support for sexual assault.
    *   **Disclaimer** The following content is from brook.org.

## Unprotected sex
**Understanding the risks** Unprotected sex is sex without a condom or other form of contraception. It also includes situations where contraception failed, such as a condom splitting, slipping or coming off, or if you missed a pill, were late with an injection, or if a patch or ring fell off. If you’ve had unprotected sex, there is a risk of pregnancy and sexually transmitted infections (STIs). You may need to seek advice or medical help as soon as possible, especially if you want to access emergency contraception.

## Emergency contraception: Part 1
**Types and effectiveness** If you have had unprotected sex and you don’t want to get pregnant, you can use emergency contraception. There are two main types. The first is the non-hormonal emergency contraceptive (the copper coil or IUD), which is the most effective form of emergency contraception. It can be fitted up to five days after unprotected sex, or sometimes even longer. The second type is the emergency contraceptive pill (the morning after pill). There are two kinds of pills: one containing levonorgestrel and one containing ulipristal acetate.

## Emergency contraception: Part 2
**Missed doses and timing** The morning after pill needs to be taken as soon as possible after unprotected sex to be most effective. Levonorgestrel can be taken up to 3 days (72 hours) after sex, while ulipristal acetate can be taken up to 5 days (120 hours) after sex. If you are already using hormonal contraception and have missed a pill or are late with a patch or ring, the advice for what to do depends on the type of contraception you are using. You should check the patient information leaflet or consult a professional.

## Sexually transmitted infections (STIs)
**Testing and window periods** If you have had unprotected sex, there is also a risk that you may have caught or passed on a sexually transmitted infection (STI). Many STIs don’t have any symptoms, so the only way to know for sure is to get tested. You can get an STI test at a sexual health clinic or order a kit to use at home. It is often recommended to wait a certain amount of time after the unprotected sex before testing to ensure the results are accurate. For example, it’s best to wait two weeks for a chlamydia test.

## Sexual assault and violence
**Support and safety** If something has gone wrong because you were forced or pressured into having sex without your consent, this is sexual assault or rape. It is not your fault and there is support available. You can visit a sexual health clinic, a SARC (Sexual Assault Referral Centre), or talk to a GP. These services are confidential and can help you with emergency contraception, STI testing, and emotional support. They will not pressure you to report the incident to the police, but they can help you if you decide to do so.
