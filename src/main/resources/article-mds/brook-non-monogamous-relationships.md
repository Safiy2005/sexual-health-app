# Polyamory and Non-monogamous Relationships

There are no one-size-fits-all rules for relationships; it’s about finding what works for you. For some, this means being monogamous – having only one partner. For others, it means being non-monogamous, which involves having more than one partner, or having one partner but also having sex with other people.

Research indicates that about five per cent of relationships are openly non-monogamous, or polyamorous. This discussion focuses on agreed-upon non-monogamy, not secret non-monogamy, otherwise known as cheating.

Non-monogamy is an arrangement where partners agree to be together and are open and honest about having other partners. For this reason, it is sometimes referred to as ethical non-monogamy.

Polyamory is a type of non-monogamy where people have loving relationships with more than one person. In contrast, in other non-monogamous relationships, such as open relationships, love may be reserved for one person, while sexual relationships may be had with other people.

## Types of Non-monogamous Relationships

Here are some common forms of non-monogamous relationships:

* **Open relationships:** In an open relationship, a couple agrees that they are both free to have sex with other people. While sex with others is permissible, loving someone else is generally not.
* **Polyamory:** Polyamory literally means ‘many loves’. In polyamorous relationships, it is considered acceptable to love more than one person, as well as have sex with them.
* **Hierarchical polyamory:** This structure involves people having one main partner alongside other relationships that are considered less of a priority (often termed primary and secondary relationships).
* **Egalitarian polyamory:** In egalitarian polyamory, there is no hierarchy; each partner is considered equal. Individuals in this relationship structure may live together in configurations such as a triad or quad.
* **Solo polyamory:** Solo polyamory describes a person who lives alone but visits or is visited by several partners.
* **Relationship anarchists:** Relationship anarchists question traditional distinctions between friends and partners and may have many close individuals in their lives.

One common thread in most open (or ethical) non-monogamous relationships is that everyone involved is aware of the arrangement and consents to it.

## Experiences and Common Themes

Non-monogamous relationships, much like monogamous ones, can be happy, satisfying, and long-lasting, though they can also be difficult and challenging. Being in a non-monogamous relationship does not inherently make one unhealthier or unhappier.

For example, Emmie and Theo, a couple interviewed as part of the Enduring Love? research project, are in a hierarchical polyamorous relationship. They view themselves as a primary couple and their main source of comfort, but both can see other people and share a mutual lover. As Emmie states:

"The rules stay the same for both of us, we can both have other partners but we have to be open and honest about it …it’s that kind of openness, that kind of honesty and that is really at the heart of our relationship."

## Managing Jealousy

The question of jealousy is common and for many, it might be a natural response to a partner having some form of relationship with another person. However, people who are polyamorous have challenged this by encouraging the idea of ‘owning’ those feelings or even feeling ‘compersion’ or ‘frubble’. These terms have developed to express the opposite of jealousy, referring to the feeling of happiness or joy someone experiences when their partner is happy with someone else.

## Relationship Management and Rules

Just as with monogamous relationships, people in non-monogamous relationships employ diverse methods for managing their dynamics. For instance, some prefer clear rules for their relationship, while others grant each other considerable freedom, trusting in their judgment. Some in open relationships share all details, whereas others opt to keep their various relationships private. Naturally, individuals in non-monogamous relationships are just as capable of breaking rules and keeping secrets as those in monogamous ones.

## Monogamy and Non-monogamy as a Spectrum

It can be useful to view monogamy and non-monogamy as existing on a spectrum rather than being an either/or choice. The following activity may help you consider your preferred relationship style:

1. **Emotional Closeness Continuum:** Imagine a line representing emotional closeness. At one end is monoamory (one close intimate relationship with no close relationships outside this), and at the other is polyamory (multiple close relationships).
 * Draw an X at the point on the line where you think you’d generally like to be (recognizing this might change over time).
 * Consider where any past and current partners might be on this scale. Are you both in the same place? Perhaps different places? Is everyone content with their position?
 * Specific examples for the emotional closeness continuum could include:
 * At the far monoamory end: Just having one partner you discuss everything with and nobody else who feels that close.
 * Towards the middle: Having several friends who are as close to you as your partner – people you could call at 3 am or share something with every day.
 * Towards the polyamory end: Maintaining a very close relationship with an ex-partner or staying up all night talking with a person you’ve recently met.
2. **Physical Intimacy Continuum:** Now, do the same for physical intimacy. Imagine a line with monosexuality (no physical intimacy outside a main relationship) at one end and polysexuality (multiple sexual partners) at the other.
 * Examples for this continuum could include:
 * At one end: Only hugging your partner and no one else.
 * Towards the middle: Being okay with fantasizing about other people but not acting on it, or perhaps engaging in online sex.
 * Towards the polysexuality end: The kinds of non-monogamous relationships mentioned earlier.

The important takeaway is that there is no ‘right’ or ‘wrong’ place to be on these lines. Different approaches work for different people. If you find that you and your partner(s) are in different places, it may be beneficial to discuss it openly.

## Navigating Differences in Relationships

If it turns out that you and your partner are in very different places on the monogamy or non-monogamy spectrum, various feelings may arise. If you are monogamous, you might feel hurt and rejected if your partner desires to be with other people, given societal narratives that emphasize finding ‘the one’. Conversely, if you are non-monogamous, you might feel stifled by the idea of monogamy and resent a monogamous partner’s demands.

First, understand that a difference in preference does not mean one person is wrong. It does mean that if you wish to remain together, both parties will need to make compromises, a truth applicable to all long-term relationships.

The crucial step is to be honest and clear with each other about your desires for the relationship and any concerns you may have. This approach allows you to work together to establish a set of relationship rules or a contract that can foster happiness for both. For example, a monogamous partner might express a desire to meet their partner’s other partners, while a non-monogamous partner might request that their other partners are treated with respect.

Once relationship rules are agreed upon, it is vital to adhere to them unless both parties consent to renegotiate. Breaking rules, lying, cheating, or neglecting each other’s feelings will inevitably strain the relationship (applicable to both monogamous and non-monogamous dynamics).

You may ultimately decide that a mixed-orientation relationship (monogamous/non-monogamous) is not viable for you and agree to separate. While this can be painful, it’s important to remember that neither person intends to hurt the other; rather, the differences were simply too wide to bridge.
