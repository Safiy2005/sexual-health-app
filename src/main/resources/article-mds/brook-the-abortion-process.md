# The Abortion Process

## Summary
*   **Overview** A comprehensive guide to the abortion process, detailing initial consultations, medical and surgical procedures, physical recovery, and emotional support options.
*   **Disclaimer** The following content is from brook.org.

## What is abortion?
Abortion is when someone sometimes chooses to end a pregnancy because they don’t want to have a baby. It is sometimes called ‘termination’.

Your first contact with an abortion service may be by phone. Depending on how many weeks pregnant you are, or how sure you are of that, you may have a telephone consultation or be asked to attend a clinic appointment. 

If you attend a clinic you may be offered a finger prick blood test, STI tests and an ultrasound scan which can help the doctors know exactly how far along the pregnancy is, so they know the best abortion procedure to use. 

If they need to give you a vaginal examination or use a vaginal ultrasound and you’ve not had one before, don’t worry – just let the nurse or doctor know, and they should tell you what to expect, and will be extra careful with you. 

You may have a chance to talk about different types of contraception with a doctor or nurse, so that you can choose a method which works for you. 

Finally, you will be asked to provide formal consent, to agree that you understand what is about to happen. You can still change your mind about having an abortion even after you have signed the form – just let the clinic or hospital staff know as soon as you do.

## How and where an abortion takes place
How and where an abortion takes place will depend on the type of abortion and how many weeks pregnant you are.

*   **A medical abortion at home** An early medical abortion will take place at home or at another place you feel comfortable. After taking the first part of the medication mifepristone, you can carry on as normal going to school or work. When you take the second part of the medication, misoprostol, 24-48 hours later, the abortion will begin to take place.
*   **Abortion at a clinic** If you are going to a clinic for an abortion make sure you bring all your documents with you, like any letters from your GP or the sexual health clinic. If you take any other types of medicine, for example if you have an inhaler for asthma or if you carry an EpiPen for allergies, make sure to take this with you, too. 

You can take one person along for support, who can sit with you in the waiting room, and meet you afterwards. This can be anyone you like – a parent, relative or friend. If you are having a general anaesthetic you will be asked to bring an adult to go home with to ensure you are looked after.

If you’re having a medical abortion, which usually happens if you’re over 10 weeks pregnant, the abortion will usually take place in one day, with no need to stay overnight. 

For some types of surgical abortions, you may have to fast (not eat) on the day – the abortion provider will make sure you know what to do. It’s a good idea to take some sanitary towels with you, along with a clean pair of underwear. 

You might experience bleeding – like a heavier than usual period – after an abortion, so it’s best to be prepared.

Sadly, some people who are against abortion protest outside clinics, and try and tell people going in to clinics that abortion is wrong and they shouldn’t have one. This can be really upsetting. Taking someone with you can help. If you’re worried, you can call the clinic and ask for an escort inside, or see if there is another entrance you can use.

## How you might feel physically
Immediately after an abortion, you might feel a bit woozy, or wobbly. If you can, it’s good to be able to have some rest, and some time to relax and recover. If you are in any discomfort, you can take painkillers like aspirin or paracetamol – you can ask at the clinic about what you can take. 

Medical abortion will result in bleeding. Some bleeding is normal after any kind of abortion, but if the bleeding hasn’t stopped after two weeks, you should go and see a doctor or contact the clinic where you were treated. 

If the pain becomes really bad, and/or if you are bleeding very heavily, and/or if you have a high temperature you should also go to a doctor as soon as you can or call NHS 111. 

Please use sanitary towels rather than tampons if there is any bleeding, and wait until your next period to use tampons again. It’s also a good idea to not have penetrative sex until you feel recovered from the abortion.

**You can get pregnant immediately after an abortion**
If you don’t want to get pregnant, it’s important to use contraception. Talk to a sexual health clinic, to find out which type of contraception works best for you.

## How you might feel emotionally
The majority of people who have abortions say that the main emotion they feel afterwards is relief. That’s not true in all cases, and if you feel sad or upset you can talk to a counsellor. 

Watch out for crisis pregnancy centres, which sometimes offer to help with mixed feelings but may actually make people feel guilty after having an abortion. How you feel will depend on who you are and what your life is like, and everyone will have a different experience. 

In most people’s cases, any feelings of sadness will fade in time, and many studies have found that abortion has no long lasting effect on mental health. If you find you are still really sad, though, please see a doctor or a counsellor, as it could be depression or another mental health issue which is affecting you.

## If you would like more help or advice about abortion you can:
*   **British Pregnancy Advisory Service (BPAS)** Call them on 03457 30 40 30.
*   **MSI Reproductive Choices UK** Call them on 0345 300 8090.
*   **National Unplanned Pregnancy Advisory Service (NUPAS)** Call them on 0333 004 6666.
*   **Abortion Talk** Abortion Talk is a free, confidential abortion and non-judgmental talkline where you can discuss your decision to have an abortion or your experience of having an abortion.

## If you need help quickly you can contact these organisations:
*   **NHS** For medical advice contact NHS by dialling 111 24 hours a day, 365 days a year.
*   **Childline** For help if you’re under 18 contact Childline on 0800 1111.
*   **Samaritans** For urgent emotional support contact the Samaritans on 08457 90 90 90.
