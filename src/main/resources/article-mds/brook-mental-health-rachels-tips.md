# Supporting a partner with their mental health: Rachel’s tips

Rachel, 21, shares her tips on how to support a partner who has mental health issues.

**Content Warning:** This page contains discussion of mental illness.

It is important to remember that even if you do not have diagnosed mental health problems such as anxiety or depression, everyone can experience poor mental health days. This is a normal part of the human experience.

## Challenges in Relationships
Being in a relationship with someone who suffers from a mental health problem can sometimes be a struggle. Common challenges include:
* Your partner appearing distant or blocking you out.
* Difficulties with communication.
* Decreased frequency of sexual intimacy.
* Feelings of frustration for the supporting partner.

## Tips for Supporting Your Partner

### Practice Patience and Kindness
For those with anxiety-related conditions, such as hypochondria (health-related anxiety), constant reassurance is often needed. While this can feel draining or annoying for a partner, the best way to support them is with kindness.
* Do not make them feel silly or like a nuisance for seeking reassurance.
* Listen to their worries calmly.
* Avoid negative or dismissive comments such as "you’re overreacting," "just stop worrying," or "cheer up," as these can make the situation worse.
* A calm listener and physical comfort, like a warm hug, can be very effective.

### Navigate Sexual Intimacy with Care
If a partner is experiencing poor mental health, sexual intimacy is often not a priority for them.
* Do not push or pressure your partner into sexual activity.
* Respect their need for space or alone time while they manage their mental health.
* Avoid making your partner feel guilty for not being "in the mood."
* Remember that the best sexual intimacy occurs when both partners are fully enthusiastic and comfortable.

### Support Without Trying to "Fix"
There is a fine line between supporting someone and trying to fix their problems for them.
* **Be encouraging:** If your partner suggests seeking professional help, such as counselling, offer your support for that decision.
* **Avoid over-analysing:** Do not attempt to diagnose your partner or tell them why you think they are struggling. 
* **Remain non-judgemental:** Staying open and willing to listen is the most effective way to be fully supportive.

## It’s OK Not to be OK
Approximately 1 in 4 people in the UK will experience a mental health problem each year (NHS England, 2020). If you are worried about your own mental health or someone else's, there are many resources available for help and support. You do not need a formal diagnosis to reach out for help; whenever you are ready to talk, support is available.
