# Menopause: What Is It and What Are the Symptoms?

Although menopause and perimenopause are experienced by half the population, it affects each person differently. Menopause occurs for all women and people with uteruses when periods (monthly bleeding) stop due to declining hormone levels. It typically happens between the ages of 44 and 56, though some experience early menopause due to genetics, surgery (such as the removal of ovaries), or medication (for conditions like endometriosis).

## Understanding the Timeline

"Menopause" is often used as an umbrella term, but it specifically refers to a single point in time. 
* **Perimenopause:** The time leading up to the menopause.
* **Menopause:** The specific point reached after one year of having no periods (if over 50) or two years after having no periods (if under 50). 
* **Postmenopausal:** The phase of life following that milestone.

## The Three Stages of Menopause

The transition is characterized by changing levels of the hormones oestrogen and progesterone.

### 1. Perimenopause
Perimenopause can start up to 10 years before the menopause itself, beginning on average around age 46. During this time, hormone levels follow less of a pattern and become unpredictable. While 20% of people do not experience symptoms, many find this a challenging time due to changes in their body, mood, and relationships.

### 2. Menopause
This is the transition point from perimenopausal to postmenopausal. It is officially reached once periods have ceased for the required duration (12 or 24 months depending on age).

### 3. Postmenopause
This stage lasts for the rest of a person's life. While hormone levels are much lower, they are more consistent, leading many to feel a sense of stability and confidence.

## Perimenopause: Changes and Symptoms

During perimenopause, rising and falling hormone levels cause various physiological changes:

* **Changes to Ovulation and Fertility:** Ovulation may not occur every month, and egg quality declines, reducing the ability to get pregnant.
* **Irregular Cycles:** Periods may initially become more frequent as the body attempts to maximize pregnancy chances, followed by less frequent cycles as egg counts decline.
* **Oestrogen Dominance:** Progesterone often falls before oestrogen. Progesterone has a calming effect, so its decline can lead to:
 * **Body changes:** Heavier/longer periods, PMS, tender breasts, bloating, and insomnia.
 * **Mood changes:** Mood swings, anxiety, depression, and tiredness.

## Declining Oestrogen Levels

As oestrogen levels eventually drop, further symptoms may occur:

* **Vaginal Dryness:** Reduced natural lubrication can make daily activities (sitting, exercising) or sex uncomfortable or painful.
* **Lower Sex Drive:** Lower oestrogen levels often result in a decrease in sexual desire.
* **Overactive Bladder:** Thinning of the urethral lining and weakened pelvic floor muscles can lead to urinary incontinence, nocturia (waking up to pee), and stress incontinence (leaking when coughing or sneezing).
* **Hot Flushes and Night Sweats:** These are the most common symptoms and involve sudden feelings of heat and flushing on the face and chest.
* **Brain Fog:** Difficulty remembering things or feeling "lost for words."

## Changing Relationships and Self-Perception

Hormones significantly impact mood and how we relate to others. Lower oestrogen can cause individuals to prioritize themselves more, which may shift relationship dynamics. Changes in sexual desire or physical comfort can also affect intimacy with partners. Additionally, changing hormone levels may impact self-esteem and confidence.

## Chemical Menopause

While most people experience menopause naturally, some undergo "chemical menopause." This is triggered by medication to treat conditions like endometriosis. Chemical menopause can be temporary (periods return after medication stops) or long-term/permanent, depending on the treatment.

## Life After Menopause

During the postmenopausal stage, the body produces hormones in small, consistent amounts. While this brings emotional stability for many, certain risks persist due to low oestrogen:
* Continued low sex drive and vaginal dryness.
* Increased risk of osteoporosis (weakened bones).
* Increased risk of heart disease.

Many people continue Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT) during this stage and focus on a healthy lifestyle, including a balanced diet and regular exercise, to manage long-term health risks.
