# The Menstrual Cycle and Pregnancy

## Understanding the Menstrual Cycle

The menstrual cycle refers to the series of natural changes that occur in the reproductive system of women and people with a uterus, making pregnancy possible. While often associated with periods, the cycle involves much more.

* **Duration:** Typically lasts around 28 days, but can range from 21 to 35 days. The length can vary for individuals.
* **Hormonal Control:** The cycle is regulated by hormones, and fluctuations in hormone levels can influence mood, energy, feelings, and libido.
* **Etymology:** The word "menstruation" originates from the Latin word 'mensis' (month) and the Greek word 'mene' (moon), reflecting an ancient belief in a connection to lunar cycles.

## The Reproductive System

Understanding the menstrual cycle requires knowledge of the reproductive system, which comprises sex characteristics like genitals, sex hormones, and internal organs that work together for reproduction.

* **Vulva:** The external female genitalia, including the labia, mons pubis, and clitoris.
* **Uterus (Womb):** The organ where a baby develops during pregnancy. It's typically the size of a fist and expands during gestation.
* **Cervix:** The entrance to the uterus, located at the top of the vagina. Its position and texture can change throughout the menstrual cycle.
* **Vagina:** A muscular tube connecting the cervix to the vulva, through which menstrual blood is released.
* **Fallopian Tubes:** Tubes connecting the ovaries to the uterus, where an egg travels and fertilization can occur.
* **Ovaries:** Almond-shaped glands that produce hormones and store eggs, releasing one during ovulation. A person is born with a lifetime supply of eggs, typically 1-2 million.

**Intersex:** Some individuals may have reproductive systems that do not include all of these parts, which is known as being intersex.

## Phases of the Menstrual Cycle

The menstrual cycle has four main phases:

### 1. The Menstrual Phase (Period Bleeding)

* **Days:** Approximately 1-5.
* **Hormones:** Low levels of oestrogen and progesterone, with rising follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH).
* **Discharge/Vaginal Mucus:** May be slightly brown or pink at the beginning and end.
* **Behavior:** Energy levels are at their lowest, often leading to a desire for rest and less social interaction.
* **Activities:** Recommended activities include rest, reflection, spending time alone, and low-intensity exercise like walking.
* **What Happens:** This phase begins with the start of your period. If an egg from the previous cycle was not fertilized, hormone levels drop, causing the thickened uterine lining to shed through the vagina.

### 2. The Follicular Phase

* **Days:** Approximately 1-12 (overlaps with the menstrual phase).
* **Hormones:** Rising FSH, oestrogen, and testosterone.
* **Discharge/Vaginal Mucus:** Starts dry after menstruation and gradually becomes more abundant and potentially white/cloudy.
* **Behavior:** Characterized by higher energy, improved mood, increased sex drive, and better concentration.
* **Activities:** Ideal for socializing, trying new things, and high-energy workouts.
* **What Happens:** FSH stimulates the ovaries to produce follicles, which contain eggs. One follicle grows larger and leads to an increase in oestrogen. Oestrogen causes the uterine lining to thicken in preparation for a potential pregnancy.

### 3. The Ovulation Phase

* **Days:** Approximately 13-15.
* **Hormones:** Rising oestrogen, testosterone, and luteinizing hormone (LH).
* **Discharge/Vaginal Mucus:** Becomes stretchy, slippery, and resembles raw egg whites in the days leading up to and during ovulation.
* **Behavior:** High energy, improved mood, increased sex drive, and greater confidence.
* **Activities:** Suitable for socializing, trying new activities, meeting people, and high-intensity workouts.
* **What Happens:** Around day 14, a surge in LH triggers the release of a mature egg from the ovary. This egg travels down the fallopian tube, where fertilization by sperm can occur. This is the most fertile time in the cycle.

### 4. The Luteal Phase

* **Days:** Approximately 16-28.
* **Hormones:** Declining oestrogen and testosterone, with rising progesterone.
* **Discharge/Vaginal Mucus:** Less discharge, with a dry or tacky texture.
* **Behavior:** May involve PMS symptoms, declining energy levels, and mood swings.
* **Activities:** Recommended to slow down, engage in lower-intensity exercise, and prioritize self-care.
* **What Happens:** If the egg is not fertilized, it will be shed with the next period. Oestrogen and progesterone levels fall, often leading to PMS symptoms such as bloating, breast tenderness, mood changes, and fatigue. Progesterone prepares the uterus for pregnancy; if pregnancy doesn't occur, progesterone levels drop.

## Pregnancy

Pregnancy occurs when a sperm fertilizes an egg in the fallopian tube.

* **Conception:** Can happen up to five days after intercourse, as sperm can survive in the body for several days. Pregnancy is only possible if having unprotected sex in the days leading up to or during ovulation due to the egg's 48-hour lifespan.
* **Implantation:** The fertilized egg travels to the uterus and attaches to the uterine lining, a process called implantation. This can sometimes cause light implantation bleeding.
* **hCG Hormone:** The body releases human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG), which pregnancy tests detect. This hormone maintains the uterine lining.
* **Missed Period:** A missed period is often the first sign of pregnancy.

## Menopause and Perimenopause

The menstrual cycle continues throughout life until menopause, which typically occurs between ages 45-55, due to declining hormone levels. The period before menopause, characterized by hormonal changes, is called perimenopause and can involve symptoms like hot flushes, mood swings, and irregular periods.

## Can I Get Pregnant on My Period?

While less likely, it is possible to get pregnant if you have unprotected sex during your period, especially if you have a shorter cycle where ovulation occurs soon after menstruation. Sperm can survive for several days.

## When to See a Doctor

It's important to consult a GP if you experience:

* Unusual discharge, spotting, or pain.
* Period cramps that significantly affect daily tasks or require painkillers.
* Heavy or light menstrual flow.
* Periods consistently shorter or longer than 2-7 days.
* Intermittent spotting during your period.
* Irregular periods (unless within the first year or two of starting menstruation or approaching menopause).

## Tracking Your Cycle

Understanding your menstrual cycle can help you:

* Prepare for your period and manage energy levels.
* Plan social events and exercise accordingly.
* Identify any changes that may require medical attention.
* Easier spot early pregnancy signs.

Methods for tracking include:

* **Period tracking:** Marking the start and end dates of your period.
* **Basal body temperature monitoring:** Taking your temperature first thing in the morning.
* **Discharge monitoring:** Observing the quantity and consistency of vaginal discharge.
* **Cervical texture:** Feeling the cervix for changes in hardness and openness.

## How to Know if You're Pregnant

* **Missed Period:** The most common indicator.
* **Other Symptoms:** Tiredness, nausea, breast tenderness, frequent urination, light spotting, or bloating.
* **Pregnancy Test:** Detects hCG in urine. It's recommended to wait 21 days after unprotected sex or a missed period for accurate results.

## Irregular Periods

Irregular cycles are normal at the beginning of menstruation and during perimenopause. Other causes can include:

* Changes in routine or stress.
* Contraception use.
* Hormone imbalances.
* Weight changes.
* Early stages of pregnancy.
* Certain medical conditions (e.g., Polycystic Ovary Syndrome - PCOS).

A GP can help determine the cause and offer solutions.

## Supporting a Partner

To support a partner experiencing changes related to their menstrual cycle:

* Have open conversations about their experiences.
* Offer support during times they may need it.
* Show awareness of where they are in their cycle.
