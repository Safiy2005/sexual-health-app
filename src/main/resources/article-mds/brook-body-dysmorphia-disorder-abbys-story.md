# Body Dysmorphia Disorder: Abby’s Story

Abby, 21, shares her experience of living with Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD), navigating puberty, and managing the feelings of being the "odd-one-out" while growing up.

## Content Warning
This story contains discussions regarding:
* Body Dysmorphia Disorder (BDD)
* Unhealthy weight loss methods
* Racial identity struggles
* Self-esteem issues

## Understanding Body Dysmorphia Disorder (BDD)
For those living with BDD, the perception of self is often warped. This means an individual does not see themselves as others do. For Abby, this manifested in several ways:
* **Fluctuating Self-Image:** Her perception of her body changed daily based on selfies, group photos, or looking in the mirror.
* **Uncertainty:** Finding it difficult to know what she truly looked like.
* **Unhealthy Habits:** Engaging in short-term, unsustainable weight loss methods in an attempt to change her appearance.

## The Impact of Environment and Racial Identity
Abby’s struggles with BDD were deeply connected to her upbringing in a predominantly white area. Being surrounded by a specific beauty standard—skinny, blonde, and white—led to a feeling of being "not normal."

This disconnect between her own reflection and the societal "ideal" she saw around her led to:
* A lack of understanding regarding her place in society.
* Dangerous attempts to drastically change her skin color.
* The internalizing of being "different" in terms of skin, weight, and hair.

## Challenges During Puberty
Puberty can be a particularly difficult time for those struggling with body image. Abby experienced her first period at age ten, which led to a rapid bodily transformation that she didn't fully understand.

As a cis-woman, she notes that:
* Puberty brings significant physical pain and bodily changes that are often not discussed enough.
* Social labels (such as being known as the "chubby" or "spotty" kid) can severely impact mental health.
* The pressure to fit a certain mold is heightened during these developmental years.

## The Power of Shared Experience
A significant turning point for Abby was leaving school and speaking openly with her peers. She discovered that many people she admired were actually experiencing the same insecurities. 

Key takeaways from her journey include:
* **You are not alone:** Realizing that others feel the same way can reduce the sense of isolation.
* **Communication is vital:** Talking about feelings and troubles with trusted people is a crucial step in seeking help.
* **Community support:** Understanding that many people are in the same position allows for mutual support.

## Current Mindset and Recovery
While Abby still lives with BDD, it is no longer the primary focus of her life. Her priorities have shifted from aesthetic weight loss to overall fitness and health. 

Her advice to others is to remember that you are your own person and do not need to fit into a specific mold. Recovery is a journey, and while it takes time, it is possible to reach a place of greater positivity and clarity.
