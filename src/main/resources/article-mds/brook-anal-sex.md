# Anal Sex: Risks, Staying Safe & How To Prepare

Anal sex is any type of sexual activity involving the anus. Like with all sexual activity, some people enjoy anal sex and some don’t. It’s important to remember that sex isn’t about ticking off things you’ve done or trying to impress other people.

This page will help you understand the reality of anal sex, including potential risks and how to stay safe.

Anal sex can be enjoyable as the anus and the rectum have sensitive nerve endings. Men and people with penises also have the prostate gland which can be pleasurable when stimulated.

It’s perfectly normal to try anal sex and decide you don’t like it, or to just know it’s not something you want to try. Everyone’s sexual experiences and turn-ons are unique. Many people, including a significant percentage of gay and bisexual men and men who have sex with men, do not have anal sex and prefer other types of sex. Anal sex may just be one part of your sexual expression; it does not have to define it.

## What is Anal Sex?

Anal sex can include:

* Penetration, including inserting a sex toy, penis, or fingers into the anus.

## Step 1: Consent

You should never pressure anyone, or feel pressured into doing anything you or they don’t want to do. Anal sex, like all types of sex, should be discussed and agreed upon beforehand, even if someone has consented to another type of sex.

Consent is a continuous action, not a one-time thing. Trying a new type of sex without talking about it first is wrong as there is no consent involved.

If you’re going to try anal sex, it’s really important that it’s with someone you can trust and communicate well with. Safe anal sex requires preparation, patience, and communication, especially as the muscles around the anus can take some time to relax. You need to feel comfortable and trust the person you’re with – if you feel anxious, these muscles will be harder to relax, and you are more likely to have an uncomfortable or painful experience.

**Note:** Sex shouldn’t be painful. If you’re relaxed and comfortable but experience pain, you should visit a sexual health clinic or your GP.

It’s a great idea to talk beforehand about any worries and expectations, and to discuss things such as speed and depth if you are considering trying penetration. Communication shouldn’t stop there – during sex, you should regularly check in with your partner, including before and after you try anything new. This will help you understand what you both like, dislike, would like to try, and want to do differently. You might also find it useful to have a more in-depth conversation after sex.

Consent can also be withdrawn at any point. If either of you want to stop at any point, that’s okay – you should stop immediately and check in with each other.

## Step 2: STIs and Viruses

### Wash

For all types of anal sex, it is a good idea to gently wash the area first with water and gentle soap. Some infections caused by bacteria and viruses can be passed on through oral to anal sex, for example, Shigella, Hepatitis A, Salmonella, or E. coli.

If you want to have vaginal sex as well, make sure to wash your hands if you have touched the anus and use a new condom. This is to prevent transferring bacteria from the anus to the vagina, which could cause a urinary infection. You could use gloves to touch the anus as a precaution.

### Condoms

When you have anal sex involving a penis or sex toy, it’s really important to use a condom. The lining of the anus and rectum is delicate and can be damaged easily, making it easier than other types of sex for STIs to be transmitted. This thin lining is designed to absorb fluids and nutrients from your food and, in a similar way, it can absorb bodily fluids, including pre-cum, sperm, and any potential STIs. Condoms stop this transfer of bodily fluids and are also great to use if you’re worried about hygiene or any potential mess.

### Lube

Unlike the vagina, the anus isn’t self-lubricating, so to make it more comfortable for both partners and prevent tearing (which can increase the risk of STIs), it’s important to use lots of lube. Most condoms can only be used with water-based or silicone-based lube – avoid oil-based products as they can damage condoms and leave you unprotected.

### More Ways to Protect Against STIs

Aside from using condoms, you can also protect against STIs during anal sex by:

* Getting tested for STIs regularly and each time you have sex with a new partner.
* Never engaging in oral sex if there are open sores or lesions on the mouth, anus, or genitals.
* Communicating honestly and openly with your partner(s) about the potential risk of STIs.
* If you’re using sex toys, avoid sharing them. If you do share, clean them thoroughly in between being used by different people.

**Douching can be damaging as:**

* It washes away the healthy bacteria and protective mucus in the anus – harmful bacteria can grow in its place, which can lead to sickness.

* Avoid using douches with chemicals that can irritate the rectal lining.
* Use infrequently (no more than 2 or 3 times a week).
* Only use water (water itself will wash away bacteria – the good and the bad).
* Make sure the water is at body temperature.
* If using a shower douche, use one that allows you to adjust the pressure, or else it could be too strong.
* Thoroughly clean the douche before and after use.
* Use a lubed finger to relax your anus before using the douche.

* Don’t share douches – you can pass on STIs and bacteria, leading to infections.

**When to see a doctor:**

If the bleeding doesn’t stop, is a very dark colour, you are in a lot of pain or discomfort, or you are worried, then you should seek medical advice. This could be from a Brook service, another sexual health service, GP, or hospital.
