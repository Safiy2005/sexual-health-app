# Looking for ‘The One’: Em’s Story

Em, 22, shares their experience of how the consumption of romance-based films and television from an early age fostered an unhelpful obsession with finding "The One." By learning to decouple their self-worth from romantic ideals, Em has significantly improved their relationship with themselves and others.

**Content warning:** This article contains discussions of mental health challenges and abusive behavior.

## The Influence of Romantic Media

Growing up, many individuals are immersed in romanticized media, such as rom-coms and love songs. For Em, this obsession with the romance genre had unforeseen consequences:

* **Heteronormative bias:** Most romantic stories were presented within heterosexual and gender-binary boundaries, which Em later identified as problematic.
* **A "sole purpose" narrative:** Media consumption convinced Em that their primary goal in life was to find a soulmate, feeling as though they were the star of their own romantic film.
* **Unrealistic expectations:** The belief that meeting "The One" would result in an immediate, destined, lifelong connection led to a rush into dating at a young age.

## Cycles of Relationship Addiction

Starting at age 13, Em entered a repetitive cycle of dating characterized by:

* **Intense beginnings:** Relationships would become intense very quickly.
* **Rapid termination:** As soon as a partner was deemed not to be "The One," the relationship was ended.
* **Addictive patterns:** Being in a relationship became a cycle that was difficult to break, driven by the desire for the "rom-com" experience.

## Sexuality and Heteronormativity

During their youth, Em identified as bisexual but exclusively dated men. This was largely due to the pressure of heteronormative love stories. Em felt that "The One" could only exist within a straight-passing relationship, leading them to:

* Repress their queerness.
* Avoid exploring their true identity.
* Prioritize modeling their life after media tropes rather than personal authenticity.

## Mental Health and Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD)

At age 17, Em was diagnosed with Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD). This diagnosis provided clarity on several behaviors:

* **Emotional volatility:** Understanding why feelings shifted from 100% to 0% very rapidly.
* **Validation seeking:** Recognizing when a craving for a relationship was actually a need for external validation driven by BPD.
* **Pattern recognition:** Identifying the root causes of unhealthy relationship cycles.

## Recognizing Abuse and Red Flags

Despite their diagnosis, Em entered a toxic relationship with an individual they believed was "The One." This experience highlighted several "red flags" and abusive behaviors, including:

* Comparison to ex-partners.
* Coercive behavior regarding sexual activity.
* Mockery of clothing, hair, and educational choices.
* The normalization of pain as a component of "love."

It took significant time after the relationship ended for Em to recognize these behaviors as abusive rather than romantic intensity.

## Recovery and Self-Relationship

Em’s journey involved "falling out of love with love"—deconstructing the idealized versions of romance learned in childhood. Key takeaways from their recovery include:

* **Prioritizing the self:** Focusing on personal wants and needs rather than catering to others.
* **Communication:** Learning that healthy relationships are built on a foundation of good communication rather than cinematic drama.
* **Internal validation:** Distinguishing between genuine connection and the impulse to repeat old patterns.
* **Self-love:** Recognizing that the relationship with oneself is the most important foundation for mental wellbeing.

## Mental Health Support

Approximately 1 in 4 people in the UK experience a mental health problem each year. If you are concerned about your mental health or the wellbeing of someone else, support is available. You do not need a formal diagnosis to seek help; you can reach out to support services whenever you are ready to talk.
