# Vaginas and Vulvas: Getting to Know Your Body

Like every other part of your body, every vagina and vulva is different. While worrying about what is "normal" is common, it is helpful to understand the basics of how the vagina and vulva actually work.

## Understanding the Vagina and Vulva

We often refer to the external genital organs (the parts you can see) as the vagina, but this area is actually called the **vulva**. The **vagina** is the muscular tube inside the body that leads from the cervix (the opening of the womb) to the vulva.

The main parts of the vulva include:
* **Labia**: You have two sets of labia. The outer labia are usually fleshier and covered in pubic hair. The inner labia do not have pubic hair. Labia size varies significantly from person to person.
* **Mons**: The area above the labia where pubic hair grows.
* **Clitoris**: A small, pea-shaped bump located at the top of the labia, covered by a hood of skin. Its sole purpose is to provide sexual pleasure. It is packed with nerves and is extremely sensitive to touch.
* **Urethra**: A small dot or slit located underneath the clitoris; this is where urine leaves the body.
* **Vaginal opening**: Located below the urethra, this is where menstrual blood exits the body and where tampons, cups, or objects for sexual penetration are inserted.

## Sexual Arousal and Orgasm

When you become aroused, blood flow to the vaginal walls increases, causing the vagina to become wet through lubrication. The external genitals may swell, and the internal vagina expands. 

As you get closer to orgasm, the clitoris pulls back against the pubic bone. During orgasm, sexual tension is released in a short burst, genital muscles contract, and the brain releases feel-good chemicals.

## Pain During Sex

Penetrative sex should not hurt. If you find penetration painful, it could be due to:
* Insufficient lubrication
* Sexual position
* Emotional factors
* An underlying infection

You should not have to put up with pain during sex. If you are concerned, speak to a doctor or nurse.

## The Hymen (Corona)

The hymen—sometimes called a corona—is a ring of thin skin that covers part of the opening of the vagina. It does not cover the opening completely, allowing menstrual blood to pass through. 
* Everyone’s hymen is different, and its biological purpose is unknown. 
* It can stretch or tear during the first time having sex, which may cause minor bleeding, but this does not always happen. 
* The hymen can also stretch through physical activities, sports, or the use of tampons.

## Care and Hygiene

To maintain a healthy balance, you should follow these hygiene tips:
* **Wash the vulva gently**: Use mild, unperfumed soap every day.
* **Avoid douching**: The vagina cleans itself internally through secretions. Washing inside the vagina (douching) removes helpful bacteria and disrupts the pH balance, which can lead to infections like thrush or bacterial vaginosis (BV).
* **Avoid scented products**: Scented wipes and vaginal deodorants can disrupt the healthy bacterial balance.

## When to Seek Help

If you notice any of the following, it may be a sign of an infection:
* Itching or soreness in the vaginal area
* Changes to your discharge
* A noticeable change in smell

If you have had sex without a condom, you should consider getting an STI test or seeking emergency contraception if necessary.

## Contraception and Screening

If you are sexually active, there are several contraceptive methods that are inserted into the vagina, including:
* Internal condoms
* The vaginal ring
* IUD (Intrauterine Device) or IUS (Intrauterine System)

### Cervical Screening
Cervical screening (a smear test) is a health test that involves taking a small sample of cells from the cervix to detect early changes or unhealthy cells. 
* Routine screening usually begins at age 25.
* A doctor or nurse uses an instrument called a speculum to open the vagina to see the cervix.
* While some find it uncomfortable or embarrassing, it is generally not painful.
