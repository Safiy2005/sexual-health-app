# Syphilis: Symptoms, Tests and Treatment

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by bacteria (*Treponema pallidum*). While it is less common than other STIs, cases are currently on the rise. Syphilis develops in three distinct stages, but it can be cured if treated early.

The symptoms of syphilis are often mild, which may lead some people to ignore them. The symptoms are similar for all genders.

## Stages of Syphilis

### Stage One: Primary Syphilis
The first symptom usually appears two to four weeks after exposure, though it can appear sooner or later.
* **The Chancre:** A small, painless sore called a chancre (pronounced 'shanker'). 
* **Location:** It typically appears where the infection entered the body, such as the vagina, penis, or anus. It can also appear on the lips, tonsils, hands, or buttocks.
* **Duration:** The sore is highly infectious but normally heals and disappears after two to six weeks. 

**Warning:** If left untreated, the bacteria can spread to other parts of the body, and the infection moves into the second stage.

### Stage Two: Secondary Syphilis
This stage begins a few weeks after the chancre has disappeared. Syphilis remains highly infectious during this stage. Symptoms may include:
* A non-itchy rash, often on the palms of the hands or soles of the feet.
* Small flat growths (similar to warts) around the vagina or anus.
* Flu-like symptoms (headaches, fever, joint pains, tiredness).
* Swollen glands in the neck, armpits, or groin.
* Weight loss.
* White patches on the tongue or roof of the mouth.
* Patchy hair loss.

These symptoms may disappear after a few weeks or fluctuate over months. When symptoms vanish, the infection enters the **latent (hidden) phase**, which can last for years.

### Stage Three: Tertiary Syphilis
It is rare to reach this stage in the UK; of those who do, only about a third experience serious symptoms. This stage begins years or decades after the initial infection. It can cause:
* Skin rashes and numbness.
* Paralysis and loss of coordination.
* Blindness and deafness.
* Stroke and dementia.
* Heart disease.
* Death.

## How Syphilis is Passed On
Syphilis is easily passed from one person to another through:
* Unprotected vaginal, anal, or oral sex.
* Sharing sex toys without washing them or covering them with a new condom.
* Sharing needles (for injecting drug users).
* Mother to unborn baby (can be treated during pregnancy; if untreated, it may cause miscarriage or stillbirth).

**Note:** You cannot get syphilis from kissing, hugging, sharing baths, towels, swimming pools, toilet seats, or sharing cutlery/plates.

## Testing for Syphilis
If you have had unprotected sex, you should get tested as soon as possible. Testing is free at Brook services, GUM or sexual health clinics, and some GP surgeries.

* **Examination:** A doctor or nurse will look for physical signs, including rashes or sores. This may involve an internal examination of the vagina or anus.
* **Swab Test:** If a chancre is present, a swab is used to collect fluid for lab testing. Results usually take 7 to 10 days.
* **Blood Test:** This looks for antibodies produced by the immune system. 
 * **Window Period:** It can take up to three months for antibodies to become detectable. You may be advised to repeat the test after three months to be certain of the result.
* **Home Testing:** Self-test kits are available for purchase, and free kits are available in some areas of England via specific providers.

**Note:** Cervical smear tests and routine blood tests do not detect syphilis.

## Treatment
Syphilis is typically treated with a single antibiotic injection or a course of injections (usually penicillin). If you are allergic to penicillin, alternative antibiotic tablets will be prescribed.

* **Effectiveness:** Treatment is very effective for first and second-stage syphilis. 
* **Late Stages:** Syphilis can still be cured in the latent or tertiary stages, but treatment cannot reverse permanent damage already done to the body.
* **Pregnancy:** If you are pregnant or breastfeeding, inform your healthcare provider so they can prescribe the appropriate antibiotic.
* **Medication Interactions:** Some antibiotics may interfere with hormonal contraception.

### After Treatment
* Avoid all sexual contact (including skin-to-skin contact) until you are given the all-clear.
* Inform any sexual partners from the last six months so they can be tested and treated.
* Treatment does not make you immune; you can catch syphilis again.

## Partner Notification
It is vital to tell recent sexual partners so they can get treatment. Some clinics offer a "contact slip" service where they notify your partners anonymously without mentioning your name.
