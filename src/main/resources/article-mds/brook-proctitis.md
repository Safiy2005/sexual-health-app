# Proctitis: Symptoms, Tests and Treatments

Proctitis is the soreness and swelling (inflammation) of the rectum, which is the passage that carries faeces (poo) out of the body. While proctitis itself is not a sexually transmitted infection (STI), it is frequently caused by STIs. It can occur in anyone but is more common in individuals who engage in anal sex.

## Symptoms

Proctitis symptoms may be temporary or longer-term. Common symptoms include:

* Pain during a bowel movement
* Soreness in the anal area
* A feeling that you haven’t completely emptied your bowels
* Spasms or cramping during bowel movements

## Causes

There are many different causes of proctitis. Sexually transmitted infections are the most common cause, including:

* Gonorrhoea
* Syphilis
* Herpes
* Genital warts
* Chlamydia

Other non-STI causes may include:

* Crohn’s disease and ulcerative colitis
* Foreign objects inserted into the rectum
* Trauma to the rectum
* Side effects of taking certain antibiotics

## Diagnosis

The process for diagnosing proctitis depends on the suspected cause. If the condition is not caused by an STI, you may require further testing with a specialist. Diagnostic procedures may include:

* Inserting a camera into the rectum to examine the surface.
* Taking a small tissue sample (biopsy) from the rectum.
* Taking a sample of discharge for testing.

Testing and treatment are available at GP surgeries, GUM (genitourinary medicine) clinics, or sexual health clinics. While Brook services do not offer direct testing or treatment for proctitis, they can provide advice on where to find appropriate care.

## Treatment

Because proctitis is generally caused by STIs, treatment typically focuses on clearing the underlying infection. 

Even if the case is not caused by an STI, it is possible to pass inflammation or infection on during sex. Therefore, it is recommended that you abstain from any sexual activity until the condition has completely cleared up.

## Informing Partners

If you are being treated for an STI, it is important to inform your current and recent sexual partners (from the last six months) so they can also be tested and treated. 

Many clinics offer a "contact slip" service. This allows the clinic to contact your partners anonymously to warn them of potential exposure without mentioning your name.
