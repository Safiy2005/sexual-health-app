# Experiencing Mental Illness: Chanel’s Story

Chanel shares her experience of living with mental illness, including depression, anxiety, and an eating disorder. Her story serves as a reminder that recovery is a personal journey and is rarely linear.

> **Content Warning:** This page includes discussions of mental illness and eating disorders.

## Early Struggles with Depression and Anxiety

Chanel’s experience with depression and anxiety began in early childhood. For a long time, she assumed these feelings were universal until she entered secondary school. Growing up, her anxiety was closely tied to her body image. While she felt comfortable in primary school, transitioning to an all-girls secondary school made her acutely aware of her weight, leading to feelings of isolation and self-hatred.

To cope with these feelings, Chanel often played the "class clown" to mask her internal struggle. She felt that if she could make people laugh, they wouldn't notice her insecurities or the "vicious cycle" she was trapped in: feeling depressed about her weight, eating to cope with sadness, and then becoming further depressed by subsequent weight gain.

## Sexuality and Self-Image

During her teenage years, Chanel also struggled to come to terms with her sexuality. The fear of being judged or misunderstood by others added another layer of stress to her existing struggles with weight and mental health. This period was marked by significant distress and moments where she felt she might not be able to get through the challenges she faced.

## The Journey Through an Eating Disorder

A turning point occurred after Chanel left school and started college. She found a more supportive social environment and decided to lose weight. However, the positive reinforcement and attention she received for her weight loss led to an obsession.

Chanel began starving herself and over-exercising, often spending over an hour on a treadmill without having eaten, leading to physical collapse. Following a GP visit, she was diagnosed with an eating disorder and referred to counseling. Through therapy, she began to address her unhealthy relationship with food and learned to challenge the stigmas she had developed.

## Living with Social Anxiety and Panic Attacks

While Chanel made progress with her eating disorder, she continued to face severe social anxiety, particularly in enclosed or crowded public spaces like trains, shopping malls, and concerts. After experiencing a panic attack on a busy train, she developed a persistent fear of being trapped or suffocated. This anxiety often prevents her from participating in social events with friends.

## Coping Strategies and Finding Support

Chanel has developed several practical coping mechanisms to manage her social anxiety and panic attacks:
* **Gradual Exposure:** Using trains only for short distances between stops.
* **Hydration:** Always carrying a bottle of water, which she links to managing panic.
* **Auditory Grounding:** Using headphones and music to block out overwhelming environmental noise.
* **Sensory Distraction:** Wearing and "twiddling" with rings to take her mind off anxious thoughts.

She is currently on a waiting list for therapy to specifically address her social anxiety and fear of enclosed spaces.

## Advice for Others

Chanel emphasizes the importance of seeking help and not battling mental illness alone. Her key takeaways for others include:
* **You are not alone:** Others share similar thoughts and experiences.
* **Accept help:** While it can be scary to start treatment, professional support can be transformative.
* **Communicate:** Bottling up emotions can cause more harm. Speaking to friends or family can provide significant relief.
* **Recovery takes time:** It is okay to have bad days as well as good days.

## Understanding Recovery

Recovery means different things to different people. For some, it is the absence of symptoms for a period of time. For others, like Chanel, it involves finding the right techniques and mechanisms to manage a condition so that it no longer dictates their daily life. 

Approximately 1 in 4 people in the UK experience a mental health problem each year. Support is available regardless of whether a person has a formal diagnosis.
