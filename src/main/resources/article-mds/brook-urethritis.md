# Urethritis: Symptoms, Causes, and Treatment

Urethritis is the soreness and swelling (inflammation) of the urethra, which is the tube that carries urine out of the body from the bladder. While urethritis itself is not a sexually transmitted infection (STI), it is frequently caused by STIs.

Urethritis can occur in anyone. It is one of the most common conditions diagnosed and treated in people with a penis at sexual health clinics in the UK.

## Symptoms of Urethritis

Symptoms may begin a few weeks after an infection occurs, depending on the underlying cause.

### Symptoms in people with a penis:
* A white or cloudy discharge from the tip of the penis
* Pain, burning, or stinging sensations when urinating
* The tip of the penis feeling irritated or sore
* A frequent need to urinate

### Symptoms in people with a vagina:
People with a vagina do not usually experience symptoms of urethritis unless the infection spreads to the womb or fallopian tubes, potentially causing pelvic inflammatory disease (PID). PID is a serious condition. Symptoms may include:
* Pain around the lower part of the stomach
* Discomfort or pain deep inside during sex
* Bleeding between periods or after sex
* Pain when urinating
* Heavy or painful periods
* Unusual vaginal discharge

If you experience any of these symptoms, it is recommended that you seek medical help immediately.

## Causes

### Infectious Causes
Urethritis is most commonly caused by an infection, including:
* **Chlamydia:** Responsible for approximately 43% of cases in men.
* **Gonorrhoea**
* **Trichomoniasis**
* **Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)**
* **Herpes simplex virus:** The virus that causes cold sores and genital herpes.
* **Adenovirus:** A virus usually associated with sore throats or eye infections.

### Non-infectious Causes
It is also possible for urethritis to be caused by physical or chemical irritation rather than infection:
* Irritation from products such as soap, deodorant, or spermicide used near the urethra.
* Damage caused by vigorous sex or masturbation.
* Squeezing or fiddling with the urethra.
* Damage from inserting objects into the urethra (such as a catheter).

## Diagnosis and Testing

Urethritis can be diagnosed through a swab or a urine test. Because they are common causes, you will likely be tested for gonorrhoea and chlamydia at the same time.

A swab test involves using a small cotton bud to wipe over the urethra. While this may be slightly uncomfortable, it should not be painful. Testing and treatment are available at GP surgeries, GUM (genitourinary medicine) clinics, or sexual health clinics.

## Treatment

Urethritis is generally treated with a short course of antibiotics to kill the bacteria causing the infection. If the condition is caused by gonorrhoea, a different treatment protocol may be used.

Although not all cases are caused by an STI, the infection can still be passed on during sex. It is recommended that you abstain from sexual activity until the condition has completely cleared up.

## Telling Your Partners

If you are being treated for an STI-related case of urethritis, it is important to inform any sexual partners from the last six months so they can be tested and treated as well. 

Many clinics offer a "contact slip" service. This allows the clinic to notify your partners anonymously, informing them they may have been exposed and recommending a test without mentioning your name.
