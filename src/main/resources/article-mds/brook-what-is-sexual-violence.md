# What is sexual violence?

Sexual violence is the general term used to describe any kind of unwanted sexual act or activity. It includes sexual assault, rape, sexual abuse, and sexual harassment.

## Types of sexual violence

Sexual violence can include:

* **Rape:** Penetration of the vagina, anus, or mouth with a penis without consent.
* **Sexual assault:** Any act of unwanted sexual contact, including rape.
* **Domestic abuse:** An abusive relationship which may involve sexual violence.
* **Sexual abuse:** Being forced into sexual activity, including online grooming and sexual exploitation.
* **Female Genital Mutilation (FGM).**
* **Sexual harassment:** Any unwanted behaviour of a sexual nature. This can include:
 * Sexual comments, jokes, or gestures
 * Intrusive staring
 * Intimate image abuse (revenge porn)
 * Indecent exposure, including flashing in person or cyberflashing (sending unwanted nudes)

## You are not to blame

If you have experienced sexual violence, whether it was recently or many years ago, you could be feeling a range of emotions such as anger, shock, fear, or depression. You might feel many things at once, or you may be in shock and feel numb.

Whatever you are feeling, you are not to blame and you are not alone.

## Legal definitions and consent

Both rape and sexual assault are crimes which involve someone being forced into sexual activity without their consent. While people often use the terms interchangeably, they are distinct in the eyes of the law.

* **Rape:** The penetration of the mouth, vagina, or anus with a penis without consent. Anyone can be a victim of rape, but legally it can only be committed by someone with a penis. 
* **Stealthing:** This is the intentional removal or damage of a condom without consent, which is a form of rape.
* **Sexual assault by penetration:** Penetration (without consent) with an object or another part of the body. This can be committed by anyone.
* **Other forms of sexual assault:** This includes forcing someone to engage in any sexual activity, including kissing, or sexual touching of any part of the body without consent (regardless of whether the person is clothed).

Sexual assault does not always cause physical injury or leave visible marks, but it can cause severe distress and emotional harm that takes a long time to recover from.

## Consent in relationships

Being in a relationship does not equal consent. You always have a right to say no to any form of sex or sexual activity—or to withdraw your consent—at any time. A relationship or marriage is not an entitlement to have sex. 

* Sexual assault is sexual assault whether or not it occurs within a marriage or relationship. 
* Rape is rape whether or not it occurs within a marriage or relationship.

## Your sexual health

### STIs
Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs) are passed through vaginal, anal, and oral sexual contact. If you have experienced rape or sexual assault, you should arrange to be tested for STIs as soon as possible. This is a medical necessity and nothing to be ashamed of.

### Pregnancy
If you think you could be pregnant as a result of rape, there are services where you can be tested, discuss your options, and seek support. Emergency contraception can be used to prevent pregnancy; some types can be taken up to 5 days after the incident.

## Your mental health and emotional wellbeing

Victims of sexual violence may experience many emotional and psychological reactions, including:

* Anxiety and panic attacks
* Depression
* Flashbacks
* Feelings of guilt and blame
* Problems sleeping and nightmares
* Potentially harmful ways of coping, such as self-harm or substance misuse

Your feelings are valid. It is common to feel like you are "overreacting" or that it was your fault, but none of these thoughts are true. You did not deserve what happened, but you do deserve support to move forward.

## Specialist support

Sexual violence is a crime, no matter who commits it or where it happens. There are professional organizations dedicated to helping survivors.

### Sexual Assault Referral Centres (SARCs)
SARCs offer medical, practical, and emotional support. They can assist with:
* Pregnancy and STI testing
* Medical help for injuries
* Forensic medical examinations

### Reporting to the police
Whether you report the incident to the police is entirely your choice. If you are unsure, you can visit a SARC without reporting it first. They can collect and store forensic evidence in case you decide to report it later.

Organizations like **Rape Crisis** provide Independent Sexual Violence Advocates (ISVAs) who can provide information about the reporting process and support you through it.
