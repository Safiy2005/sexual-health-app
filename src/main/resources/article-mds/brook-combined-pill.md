# The Combined Pill

## How it Works

The combined pill, often just called 'the pill', contains two hormones similar to those produced naturally by the body: progestogen and oestrogen. These hormones work to prevent pregnancy.

The combined pill works to prevent pregnancy in three ways:

* It stops eggs being released from the ovaries (ovulation).
* It makes it harder for sperm to reach the egg.
* It makes the uterus (womb) lining thinner, so a fertilised egg cannot implant.

Generally, you do not ovulate on the pill. The oestrogen in the pill stops your body from releasing an egg each month.

## Effectiveness

* **More than 99% effective** if used without mistakes.
* Effectiveness is impacted by vomiting, diarrhoea, and other medications.

## Pros

* Over 99% effective if taken correctly and no long-term effects on fertility.
* No interruption to sex.
* Can help make periods lighter, more regular, and reduce period pains.
* Can help reduce premenstrual symptoms (PMS).
* Certain brands can improve acne.
* Additional health benefits, such as reducing the risk of cancer of the ovaries, womb, and colon.

## Cons

* Temporary side effects during the first few months can include headaches, nausea, breast tenderness, and mood swings.
* Can increase blood pressure.
* No protection against sexually transmitted infections (STIs).
* Breakthrough bleeding and spotting (bleeding outside of the seven-day break) is common when you first start taking it.

## Types of Combined Pill

There are three main types of combined pill:

* **Monophasic 21-day pills:**
 * Each pill has the same amount of hormone.
 * You take one pill every day for 21 days, then have a seven-day break if you wish.
 * Examples: Microgynon, Brevinor, and Cilest.
* **Phasic 21-day pills:**
 * Contains two or three sections of different coloured pills in a pack, each with a different amount of hormones.
 * You take one pill every day for 21 days, then have a seven-day break before starting the next pack if you wish.
 * It is important to take phasic pills in the right order.
 * Examples: Binovum and Logynon.
* **Every Day (ED) pills:**
 * Packs contain 21 active pills and seven placebo (dummy) pills which look different.
 * This means you take 28 pills in total with no break.
 * It is important to take the pills in the right order.
 * Examples: Microgynon ED and Logynon ED.

## Starting the Combined Pill

* Requires a prescription from a doctor or nurse. As of December 2023, you may also be able to get the pill from a pharmacy without needing to see a doctor or nurse, following a consultation.
* You may have to use additional contraception for the first seven days depending on where you are in your cycle.

### When to Start for Immediate Protection:

* **First five days of your period:** Protected immediately.
* **Short menstrual cycle (23 days or less):** Start in the first four days for immediate protection.
* **Any other time in your menstrual cycle:** Use additional contraception (e.g., condoms) for the first seven pill-taking days.

*This information does not apply to the combined pill Qlaira. Contact a doctor or nurse or read manufacturer's instructions if unsure.*

### Starting After Pregnancy:

* **After giving birth:**
 * If not breastfeeding, you can likely start on day 21 after birth (check with a doctor). Use alternative contraception for the first seven days if starting after week three.
 * If breastfeeding, it is advised not to take the combined pill until six weeks after birth.
* **After an abortion or miscarriage:**
 * If starting within five days of the abortion or miscarriage, you are protected immediately.
 * Any later than day five, use additional contraception for the first seven days.

## How to Take the Combined Pill

* Choose a time of day that suits you best and take it at the same time every day until you finish the packet. This helps with remembering and establishing a habit.
* New guidelines (2019) confirm there is no harm in taking the pill continuously without a seven-day break, which means you are unlikely to have a withdrawal bleed. Speak to your doctor or nurse about what is right for you.

### Withdrawal Bleeds

* During your pill-free week, or when taking placebo pills, you may experience a bleed. This is called a 'withdrawal bleed' and is caused by not taking hormones during this week.
* This can happen at any point during the break week. Start your next pack on time whether or not you are still bleeding.
* There is no harm in taking the pill back-to-back to avoid a withdrawal bleed.

## Medical History and Suitability

* Medical or family history, as well as certain lifestyle factors, might mean the pill isn’t suitable for some people.
* You will usually be assessed by a nurse, doctor, or pharmacist before starting. An appointment typically includes:
 * Questions about your medical and family history.
 * Discussion of other medicines you are taking.
 * Blood pressure check and weighing.

## Follow-up

* You will usually be given a three-month supply when you first start.
* Follow-up appointments and reviews are then usually every 6-12 months.

## If You Miss a Pill or Other Issues

* **Missed a pill:** Find out what to do if you forgot your pill.
* **Vomiting within two hours of taking it:** Treat as a missed pill.
* **Very severe diarrhoea lasting more than 24 hours:** Treat as a missed pill.
* **Interactions with other medications:** Certain medicines (e.g., for epilepsy, HIV, TB, St John's Wort) and some antibiotics can reduce effectiveness. Always inform your doctor, nurse, or pharmacist you are taking the combined pill. Commonly used antibiotics do not usually reduce effectiveness, but it's always best to check.

Talk to a pharmacist, doctor, or nurse and use additional contraception (like condoms) until you are sure your contraception is protecting you from pregnancy.

## Stopping the Pill

* **How quickly it leaves your system:** Hormones usually leave the body within a couple of days.
* **How quickly you can get pregnant:** Varies depending on when ovulation restarts (days to months). Fertility levels should return rapidly; use condoms if you don't want to get pregnant.
* **What happens to your periods:** Talk to a doctor or nurse if you have irregular periods or a long time for them to restart.
* **Physical changes:** May be temporary as hormone levels self-regulate over weeks or months. See a doctor if symptoms persist or worsen.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Will the pill make me gain weight?

There is no evidence of weight gain with the combined pill.

### Will the pill lower my libido (sex drive)?

Different contraceptive methods affect people differently. It's possible contraception may increase or decrease libido. If you don't like the change, you can stop and try another method. For some, being on contraception increases libido due to reduced anxiety about pregnancy.

### What if I start on the pill and don’t like it?

Side effects in the first couple of months are usually short-term. It's best to trial a method for at least three months. Different brands or types of combined pill might suit you better. Talk to a medical professional to discuss your options.

### Do you ovulate on the pill?

Generally, you do not ovulate on the pill. The oestrogen in the pill stops your body from releasing an egg each month.

## Getting the Combined Pill

The combined pill is available free of charge from:

* Contraceptive clinics
* Your GP
* Brook
* Some pharmacies

Contraception and sexual health services like Brook are free and confidential, including for those under 16. Health professionals work to strict guidelines and will only share information if there's a serious risk of immediate harm.
