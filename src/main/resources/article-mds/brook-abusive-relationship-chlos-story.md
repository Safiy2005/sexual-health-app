# Abusive relationship: Chlo’s story

Chlo, 20, shares her experience of a long-term abusive relationship that began online when she was thirteen. Her story highlights the patterns of control, the complexities of consent, and the journey toward recovery and advocacy.

## Content Warning
This page contains discussions of emotional abuse, sexual assault, eating disorders, self-harm, suicidal thoughts, child sexual exploitation, and threats of sexual and physical violence.

## Early Relationship and Warning Signs
Chlo met Shaun on Twitter when she was thirteen. Initially, he was friendly and complimentary, providing a distraction from bullying at school. However, early signs of intensity were present:
* Sending eight-page documents declaring love.
* Refusing to socialize with friends to stay online with her.
* A significant age gap (Shaun was three years older).

When Chlo was hospitalized for anorexia and attempted to break up due to communication difficulties, Shaun became aggressive, using verbal abuse and claiming she had "ruined his life."

## The Cycle of Abuse
After Chlo left the hospital, the relationship continued with a pattern of "good times" and "bad times." Shaun often wore a "mask" of being polite and shy in front of parents, but his behavior changed when they were alone or communicating remotely.

### Emotional and Verbal Abuse
* **Name-calling:** Using derogatory slurs and criticizing her appearance.
* **Gaslighting:** Claiming she was no longer the "sweet girl" he met and making her "work" to figure out what she had done wrong.
* **Control over appearance:** Dictating what clothes she should wear and criticizing her body.
* **Isolation:** Becoming jealous of time spent with family or plans for education.

### Threats and Coercion
Shaun used self-harm and suicide threats to manipulate Chlo, stashing medication and telling her that if he died, it would be her fault. This created a sense of total responsibility and fear.

## Sexual Assault and Consent
Chlo experienced sexual assault during the relationship. At the time, she did not recognize it as illegal. 
* **Pressure:** Engaging in sexual acts to avoid his temper.
* **Physical Force:** Being touched painfully and non-consensually despite attempts to stop him.
* **The Reality of Consent:** Chlo highlights that if a person is being abused or controlled, they do not have the capacity to give free consent, even if they do not explicitly say "no" at the time.

## Cyberbullying and Harassment
Following their formal breakup, the abuse moved primarily online.
* **Monitoring:** Controlling who she spoke to and calling her names for interacting with other boys.
* **Anonymous Bullying:** Using platforms like Ask.fm to send thousands of abusive messages.
* **Targeting Vulnerabilities:** Using her history of anorexia to mock her weight and body.
* **Threats of Violence:** Describing graphic fantasies of physical and sexual violence.

## Involvement of the Police
Chlo eventually contacted the police via the 101 non-emergency number. Her experience highlights the inconsistencies in how domestic abuse is handled:
* **Initial Report:** Police confirmed his behavior was abuse, which helped Chlo validate her experience.
* **Evidentiary Challenges:** Barriers included the requirement to prove harassment through written "no-contact" requests and the difficulty of getting cooperation from international social media platforms.
* **The Arrest:** Shaun was eventually arrested for harassment after police traced emails to his IP address.

## Sentencing and Legal Protection
In March 2014, Shaun pleaded guilty to harassment. The judge described his behavior as "cruel, calculated abuse." The sentence included:
* An 18-week suspended prison sentence.
* Electronic tagging and a two-month curfew.
* A five-year restraining order prohibiting contact via any method or visiting her home, school, or workplace.
* Financial compensation to the victim.

## Recovery and Advocacy
Since the conviction, Chlo has focused on healing and helping others. She launched the **Speak Out Project** to raise awareness of domestic abuse among young people. Her work includes:
* Training teachers and mental health staff on teen relationship abuse.
* Teaching lessons to sixth-formers.
* Speaking at conferences and serving on the Women’s Aid Young People’s Advisory Panel.

## Advice for Young People
Chlo offers the following guidance for those who may be in a similar situation:
* **Identify Warning Signs:** Be wary of someone who tries to control your clothes, who you see, or how you spend your time.
* **Trust Your Instincts:** If something doesn't feel right, talk to a trusted adult, teacher, or counselor.
* **Recognize the Cycle:** Abusers often switch between being "lovely" and "vile." Affection does not excuse abuse.
* **Maintain Independence:** Do not let one relationship become your whole world.
* **Understand Responsibility:** Abuse is never your fault. You are not responsible for the abuser's actions or their mental health.

## Support Resources
If you are concerned about your relationship, you can contact:
* **Brook:** For confidential advice and local services.
* **National Domestic Violence Helpline:** 0808 2000 247 (Run by Women’s Aid and Refuge).
* **Victim Support:** For counseling and help through legal processes.
* **The Police:** Call 101 for non-emergencies or 999 in an emergency.
