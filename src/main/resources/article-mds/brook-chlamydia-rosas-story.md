# Chlamydia: Rosa's Story

Rosa, 23, shares her experience of contracting and being treated for chlamydia during university, and the valuable lessons she learned.

## The Journey to Diagnosis

During my third year at university, I was in an exclusive relationship, which meant our precautions with condoms became limited. When I became single again in the summer before my fourth year, I found it difficult to adjust. Many of my sexual encounters occurred after nights out, where I was often fairly drunk, struggling with the transition back to being single. Emotionally, I wasn't in a good place, leading me to be much less careful and conscientious than I had been previously. I felt I needed to drink to feel at ease having sex with someone new, which lowered my inhibitions.

Although I used a condom with most of my partners, there were a couple of exceptions. For instance, with one person I had known for a while, I felt a sense of trust. It wasn’t until the morning after that I realised we hadn’t used protection, and I felt anxious and exposed. Looking back now, I realise how fortunate I was not to contract something untreatable or to become pregnant.

I had no idea I had contracted Chlamydia. It wasn't until I met my current boyfriend and we became official in April 2022 that I decided to get tested. It dawned on me that there was a distinct possibility I might have contracted something before dating him and potentially passed it on. I decided it would be prudent to get tested for all STIs, despite experiencing no symptoms at all. I ordered a testing kit by post and sent it back almost immediately. I've ordered a few testing kits this way – it’s very quick and easy.

## Receiving the Diagnosis and Treatment

After about five days, I received a text message advising me to call the clinic to discuss my results. At that point, I knew something was wrong, as I had consistently used this same postal service for testing during university. With the support of my housemates, I called the clinic, and they confirmed I had tested positive for Chlamydia.

The very first thing I did was call my boyfriend and tell him my diagnosis, urging him to get tested right away. I was very upset with myself and felt panicky, which made it hard to be consoled. I was most annoyed because I knew he had always been very careful, and I was 99% sure I was the one who had transmitted it to him. However, my boyfriend was wonderful. He reassured me that there was no reason to be upset, that it could have been worse, and for all we knew, he could have been the one who transferred it to me.

After he also tested positive, we arranged to pick up our antibiotics together from the local sexual health clinic. The staff at the clinic were incredibly supportive, and I felt absolutely zero judgment, which was something I had been panicking about. The course of antibiotics was for one week. They advised us to abstain from any form of sexual activity until two weeks after taking our first antibiotic, and to wait six weeks after the first antibiotic to get re-tested to ensure the infection had cleared. When I got re-tested, the results came back after three days, confirming I was completely STI-free.

## Lessons Learned and Moving Forward

Although this experience felt traumatic, it taught me to prioritise my health. I had put off getting tested for too long because I felt ashamed at the thought of having an STI. I now feel ridiculous about that shame because the issue was so easily rectified. My experience highlights the importance of talking about STIs; the more we talk about something, the quicker it dispels the fear and stigma surrounding it. After I started talking more openly, I found many people who had either been through something similar or knew someone who had.

Things like STIs, unwanted pregnancies, or other ‘private’ health problems are often seen as taboo subjects due to the shame around sex and expressing sexuality. While Chlamydia is easily cured and not life-threatening, as is the case for the majority of STIs in this age of modern medicine, if I hadn’t caught it early, it could have led to a number of other health problems.

Since my diagnosis, I have never once felt shame about it. I am simply so happy that I took the leap and got tested. Now, I feel completely secure in my physical and mental health. I will never again put off calling the doctor or getting tested for anything that I think may be a risk to my health, because it is not worth living in fear and anxiety about. The most enjoyable sex, at the end of the day, is safe sex. Don’t put it off, just get tested!

Look after yourself and remember that non-barrier forms of birth control, such as the pill, will not protect you from STIs. So, if in doubt, ALWAYS use a condom and take the time to learn how to use one correctly. I am so happy to share my experience in the hope that even one person will see it and think to themselves, ‘I should put my health first too’.

## Preventing STIs

* The best way to prevent the transmission of STIs is by getting regularly tested and using condoms during sex.
* Completing a home test kit is quick and easy. You can do this by ordering an STI kit online that you complete at home and send back in the post.
