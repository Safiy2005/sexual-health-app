# Visiting a Sexual Health Clinic: Rachel’s Story

Rachel, 21, shares her first experience at a sexual health clinic. While the visit presented challenges, it provided her with valuable lessons on self-advocacy, informed consent, and navigating contraception options.

## Background and Accessing Services

Despite limited sex education in school, Rachel was aware of the location of her nearest sexual health clinic. When a condom broke during her second experience with penetrative sex, she felt confident enough to seek help because her school had previously shown videos of the local clinic and what to expect upon arrival.

Upon arriving at the clinic:
* She checked in at the welcome desk.
* She clearly stated her reason for visiting: "I need the morning after pill."
* She was asked to wait in the reception area until a clinician was available.

## The Initial Consultation

During the first consultation, Rachel experienced a disconnect between her expectations and the clinician’s advice. While Rachel requested the emergency contraceptive pill, the clinician strongly recommended the copper IUD (intrauterine device) as it is the most effective form of emergency contraception.

Rachel felt unsettled for several reasons:
* The environment was unfamiliar.
* She felt pressured to follow the clinician's advice rather than her own judgment.
* She lacked prior information about the different types of emergency contraception.

## Advocacy and Informed Consent

The situation changed when Rachel was taken to a procedure room to have the IUD inserted. The second clinician involved in the procedure provided more detail about the process and explicitly stated that Rachel could stop the procedure at any time.

When the procedure became physically and emotionally distressing, Rachel advocated for herself:
* She asked the clinician to stop the procedure.
* She explained her personal history, including difficulties with tampons and limited experience with penetrative sex.
* The second clinician listened without judgment and confirmed that an IUD might not be the most suitable choice for Rachel’s body at that time.

## Choosing the Right Method

After discussing the differences between the emergency contraceptive pill, the IUD, and the IUS (intrauterine system), Rachel was able to make an informed decision with the support of the healthcare professional.

The final outcome of the visit included:
* **Emergency Contraception:** Rachel chose to take the emergency contraceptive pill.
* **Future Planning:** The clinician helped Rachel create a contraceptive plan and provided a three-month supply of the oral contraceptive pill.
* **Professional Support:** The clinician’s welcoming and educational approach helped Rachel navigate the situation comfortably.

## Lessons Learned and Overcoming Stigma

Rachel’s experience taught her the importance of being an active participant in her own healthcare. She highlights several key takeaways for others:

* **Self-Advocacy:** It is important to speak up if a procedure or recommendation does not feel right for your body.
* **Informed Decisions:** Patients have the right to understand all their options before consenting to a treatment.
* **Breaking Stigma:** Requiring emergency contraception is not shameful, and one difficult experience should not prevent someone from accessing services in the future.
* **Reliable Information:** Utilizing resources like Brook or the NHS website can help individuals prepare for clinic visits.
* **Open Communication:** Speaking openly with peers can help reduce the stigma surrounding sexual health and encourage others to seek help when needed.
