# LGBT+ Abuse and Violence

Anyone can experience abuse and violence, but for LGBT+ people, these experiences often come in forms directly linked to their gender identity or sexual orientation. Abuse is always illegal and wrong. Under the Equality Act 2010, it is also against the law to treat someone differently because of their gender identity or sexual orientation.

While social attitudes have improved, prejudice continues to impact the daily lives of LGBT+ people:
* LGBT+ people are nearly four times more likely than non-LGBT people to be victims of violent crime.
* More than a third of LGBT+ people feel uncomfortable holding hands in public.
* Nearly 7 in 10 (68%) LGBT+ people have been sexually harassed at work.

## Domestic Abuse in LGBT+ Relationships

Domestic abuse occurs when a partner, ex-partner, or family member uses control to exert power over you. While it can happen to anyone, for LGBT+ people, it often includes specific elements of control linked to their identity. An abuser might:
* Use ‘outing’ as a method of control (threatening to reveal sexual orientation, gender identity, or HIV status without consent).
* Pressure the person to keep their identity or relationship a secret.
* Pressure them into sex or specific sexual acts by stereotyping their orientation (e.g., “You’re not a real lesbian if you don’t like...”).
* Claim that abusive behaviors are ‘normal’ in LGBT+ relationships.
* Isolate the person from friends, family, and the LGBT+ community.
* Withhold or use hormones and medication as a method of control.
* Attempt to change or suppress the person's sexual orientation or gender identity.

Domestic abuse can undermine an individual's self-worth and reinforce internalized homophobia, biphobia, or transphobia. If you are experiencing this, remember it is not your fault. Specific organizations like **Galop** offer an LGBT+ Domestic Abuse Helpline at **0800 999 5428**.

## Sexual Violence and Harassment

Sexual violence includes sexual harassment, rape, and sexual assault. For LGBT+ individuals, these acts may be driven by prejudice (homophobia, biphobia, transphobia, or acephobia), but they can also be perpetrated by people within the LGBT+ community.

### Sexual Harassment
Research shows that 88% of LGBT+ survey respondents have experienced sexual harassment since the age of 18. This includes:
* Unwelcome sexual comments, jokes, or questions.
* Coercion or pressure to perform sexual acts based on orientation stereotypes.

If you experience harassment at work, you can speak to a manager or HR department. At school, talk to a trusted teacher or parent. You can also contact support organizations to understand your options.

## Anti-LGBT Hate Crime

An anti-LGBT hate crime is any behavior motivated by prejudice against someone’s sexual orientation or gender identity. Statistics show 1 in 5 LGBT+ people have experienced a hate crime in the last 12 months.

Hate crimes include:
* Verbal abuse and slurs.
* Physical assaults.
* Theft or damage to property.
* Blackmail or threatening behavior.
* Abusive digital messages.

These crimes target an individual's core identity and can have lasting effects on mental and physical health. You can report hate crimes anonymously through Galop or contact the **LGBT Foundation** at **0345 3 30 30 30**.

## Conversion Therapy and Honour-Based Violence

### Conversion Therapy
Conversion therapy refers to any practice that attempts to change, ‘cure’, or suppress someone’s sexual orientation or gender identity. Nearly 1 in 5 LGBT+ people in the UK have been subjected to these practices. Methods may include:
* Physical, sexual, or emotional abuse.
* Exorcisms or forced prayer.
* Controlling diet and behavior.

It is not possible to change someone’s gender identity or sexual orientation. If you are being pressured into conversion therapy, contact **Galop’s Conversion Therapy Helpline** at **0800 130 3335**.

### Honour-Based Violence
This occurs when a family or community believes an individual's LGBT+ identity brings "dishonour" to the group. This can lead to physical abuse, forced marriage, or total exclusion from the community. You have the right to be respected for who you are; you are never to blame for this violence.

## Barriers to Seeking Help

LGBT+ individuals often face unique challenges when reporting violence, including:
* Fear of being "outed" accidentally during the process.
* Concerns that police or courts may use "deadnames" or misgender them.
* Fear of receiving a drug offence if the incident involved chemsex.
* The belief that services will not understand LGBT+ specific experiences.
* Worries about being excluded from their community if the perpetrator is also LGBT+.

## What Should I Do?

* **Immediate Danger:** Call **999**.
* **Set Boundaries:** If safe, tell the person their behavior is uncomfortable and must stop.
* **Keep Records:** Save text messages, photos of injuries, and names of witnesses.
* **Talk to Someone:** Join an LGBT+ support group or talk to a trusted friend, teacher, or manager.
* **Report It:** You can report incidents to the police or anonymously via Galop.

## How to Support Someone Else

* Call 999 if they are in immediate danger.
* Check in with the person to see if they are okay.
* If safe, challenge inappropriate comments or jokes (e.g., "I don't think that's appropriate").
* Only take formal action if the person wants you to, as you may accidentally "out" them or cause unwanted consequences.
* Offer to be a witness if they choose to report the incident.
