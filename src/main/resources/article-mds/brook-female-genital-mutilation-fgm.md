# Female Genital Mutilation (FGM)

**Content Warning:** This page contains discussion of female genital mutilation (FGM), including details about the procedure which may be distressing for some readers.

Female genital mutilation (FGM), also known as cutting or female circumcision, is a procedure carried out on girls and young women/people with a vulva. It involves removing some or all of the external genitals for any reason other than genuine medical necessity. 

FGM has no health benefits and is harmful in many ways. It involves removing and damaging healthy genital tissue and interferes with the natural functions of the body.

## Legal Status in the UK
FGM is illegal in the UK. It is also illegal to take someone out of the country to undergo FGM elsewhere. The maximum sentence for carrying out FGM, or helping it to take place, is 14 years in prison.

Despite the law, the charity Equality Now estimates that 137,000 women and girls living in the UK have been affected by FGM. Globally, UNICEF reports that over 200 million girls and women have undergone the procedure.

## Seeking Help
If you are worried that you or someone you know might be at risk of FGM, it is vital to speak to someone you trust, such as a teacher or a doctor. You will always be taken seriously.

* **NSPCC FGM Helpline:** Call 0800 028 3550 or email 
* **Childline:** Call 0800 1111 or talk to them online.

If you have already experienced FGM, you might feel unsure of who to talk to. There are many organisations that can help you process your experience in a therapeutic way and connect you with others who have had similar experiences.

## Health Complications
FGM is a dangerous practice that can cause long-lasting health problems, including:
* Incontinence or difficulties urinating
* Frequent or chronic vaginal, pelvic, or urinary infections
* Painful or problematic periods
* Kidney damage and possible kidney failure
* Cysts and abscesses
* Pain during sex
* Infertility
* Complications during pregnancy and childbirth
* Loss of sensation in the clitoris and/or vagina
* Emotional and mental health problems

## Who is at Risk?
The people most at risk in the UK are those who come from or have strong family links to countries where FGM is common. This includes people born abroad and those born in the UK to parents from those countries. 

The age at which FGM is carried out varies and may take place:
* When a baby is newborn
* During childhood or adolescence
* Just before marriage
* During pregnancy

## Types of FGM
The World Health Organization (WHO) classifies FGM into four types:
* **Type 1 (Clitoridectomy):** Removal of some or all of the clitoris and/or the clitoral hood.
* **Type 2 (Excision):** Removal of some or all of the clitoris and the inner labia. Sometimes the outer labia are removed as well.
* **Type 3 (Infibulation):** Narrowing the vaginal opening using stitches. The inner and outer labia may also be removed. This often causes significant problems with urinating and menstruation.
* **Type 4 (Other):** Any other harmful procedures to the vulva or vagina for non-medical purposes, such as pricking, piercing, scraping, or burning (cauterizing).

## Support for Survivors
Many physical side effects of FGM can be mitigated with medical treatment. For example, a surgery called **deinfibulation** can be performed to open up the vagina if necessary.

If you have had FGM, you do not need to be embarrassed or scared to seek help. You will not be in trouble, regardless of your age, and you do not have to identify who performed the procedure if you do not wish to.

### Pregnancy and Childbirth
FGM can cause serious complications during childbirth. If you are pregnant and have had FGM, you should speak to your midwife so they can arrange appropriate care and support.

## Support Organisations
* **FORWARD (Foundation for Women’s Health Research and Development):** Provides advice and one-to-one support for African women and girls. Call 0208 960 4000.
* **Daughters of Eve:** A non-profit protecting the reproductive health rights of young people from FGM-practising communities.
* **FGM National Clinical Group:** A UK-based charity working with survivors of FGM.
* **The Dahlia Project:** A specialist service for women who have undergone FGM, offering support in London.
* **Brook:** You can visit a sexual health service like Brook to speak confidentially with a sexual health nurse.
