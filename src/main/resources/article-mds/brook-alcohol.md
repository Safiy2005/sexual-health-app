# Alcohol

Alcohol is a type of drink which contains the chemical ethanol. While alcoholic drinks can sometimes feel like a stimulant because they make people feel energised, alcohol is actually a depressant. This means it slows down your central nervous system. It is also important to note that alcohol can significantly affect your mood and mental health.

## Alcohol and the law

In the UK, it is legal to buy and consume alcohol when you are 18 years old. It is against the law to:
* Buy or attempt to buy alcohol if you are under 18.
* Sell alcohol to anyone under 18.
* Buy alcohol for anyone under 18 (except in very specific circumstances).

## Making your own choice

Drinking alcohol is a personal choice. You have the right to choose not to drink, and you do not need to explain that decision to anyone. People drink for various reasons, including socialising, experimenting, or escaping feelings of stress and anxiety.

### Managing peer pressure
If you feel pressured to drink by others, consider the following strategies:
* Focus on what feels right for you rather than the opinions of others.
* Communicate your reasons for saying "no" clearly to build your own confidence.
* Remember that many people around you may be feeling the same pressure.
* Talk to a trusted adult, teacher, or healthcare professional.
* If you are under 19, you can contact Childline at 0800 1111.

If friends do not respect your decision to say "no," it may be worth considering if they are prioritising your happiness and safety.

## Alcohol and sexual health

Alcohol can lower inhibitions, which may lead to social confidence but can also result in poor judgement and risky behaviours.

### Consent
The Sexual Offences Act is clear: if someone is drunk or high, they may not have the capacity to consent to any kind of sexual activity. You should ensure a person is fully coordinated and responsive before engaging in sexual activity. If you are unsure of their sobriety, wait until they have sobered up.

### Drink spiking
Spiking is a serious crime where alcohol or drugs are added to a person’s drink without their knowledge. This can lead to theft, sexual assault, and severe inebriation.

### Contraception
Alcohol can impair your ability to make safe choices regarding sex. Carrying condoms is recommended to ensure you are prepared. Condoms are the only form of contraception that protects against both pregnancy and STIs.

## Understanding units and intake

Alcohol intake is measured in units. Government guidelines recommend:
* **Men (or people with a penis):** No more than 3 to 4 units per day.
* **Women (or people with a vagina):** No more than 2 to 3 units per day.

The liver takes approximately one hour to process one unit of alcohol. Examples of one unit include:
* Half a pint of beer (3.5% alcohol).
* A single measure of spirits (25ml at 40% alcohol).
* A small glass of wine (125ml at 8% alcohol).

### Binge drinking
Binge drinking is defined as drinking twice the daily recommendation in a single session. Consuming more than 20 units in one session puts you at high risk of alcohol poisoning.

## Effects of alcohol on the body

### Blood Alcohol Concentration (BAC)
BAC is the percentage of alcohol in your bloodstream. Effects vary by weight, sex, and how much you have eaten:
* **Up to 0.05%:** Feeling relaxed and talkative.
* **0.05% to 0.08%:** Impaired judgement and reduced inhibitions.
* **0.08% to 0.15%:** Slurred speech, impaired balance, and possible vomiting.
* **0.15% to 0.30%:** Inability to walk; potential loss of bladder control or consciousness.
* **Above 0.30%:** Risk of coma or death.

### Short-term effects
* **Nausea:** Alcohol irritates the stomach lining, especially on an empty stomach.
* **Hangovers:** Caused primarily by dehydration and chemical additives in drinks.
* **Mood:** As a depressant, alcohol can increase feelings of anxiety or low mood.
* **Sleep:** While it may make you sleepy, alcohol causes restless, disturbed sleep and can worsen insomnia.

### Long-term effects
* **Social impacts:** Relationship breakdowns, debt, and unemployment.
* **Dependence:** Alcohol can be physically and psychologically addictive.
* **Mental health:** Regular use reduces serotonin and dopamine levels, which can lead to chronic depression.
* **Liver disease:** Prolonged misuse can lead to permanent scarring of the liver (cirrhosis).
* **Other health issues:** Increased risk of heart disease, lung problems, cancer, and fertility issues.

## Tips for cutting down

If you feel your drinking is becoming unmanageable, consider these steps:
1. **Set a limit:** Decide how much you will drink before you start.
2. **Set a budget:** Only take a fixed amount of money for alcohol.
3. **Tell people:** Seek support from friends and family.
4. **Take it one day at a time:** Focus on small, daily successes.
5. **Choose smaller sizes:** Opt for smaller servings.
6. **Watch the strength:** Choose lower ABV (%) drinks or use mixers.
7. **Hydrate:** Drink water before you start and alternate alcoholic drinks with soft drinks.
8. **Drink-free days:** Schedule days every week where you do not drink.

## Alcohol dependence

Symptoms of dependence include:
* Requiring more alcohol to achieve the same effect.
* Experiencing cravings within 5 hours of waking.
* Physical withdrawal symptoms (tremors, sweating, nausea, anxiety).
* Planning your entire day around alcohol.
* Continuing to drink despite negative consequences in your life.

## Where to get help

If you are worried about your drinking or someone else's, support is available:
* **GP:** A doctor can provide advice and refer you to local treatment services.
* **Drinkaware:** Provides a helpline (0300 123 1110) and an online "Drinkchat" tool.
* **Childline:** Call 0800 1111 for confidential support for those under 19.
* **Brook:** Staff at Brook services can help you access support for alcohol and substance use.
