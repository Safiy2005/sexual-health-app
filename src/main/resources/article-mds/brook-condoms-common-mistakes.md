# 12 Common Mistakes When Using Condoms

Condoms are a highly effective method of contraception and the only method that also protects against sexually transmitted infections (STIs). To ensure they provide maximum protection, it's crucial to use them correctly. Here are 12 common mistakes people make when using condoms:

## Checking for Damage

* **Not checking the packet:** Always inspect the condom wrapper for any signs of damage, such as holes or tears, before use.
* **Heat damage:** Condoms can be damaged by heat. Avoid storing them in warm places like wallets, bags, or pockets for extended periods. While carrying condoms is a good idea, replace them regularly (e.g., monthly) to ensure they remain safe.

## Expiry Dates and Safety Marks

* **Using expired condoms:** All condoms have an expiry date printed on the wrapper. Do not use condoms past their expiry date, as they will not offer adequate protection.
* **Missing safety marks:** Ensure condoms have the European CE mark or UKCA mark. These indicate that the condoms have been tested to high safety standards.

## Opening the Wrapper

* **Tearing the wrapper incorrectly:** Before opening, feel for the rib of the condom inside the packaging. Gently push it to the side so that when you tear the wrapper open, you do not accidentally tear the condom itself.
* **Using sharp objects:** Never open a condom wrapper using scissors, teeth, or any other sharp object, as this can damage the condom.

## Timing and Application

* **Putting on the condom too late:** A condom must be worn *before* any genital contact or sexual activity begins. If you only put it on just before ejaculation, fluids may have already been exchanged, leaving you unprotected against STIs and pregnancy.
* **Putting the condom on the wrong way:** If you put a condom on the wrong way (so it won't roll down) and it has touched the penis, it is essential to discard it and start with a new one. The outside of the incorrectly applied condom may have come into contact with bodily fluids, increasing the risk of pregnancy and STIs.
* **Not squeezing the tip:** When putting on a condom, always squeeze the tip to release any trapped air. This prevents the condom from breaking during use.

## During Sex and Removal

* **Not using condoms for all types of sex:** Condoms should be used whenever there is contact between genital or anal areas, including after ejaculation, to prevent the transmission of STIs and unintended pregnancy.
* **Removing a condom mid-sex without consent:** Removing a condom during sex without your partner's explicit consent is a form of sexual assault known as ‘stealthing’. This is never acceptable.
* **Not holding the condom during withdrawal:** To prevent the condom from slipping off during withdrawal, gently hold it in place with your fingers as the penis is removed.

## Lubricant and Size

* **Using the wrong type of lubricant:** While lubricants can enhance comfort and reduce friction, it's vital to use only water-based lubricants. Oil-based lubricants (like those found in some massage oils or petroleum jelly) can weaken latex condoms and cause them to break.
* **Using the wrong size condom:** Condoms that are too big may slip off, while those that are too small can tear. It's important to find a condom that fits correctly, as various sizes are available.

## Switching Between Sex Types and Sharing

* **Not changing condoms between different types of sex:** If switching between anal sex and vaginal sex, or vice versa, a new condom must be used for each. Introducing bacteria from the rectum into the vagina can lead to infections.
* **Sharing sex toys without a condom:** If sharing sex toys, such as vibrators, use a new condom for each person or for each new use. Sex toys can transmit STIs if they are not cleaned or covered.

## What to Do If Something Goes Wrong

Even with careful use, condoms can sometimes break or come off during sex. If this happens, it's crucial for both partners to be aware so they can take appropriate steps. This may include discussing emergency contraception or getting tested for STIs.
