# Bacterial Vaginosis (BV)

Bacterial vaginosis (BV) is the most common cause of vaginal discharge. While it is not a sexually transmitted infection (STI), it is more common in people with a vagina who are sexually active. BV does not occur in people with a penis.

## Symptoms of BV

Approximately half of the people who have BV will not experience any symptoms. For those who do, the most common symptom is unusual vaginal discharge. 

Characteristics of BV discharge include:
* A strong, fishy smell (particularly after sex)
* A watery consistency
* A white or grey color

It is important to note that BV does not typically cause itchiness or soreness. If these symptoms develop, there may be another condition affecting the vagina.

## Causes and Risk Factors

BV occurs when the natural balance of bacteria in the vagina is upset. While the exact cause remains somewhat unclear, you are more likely to develop BV if you:
* Use scented soaps or bubble baths
* Use vaginal deodorant
* Douche (clean out the inside of the vagina)
* Use strong detergents to wash your underwear
* Are a smoker
* Have an IUD (intrauterine device) or IUS (intrauterine system) fitted
* Experience hormonal changes
* Have semen in the vagina after sex

BV can be passed between sexual partners who both have vaginas. However, it cannot be passed from a person with a vagina to a person with a penis.

You cannot get BV from:
* Hugging
* Sharing baths or towels
* Swimming pools
* Toilet seats
* Sharing cups, plates, or cutlery

## Diagnosis and Testing

A doctor or nurse can diagnose BV by discussing your symptoms and examining your vagina. During the exam, they may:
* Take a sample of cells using a cotton bud or small plastic loop (this may be uncomfortable but should not be painful).
* Test the acidity (pH level) of your vagina, as BV causes the pH to change.

Results are typically available within a week. Please note that routine blood tests and cervical smear tests (pap tests) will not detect BV.

Testing and treatment are available at Brook services, GP surgeries, or GUM (genitourinary medicine) and sexual health clinics.

## Treatment

BV may sometimes go away on its own, but it is easily treated with:
* Antibiotic tablets
* Antibiotic creams or gels used inside the vagina (usually for around a week)

If you experience repeated episodes of BV and use an IUD or IUS, a healthcare provider may recommend trying a different method of contraception.

## BV and Pregnancy

BV is generally not serious unless you are pregnant, in which case it can cause complications. If you are pregnant, inform your doctor or nurse so they can provide safe and appropriate treatment.

## Safety Warning

Some creams used to treat BV can weaken the latex in condoms, diaphragms, and caps. If you are using these methods of contraception, ask your doctor or nurse for advice regarding alternative protection during treatment.
