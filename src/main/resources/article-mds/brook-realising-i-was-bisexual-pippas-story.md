# Realising I was bisexual: Pippa's story

Pippa shares her personal journey of discovering her bisexuality, addressing common misconceptions, and offering advice for those navigating their own sexual identity.

## Early Realisations and Denial

For a long time, Pippa did not realise she was bisexual. While she had romantic crushes on boys during her childhood, her first experience of sexual attraction to someone on screen was toward a woman. Initially, she dismissed these feelings, assuming she simply wanted to emulate the woman's appearance.

By age 18, Pippa began experiencing sexual fantasies where more than half involved women. This led to a period of denial, where she convinced herself these thoughts were "just a phase" or a result of finding something "different" exciting. However, by age 21, the weight of this denial began to affect her happiness and her relationships.

## The Struggle with Identity

Accepting a non-heterosexual identity was challenging, particularly because Pippa felt she did not fit into the binary categories of "gay" or "straight." This led to two primary anxieties:
* The reality of being bisexual.
* The fear that she might actually be gay and was simply "confused."

During this time, Pippa experienced negative thought patterns, anxiety, and depression. She worried about her future ability to have a family, whether she could ever be happy in a relationship, and whether there was something "biologically wrong" with her. 

## Finding Support and Talking it Through

Pippa eventually realised that she needed to talk about her feelings to protect her mental health. After an initial attempt to tell a friend was not taken seriously, she sought support through anonymous and safe channels:
* **Online Communities:** Joining LGBT+ friendly websites for informal support.
* **University Groups:** Participating in university LGBT+ social groups that offered a secure environment.
* **Youth Charities:** Contacting LGBT+ charities to speak with advisors.

Talking to people without existing social or family connections allowed her to practice articulating her identity without fear of gossip or judgment.

## The Coming Out Process

Pippa’s first successful "coming out" was via text to a trusted friend. His supportive and non-judgmental response provided the validation she needed to move forward. This was followed by telling her brother, parents, and other friends. 

Through this process, Pippa learned several lessons:
* Coming out becomes easier over time.
* It is okay to ignore the opinions of ignorant people.
* You have the right to choose who you tell and when.
* If people truly care about you, they will accept you for who you are.

Today, Pippa no longer feels the need to formally "come out." She discusses her life and relationships freely, treating her sexuality as just another detail of her identity.

## Addressing Common Misconceptions

Pippa highlights several harmful myths regarding bisexuality:

* **Promiscuity:** Being bisexual does not mean a person has more sexual partners or wants to sleep with everyone.
* **Confusion:** Identifying as bisexual is a valid choice; it is not a state of being "undecided" or "confused."
* **Seeking Attention:** Bisexual women are interested in women because they find them attractive, not to gain attention from men.
* **Non-existence (Bi-erasure):** A bisexual person’s identity does not change based on the gender of the person they are currently dating.

## Advice for Others

Pippa offers the following guidance for anyone struggling with their sexuality:
* **Accept yourself on your own terms:** Claim the freedom to make decisions about your life without compromising for others.
* **Choose your circle wisely:** If friends are unsupportive, do not be afraid to find new, supportive communities.
* **Ignore the haters:** Those who gossip or show disrespect are not worth your time.
* **Communication is key:** If you feel bad about your sexuality, find someone trustworthy and understanding to talk to.
