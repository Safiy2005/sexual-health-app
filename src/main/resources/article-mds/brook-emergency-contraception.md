# Emergency Contraception: The IUD (Copper Coil)

If you have had unprotected sex, you may be able to prevent pregnancy by using the copper coil (IUD). While there are two types of emergency contraception—the copper coil and the emergency contraceptive pill (the morning after pill)—the copper coil is the most effective method.

## How the IUD Works
The morning after pill works by delaying or preventing the release of an egg (ovulation). However, it is less effective than the copper coil because it only works if ovulation has not yet occurred. In contrast, the copper coil can be used to prevent pregnancy at any time during your menstrual cycle.

* **Timing:** The copper coil can be fitted within 120 hours (five days) of unprotected sex, or up to five days after the earliest time you could have ovulated.
* **Mechanism:** It prevents a pregnancy from happening; it does not cause an abortion.
* **Long-term use:** Unlike the morning after pill, the copper coil acts as a regular method of contraception for up to ten years if kept in.

## The Fitting Process
The copper coil must be fitted by a specially trained doctor or nurse. You can access this service at sexual health clinics (such as Brook) or through your GP.

* **Consultation:** A healthcare professional will ask about any medications you are taking, when you had unprotected sex, and where you are in your menstrual cycle to ensure the IUD is suitable for you.
* **Procedure:** Fitting usually takes 15–20 minutes. It can be uncomfortable, but you can request a local anaesthetic to help manage any pain.
* **Interim protection:** If you cannot have the coil fitted immediately, you may be advised to take the emergency contraceptive pill in the meantime.
* **Follow-up:** You can continue using the coil as your regular contraception. If you wish to have it removed, this must be done by a professional after your next period.

## Side Effects and Safety
There are no serious short or long-term side effects from using the copper coil, though some individuals may experience:
* Periods that are heavier, more painful, or longer-lasting.
* A small risk of infection following the procedure.
* A small risk of the coil being pushed out by the body.

The copper coil is safe to use when breastfeeding. It can also be used as soon as three weeks after giving birth or two weeks after an abortion or miscarriage.

## Knowing if Emergency Contraception Worked
If your next period arrives around the expected time and is not unusually light, it is unlikely you are pregnant. 

You should see a doctor or nurse if:
* Your period is lighter or shorter than normal.
* You miss your next period or withdrawal bleed.
* You have any concerns.

A pregnancy test is recommended if your period is late, but it must be taken at least **three weeks** after the unprotected sex to ensure an accurate result.

## What is Unprotected Sex?
Unprotected sex refers to sexual intercourse without any form of contraception. Examples include:
* The condom broke or slipped off.
* You forgot to take your contraceptive pill or failed to change your patch or ring.
* No contraception was used at all.

**Note:** Unprotected sex carries a risk of sexually transmitted infections (STIs). It is highly recommended that you get tested for STIs following any unprotected encounter.

## What if it’s been more than five days?
If more than five days have passed since unprotected sex, emergency contraception will no longer be effective. In this case, you should take a pregnancy test:
* As soon as you realize your period is late.
* If your period is shorter or lighter than usual.
* Three weeks after the unprotected sex.

If you are unsure whether you need emergency contraception, speak to a pharmacist, GP, or a nurse at a sexual health clinic as soon as possible. The longer you wait, the less effective emergency contraception becomes.
