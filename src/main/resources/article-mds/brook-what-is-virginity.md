# What is 'virginity'?

The word ‘virginity’ has been used for thousands of years to describe the state of not having had sex. It is generally used more in relation to women and people with vaginas than men and people with penises. While the term is common, the concept of virginity is problematic, unscientific, and often used to enforce gender stereotypes.

## What does ‘having sex’ mean?

Often when people talk about ‘having sex’, they really mean two people having penis-in-vagina (PIV) sex, also known as vaginal sex. Consequently, ‘losing your virginity’ is often used as code for having vaginal sex for the first time.

At Brook, we use the word ‘sex’ to include all sexual activity, not just activity relating to penetration. There are many ways to be sexually intimate, to feel sensual, or to orgasm. Therefore, ‘sex’ and having sex ‘for the first time’ can mean different things for different people.

* **Sexual debut:** Some people prefer the phrase ‘sexual debut’ instead of ‘losing your virginity’ because it doesn’t treat the experience as a loss or a change in status.
* **A spectrum of activity:** Other people view having sex for the first time as just one moment in a life of sexual experiences, rather than a significant turning point.

Regardless of how you think about it, the most important factor is that any sexual activity is consensual, happy, and comfortable.

## Why the concept of virginity is problematic

Historically, a ‘virgin’ has been considered ‘pure’ or ‘innocent,’ while those who have sex (especially outside of marriage) may be labeled ‘impure.’ These ideas cause several issues:

* **Gender stereotypes and double standards:** Men and boys may feel pressure to have sex before they are ready to prove their masculinity. Conversely, women and girls may feel pressure to ‘preserve their virginity’ to avoid negative social perceptions.
* **Restrictive ideas about sex:** The focus on PIV sex often excludes the LGBT+ community. It also suggests that oral sex, anal sex, or hand-to-genital contact don't ‘count’ as sex, even though they are all forms of sexual activity that require consent.
* **Pressure to have sex before you are ready:** When the focus is on a one-off event, people may think less about their own readiness, their feelings for their partner, or what kind of intimacy they actually want.

Whether or not you have had sex has no impact on your worth as a person.

## The medical reality of virginity

‘Virginity’ is not a medical or scientific concept. There is no way for a medical professional to tell from someone’s body if they have or haven't had sex. 

The idea that you can verify virginity by examining the tightness of a vagina is false. The vagina is elastic and stretches or constricts based on factors like mood, arousal, and recent penetration. Using creams or chemicals to ‘tighten’ the vagina is dangerous and can lead to infection.

## What is a hymen?

The hymen is a ring of thin skin that partially covers the opening of the vagina. It does not cover the opening completely, as it must allow menstrual blood to pass through.

* **Diversity of bodies:** Everyone’s hymen is different. Some people are born without one.
* **Changes to the hymen:** The hymen can stretch or tear through sports, using tampons, or vaginal sex.
* **Bleeding:** While some people experience bleeding the first time they have vaginal sex, many do not. It is possible to have vaginal sex with no changes to the hymen at all.

## Virginity testing

Virginity testing is an invasive procedure where someone’s vulva and vagina are inspected to determine if they have had sex. 

* **Inaccuracy:** There is no scientific way to test for virginity. Doctors cannot accurately assess sexual history by looking at the hymen or genitals.
* **Legality:** Virginity testing is illegal in the UK as of March 2022.
* **Harm:** Often performed without consent, it is a form of sexual assault that can cause significant physical and psychological trauma.

## Hymen ‘repair’ surgery (Hymenoplasty)

Hymenoplasty is a surgery intended to give the physical appearance of ‘virginity’ by reshaping the hymen.

* **Legality:** It is a criminal offence to provide hymenoplasty in the UK.
* **Risks:** Like labiaplasty or vaginaplasty, this surgery is often performed to comply with harmful social myths. It carries risks of pain, infection, and loss of sensation.
* **Brook’s Stance:** Brook believes medical professionals should challenge dangerous myths about virginity rather than reinforcing them through unnecessary surgery.

## FGM and ‘honour’ violence

The preservation of virginity is often cited as a reason for Female Genital Mutilation (FGM), which is harmful and illegal. In some cultures, the loss of virginity before marriage is considered ‘shameful’ and can lead to ‘honour-based’ violence or exclusion from the family.
