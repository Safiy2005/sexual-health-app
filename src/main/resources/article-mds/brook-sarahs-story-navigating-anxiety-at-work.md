# Navigating anxiety at work: Sarah’s story

Sarah, 34, shares her experience of how the COVID-19 pandemic and the transition to working from home affected her mental health. She discusses her journey with anxiety and the personal "toolbox" she developed to manage her symptoms and maintain her wellbeing in professional and social environments.

## Recognising the shift

For many years, Sarah worked in the dating industry, a role that required constant social interaction and high levels of energy. Before the pandemic, she felt energised by connecting people and was comfortable speaking in front of large audiences or hosting busy events. 

However, as the world moved into a post-pandemic phase, she began to experience a significant shift. What used to be effortless—such as drinks evenings or busy office environments—became exhausting. She found herself needing to "psych herself up" before events and requiring significant recovery time afterward to address a depletion of "energetic resources."

## Understanding the cause

Through coaching and self-reflection, Sarah explored whether she was experiencing imposter syndrome. She discovered that her time spent working from home and interacting via "zoom rooms" had encouraged a more introverted nature. 

She describes the disconnection from human interaction during the pandemic as a "shock to the social system." This led to an adverse reaction that manifested as anxiety. Her symptoms often include:
* Feeling tense and nervous.
* A sense that something terrible is about to happen.
* Struggling to speak up in a room or wanting to avoid attention.
* Feeling emotionally depleted or wanting to "sleep for days" after performative work situations.
* Subconsciously withdrawing from personal social life to "refill batteries" after a busy work week.

## The anxiety toolbox

To manage these feelings, Sarah created a "toolbox" of techniques. She identified these by journaling about the moments and environments where she felt most calm, relaxed, and authentic. 

Instead of fighting the feelings of anxiety, she now uses these tools to soothe her state of mind:

* **Gentle movement:** Engaging in daily yoga, Pilates, or swimming.
* **Creative flow:** Listening to acoustic music to reach a productive flow state.
* **Mindfulness:** Keeping a gratitude diary at the start and end of each day.
* **Strategic scheduling:** Planning social events for the end of the week to allow for weekend recovery.
* **Nature and animals:** Spending time with her dog and walking by water.
* **Digital boundaries:** Switching off app notifications to reduce overwhelm.
* **High-intensity exercise:** Using running as a way to combat periods of high anxiety.
* **Self-care:** Incorporating bubble baths or massages.
* **Proactive tasks:** Completing small chores like cleaning or car maintenance to regain a sense of control.
* **Social boundaries:** Limiting input and time spent at large social gatherings.
* **Honesty:** Being open with others about how she is feeling.

## It’s okay not to be okay

Approximately 1 in 4 people in the UK experience a mental health problem each year. Acknowledging anxiety, figuring out the specific needs required to support an anxious state of mind, and being honest with others are key steps in management. 

If you are worried about your mental health or someone else's, support is available. You do not need a formal diagnosis to seek help or talk to someone about your experiences.
