# What is Sexual Harassment?

If you have experienced sexual harassment or assault, it is not your fault and you are not alone. Sexual harassment is any form of unwanted sexual behaviour. It can happen to anyone, regardless of gender or sexuality, and could be from someone of the same or different sex.

## Understanding Sexual Harassment

Sexual harassment can be a one-off incident or a pattern of repeated behaviour. It can happen anywhere, including at school, on public transport, or in the workplace. It is often linked to other forms of harassment such as homophobia, transphobia, biphobia, racism, ableism, or religious discrimination.

## Common Forms of Sexual Harassment

### Sexual Comments, Names, and Jokes
* Making sexual comments, jokes, or gestures, including jokes about someone's sexuality.
* Calling someone names such as “slut” or “whore.”
* Sending unwanted sexual emails, DMs, or text messages.
* Spreading sexual rumours about someone.
* Showing sexual photos or videos in an educational or professional setting.

### Unwanted Sexual Attention and Contact
* Staring or leering at someone’s body.
* Someone exposing private parts of their body or masturbating in front of someone without their consent.
* Unwanted touching or physical contact without consent.
* Pressuring someone to perform sexual acts they do not want to do.
* Offering rewards in return for sexual favours.
* Verbal harassment, such as catcalling or wolf-whistling.

## Cyberflashing

Cyberflashing is the act of sending someone sexual or explicit images or videos (nudes) without their consent. This can be shocking and may cause feelings of anger, intimidation, or distress.

If you experience cyberflashing:
* Block the sender.
* Speak to someone you trust.
* Report the incident to the platform where it occurred (e.g., social media or dating sites).
* Seek professional support.

It is also sexual harassment if someone pressures you to send nude images. Sending naked images of, or to, people under the age of 18 is illegal.

## Intimate Image Abuse ("Revenge Porn")

Intimate image abuse occurs when someone shares private, sexual, or explicit videos or photos of another person without their consent. 
* It is illegal in England and Wales.
* It can occur via social media, texting, email, or physical distribution.
* For those under 18, this content is classified as child abuse imagery.

### Where to Get Help
* **Under 18s:** Contact Childline (0800 1111) or the Internet Watch Foundation (IWF).
* **18 and Over:** Contact the Revenge Porn Helpline (0845 6000 459).

## Upskirting

Upskirting is taking a photo under someone’s clothing without their consent. This is a criminal offence in England and Wales, regardless of whether it was done for sexual gratification or to humiliate the victim. It can happen to anyone, regardless of gender, in any location.

## Sexual Assault and Violence

Some forms of harassment may also be classified as sexual assault. Sexual assault, abuse, or violence includes any act of unwanted sexual contact, including:
* Rape (sexual intercourse without consent).
* Unwanted sexual touching.
* Online grooming.
* Domestic abuse.
* Sexual exploitation.

## Compliments vs. Sexual Harassment

Understanding the difference between a compliment and harassment is essential:

**A Compliment:**
* Usually said by a friend or acquaintance.
* Said in a respectful way with no expectation of anything in return.
* Does not sexualise the person.
* Makes the person feel happy and safe.

**Sexual Harassment:**
* Often shouted across the street or in public.
* Makes the person feel intimidated or unsafe.
* Refers to body parts or includes sexual comments.
* Occurs in places where the person may feel unable to leave.

## The Impact of Sexual Harassment

Experiencing sexual harassment can cause significant distress, leading to:
* Anxiety and depression.
* Problems with sleeping and eating.
* Headaches and physical tension.
* Loss of self-confidence.

If someone's behaviour makes you feel uncomfortable, it is not okay, and your feelings are valid.

## Steps to Deal with Sexual Harassment

If you have been harassed, remember it is not your fault. You can take the following steps:

* **Ask them to stop:** If you feel safe and comfortable, tell the person their behaviour is unwanted.
* **Make a note of what happens:** Keep a diary or record of dates, times, locations, and any witnesses. Save any unwanted electronic communications as evidence.
* **Speak to someone:** Talk to a trusted family member, teacher, or friend. You can also contact organisations like Victim Support.
* **Report it:** You can report harassment to your school, college, or workplace. They are required to investigate.
* **Legal Protections:** If you are harassed at work or by a landlord, you are protected by the Equality Act 2010.
* **Emergency:** If you are in immediate danger, call the police on 999.

## How to Support Others

Witnessing sexual harassment and doing nothing can send a message that the behaviour is acceptable. You can help by:

* **Checking on the person:** Ask if they are okay to let them know the behaviour was noticed and was unacceptable.
* **Speaking up:** Calmly state that the behaviour "is not cool" or "is not okay."
* **Interrupting:** Distract the harasser by starting a different conversation or asking a question to stop the interaction.
* **Helping them find support:** Signpost them to the resources on this page.
* **Telling a trusted adult:** Report the incident to a teacher or parent.
* **Reporting a crime:** You can report an incident to the police on someone else's behalf.

*Never put yourself in danger. If you feel unsafe, walk away and act later.*
