# Anxiety & sex: Iona's story

Iona, 23, discusses the impact of anxiety on her sexual well-being and the strategies she uses to manage her mental health.

> **Content Warning:** This page contains discussions of mental illness.

## The impact of anxiety on sexual experience

Anxiety can significantly disrupt sexual function and pleasure. For many individuals, periods of high stress or mental illness can lead to:
* Difficulty reaching orgasm.
* Intrusive thoughts (often described as "incessant chattering" in the mind).
* Feelings of guilt or pressure regarding a partner's satisfaction.
* Physical symptoms, such as vaginal dryness, even when a sex drive is present.

## The mind-body disconnect

A common experience when navigating anxiety and sex is a sense of dissociation or disconnect between the mind and the body. While the body may be engaged in sexual activity, the mind can remain preoccupied with anxious thoughts, preventing a person from being fully present. This frustration can often lead to a cycle of self-criticism, which further exacerbates the anxiety.

## Communication and support

Opening up about how mental health affects intimacy is a crucial step in managing these challenges. 

* **Peer Support:** Speaking with a trusted group of friends can provide a safe space to express fears and frustrations that may feel too "unsexy" or difficult to share with a partner initially.
* **Partner Communication:** Although it can be difficult to broach the subject, honest communication helps partners understand that sexual difficulties (like the inability to orgasm) are often a result of mental health rather than a lack of attraction or connection.
* **Vulnerability:** Emotional releases, such as crying during or after sex, can serve as a catalyst for important conversations about one's mental state.

## Holistic approaches and meditation

Taking a holistic approach to mental health can help individuals regain a sense of power over their bodies. Meditation and mindfulness practices can act as a form of "hypnotism" for anxious thoughts, helping to:
* Transition the body into a state of calm.
* Bridge the gap between the mind and body.
* Reduce the frustration associated with intrusive thoughts.

## Normalising sexual health and mental health

Mental health issues are widespread and frequently affect sexual function. It is important to recognise that the following issues are common and experienced by many individuals and couples:
* Erectile dysfunction
* Fluctuations in sex drive (libido)
* Difficulties with orgasm

Reducing the stigma around these topics through frank conversation can help individuals feel less isolated and more empowered to seek help.

## It’s OK not to be OK

Approximately 1 in 4 people in the UK will experience a mental health problem each year (NHS England, 2020). If you are worried about your mental health or how it is affecting your life, support is available. You do not need a formal diagnosis to reach out for help; whenever you are ready to talk, there are services available to listen.
