# Consent Myths and Facts

It’s important to remember that you always have a right to say no to any form of sex or sexual activity – and/or to withdraw your consent – at any time. It doesn’t matter who the other person is, what your relationship with them is, or how far you’ve gone with them or others in the past.

Sometimes people don’t really understand what consent means in different situations. Here are some common myths and facts about sex and consent.

## Relationship and Past History

**Myth:** “If two people have had sex with each other before, or are in a relationship, it’s always OK to have sex again.”

**Fact:** Just because you had sex with someone before doesn’t mean that you’ve automatically given consent to have sex with them again. This includes if you are in a relationship. 
* It’s always up to you if you want to have sex.
* Consent must be sought and given every time. 
* You can’t assume that someone wants to have sex at any given time – you have to ask and listen to the response.

## Types of Sexual Activity

**Myth:** “You only need consent when it comes to penetrative sex.”

**Fact:** There are many different ways to have sex with someone, many of which don’t involve penetration. 
* You need consent for all types of sex. 
* People of all genders need to ask for consent.
* Consenting to one sexual activity does not mean you have consented to everything.

## Physical Arousal

**Myth:** “If your body is turned on, then you are giving consent.”

**Fact:** Physical arousal is not an invitation to have sex or a sign that someone gives their consent.
* Sometimes people’s bodies will react (such as getting an erection or the vulva becoming wet) even though they do not want to engage in sexual activity. 
* Physical changes in the body can be a sign that someone is aroused, but this is not always the case. 
* Someone might be feeling aroused but still not want to take part in a sexual activity, and this is fine.

## Alcohol and Drugs

**Myth:** “It’s fine to have sex with someone if they are drunk or high.”

**Fact:** It is possible to negotiate consent when you have been drinking or taking drugs, but not when you are too drunk or high to know what is happening. Having sex or sexual contact with someone when they are too drunk or high to understand what is going on is sexual assault or rape, as they cannot consent.

## Gender and Sexual Violence

**Myth:** “Women cannot be raped or sexually assaulted by other women.”

**Fact:** In UK law, rape is specifically defined as non-consensual penetration with a penis. However, any non-consensual penetration with anything other than a penis is defined as ‘sexual assault by penetration.’ 
* This legal definition can feel restrictive to survivors, and many people use the word ‘rape’ to apply to any form of sexual assault by penetration. 
* Anyone can be a victim or perpetrator of sexual assault, regardless of gender. A woman can sexually assault another woman.
* Support is available for members of the LGBT+ community through organizations like **Galop**.

**Myth:** “Men don’t get sexually assaulted.”

**Fact:** While the majority of sexual assaults and rapes are committed by men against women, men can also be victims. 
* Statistics from Safeline state that 1 in 6 men are victims of sexual violence in the UK. 
* Support for men is available through the **Safeline** and **SurvivorsUK** websites.

## Appearance and Behavior

**Myth:** “People who flirt and wear revealing clothes are ‘asking for it’.”

**Fact:** There is no excuse for sexual violence and it is never the survivor’s fault. 
* Whether or not someone is flirting, looks attractive, or has laughed at the other person’s jokes is irrelevant when it comes to consent. 
* It doesn’t matter how long you have flirted with someone or how much you think they fancy you; flirting does not equal consent.

## Age of Consent (UK Law)

**Myth:** “You can’t consent if you are under 16.”

**Fact:** This is a complex area of the law. In England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, the legal 'age of consent' is 16. However:
* The law is designed to protect young people from abuse and exploitation by adults.
* It is not intended to criminalize young people of a similar age where activity is mutually agreed (consensual).
* The **Sexual Offenses Act 2003** states that a child under 13 does not have the capacity to consent to sex under any circumstances; activity with someone under 13 is viewed as rape or sexual abuse.
* Sexual activity between someone under 16 and someone over 16 is considered statutory rape.
* It is illegal for a person in a **position of trust** (such as a teacher, doctor, or social worker) to engage in sexual activity with a person under 18, even if consent is given.

## Summary

Consent to sex or sexual activity should be something you do freely and should be something you want and feel excited about. Whatever your age, it should never be something you feel scared, uncomfortable, or pressured to do. Just because someone is over 16 doesn’t mean that consent is automatically given.
