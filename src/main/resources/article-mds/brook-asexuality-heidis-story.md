# Realising I was asexual: Heidi’s story

Heidi, 17, shares their personal journey of discovering their identity as an asexual person and discusses why asexual people are a vital part of the LGBTQ+ community. 

In this story, Heidi explores their experience with sex-repulsion, which was a significant factor in discovering their asexuality. It is important to note that this is Heidi's unique perspective; while some asexual people are sex-repulsed, others may enjoy or have sex.

## Early realizations and sex education

The first signs of Heidi’s asexuality appeared around the age of 11 during primary school sex education lessons. While peers seemed to accept that sex was an inevitable part of the future, Heidi found the concept confusing and uncomfortable. Despite years of Relationships and Sex Education (RSE), Heidi noted that the option of not having sex was never explicitly taught.

## Romantic attraction vs. sexual attraction

Heidi identifies as **alloromantic** (someone who experiences romantic attraction but not sexual attraction). After discovering they were bisexual at age 12, Heidi realized they desired deep romantic connections—described as "mountain climbing and star-gazing"—but without a sexual component. For Heidi, the desire to have sex with someone they admired simply never occurred.

## Navigating the LGBTQ+ community

A common challenge for asexual individuals is the feeling of not being "queer enough." Because LGBTQ+ culture and liberation movements are frequently associated with sexual attraction and sexual activity, it can be difficult to navigate a queer identity that does not involve sex. Heidi emphasizes the importance of acknowledging that romantic and sexual relationships are distinct, and both are valid within the community.

## The journey to self-acceptance

Heidi initially resisted using the "ace" (asexual) label due to several factors:
* **Fear of judgment:** Worries that others would not take the identity seriously.
* **Lack of vocabulary:** Conversations around sex were often non-existent in relationships, making it hard to describe a lack of sexual feelings.
* **Age-related doubt:** A belief that they were "too young" to know or that their libido might change after reaching the age of consent or age 18.

At age 13, Heidi first voiced the possibility of being asexual during an uncomfortable encounter involving a request to sext. However, it wasn't until age 16 that Heidi decided to unapologetically embrace the label. 

## Coming out

Heidi officially came out at Pride 2021. Embracing the asexual label significantly improved Heidi’s self-image, confidence, and relationship with their body. Having access to supportive resources and language to articulate intimate feelings proved life-changing.

## Key takeaways for others

For those on a similar journey, Heidi offers the following insights:
* Asexuality is a beautiful and valid queer identity in its own right.
* You do not have to "just try" sex or any sexual activity before identifying as asexual.
* You are never too young to know who you are.
