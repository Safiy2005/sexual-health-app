# Diaphragms and Caps

Diaphragms and cervical caps are barrier methods of contraception. They are dome-shaped devices made of latex or silicone that are inserted into the vagina to cover the cervix. While they are similar, diaphragms fit into the vagina and over the cervix, whereas cervical caps are smaller and fit directly onto the cervix.

## Key Facts

* **Hormones:** Contains no hormones.
* **Mood/Emotions:** No effect on mood.
* **STIs:** Does not provide protection against STIs; condoms should be used for STI protection.
* **Periods:** Periods will stay the same.
* **Usage:** Must be used every time you have sex and kept in for at least six hours afterward.
* **Skin:** Does not affect acne or skin.
* **Effectiveness:** 92-96% effective when used correctly with spermicide.
* **Availability:** Available for free from some sexual health services. A consultation is required to ensure correct sizing and usage.

## How They Work

Both the diaphragm and the cap work by creating a physical barrier that stops sperm from entering the womb. To be effective, they must be used with a spermicide gel, which contains chemicals that kill sperm.

**Important:** Diaphragms and caps should not be used during your period due to the risk of Toxic Shock Syndrome (TSS). You should use an alternative method, such as condoms, during this time.

## Pros and Cons

### Pros
* Can be inserted before sex (extra spermicide is needed if sex occurs more than three hours after insertion).
* Unaffected by other medicines.
* 92-96% effective when used correctly.
* No hormonal side effects.

### Cons
* Does not protect against STIs.
* Can take time to learn how to use them confidently.
* Some users may be sensitive to latex or the chemicals in spermicide.
* Less effective than some other methods of contraception.
* May increase the risk of bladder infections (cystitis).

## Getting Fitted

A trained doctor or nurse must fit your diaphragm or cap for the first time. You can access these services at Brook centers, contraception clinics, GUM clinics, and some GP surgeries.

### At the Appointment
* A healthcare professional will ask about your medical and family history.
* An internal examination will be performed to determine the correct size and shape.
* You will be shown how to insert and remove the device and how to apply spermicide.
* You may be given a "practice" device to try at home. Note: You are not protected from pregnancy during this practice period and must use additional contraception like condoms.
* A follow-up appointment is usually required to check that the device is the correct size.

### When to Get a New Fit
You should have your fitting checked if:
* You lose or gain more than 3kg (7lb) in weight.
* You have a baby, miscarriage, or abortion.

## How to Use

### Putting a Diaphragm In
1. Wash your hands.
2. Apply a small amount of spermicide to the upper surface (and optionally the rim) of the diaphragm.
3. Find a comfortable position.
4. Squeeze the diaphragm between your thumb and fingers and slide it into the vagina, upwards and backwards.
5. Check that your cervix (which feels like the end of your nose) is covered.
6. If inserted more than three hours before sex, you must add extra spermicide.

### Putting a Cap In
1. Fill one-third of the cap with spermicide. Do not put spermicide on the rim, as it may prevent the cap from staying in place.
2. Add spermicide to the groove between the dome and the rim.
3. Squeeze the sides together and slide it into the vagina.
4. The cap stays in place by suction; ensure it fits neatly over the cervix.
5. If inserted more than three hours before sex, you will need to add more spermicide.

### Removal and Timing
* **Wait:** You must leave the device in for at least six hours after the last time you had sex.
* **Maximum Time:** Do not leave the device in for longer than 30 hours (latex) or 48 hours (silicone).
* **Removal:** Hook your finger under the rim or loop and pull downwards.

## After Pregnancy, Abortion, or Miscarriage

* **After Birth:** You can get pregnant three weeks after giving birth. You must wait six weeks after birth before using a cap or diaphragm to allow the cervix to return to its normal size.
* **After Abortion or Miscarriage:** You can use a diaphragm or cap immediately if the pregnancy lasted less than 13 weeks. If it was 13 weeks or over, wait six weeks and have your size re-checked by a professional.

## Maintenance: Dos and Don'ts

### Dos
* Wash in warm water with mild, unperfumed soap.
* Rinse thoroughly and dry carefully.
* Always use with spermicide.
* Store in its container in a cool, dry place.
* Check regularly for tears or holes.
* Be careful with sharp fingernails or jewelry.

### Don'ts
* Do not boil the diaphragm or cap.
* Do not use disinfectants, detergents, or talcum powder.
* Do not use oil-based products (like baby oil or oil-based lubricants) with latex devices, as they will damage the material.

## Replacement
You should replace your diaphragm or cap every two years, or sooner if it becomes damaged or if your weight/health circumstances change.
